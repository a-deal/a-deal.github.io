Intelligence Layer

This is the shared asset. Everything else is a surface.

Scoring engine

  • 20 health metrics across 2 tiers (Foundation + Enhanced)
  • NHANES 2017–2020 continuous percentiles, survey-weighted, stratified by age and sex
  • Clinical zone assessment (Optimal / Healthy / Borderline / Elevated) from published guidelines:
  • AHA/ACC 2017 (blood pressure)
  • ESC/EAS 2019 (ApoB)
  • ADA 2023 (glucose, HbA1c)
  • ACSM (VO2 max)
  • AHA/CDC (hs-CRP)
  • Endocrine Society (TSH, Vitamin D)
  • NHLBI/AHA (waist circumference)
  • Freshness decay—stale labs get discounted, not full credit
  • Reliability multipliers—protocol-averaged readings weighted higher than single measurements
  • Gap analysis ranked by coverage leverage with cost-to-close for every missing metric

Insights engine

  • Threshold-based coaching rules loaded from configurable YAML
  • Compound signals that connect metrics across domains:
  • Sleep debt accumulation vs. target
  • Deficit sustainability (weight loss rate × HRV × RHR interaction)
  • Sleep-deficit interaction—flags muscle loss risk under caloric restriction (Nedeltcheva 2010)
  • Nutrition deviation—unplanned surplus detection, late-meal sleep impact
  • 90-day trend computation with directional interpretation
  • Four severity levels: critical, warning, positive, neutral

Protocols engine

  • YAML-defined phased protocols with habit tracking and outcome metrics
  • Any domain: sleep, metabolic, cardiovascular, dermatologic, hormonal, training—the template is the same
  • Each protocol computes: day/week/phase, last completion rate, top nudge (highest-priority missed habit), outcome metrics vs. targets
  • Protocols are opinionated: “Hit your cutoff time,” not “consider your sleep hygiene”

What Nobody Does

1. Proactive outbound coaching. Every product in market is “open the app and ask.” Nobody sends a scheduled morning briefing that comes to you, informed by last night’s wearable data and yesterday’s protocol compliance.

2. Transparent population-benchmarked scoring. InsideTracker uses proprietary “optimal zones.” Function Health explains results. Nobody gives consumers a direct answer: “Here is where you rank against the US population on 20 metrics, scored against NHANES survey data with published clinical thresholds.”

3. Protocol engines that sequence interventions. InsideTracker recommends changes. Noom suggests habits. Nobody builds a sequenced program: “Weeks 1–2 fix sleep timing, week 3 add creatine, week 4 adjust training volume, then retest.” The closest is a Singapore government research pilot (npj Digital Medicine, 2026).

4. The integrated loop. Scoring + protocols + wearable data + proactive agent delivery as one closed system. The components exist across dozens of products. The full loop ships in none of them.

5. The builder-in-public narrative. Exactly three people are publicly documenting personal health agent builds (a FHIR engineer on Substack, a former VP of Agents on Medium, a developer on Medium). The person who owns this narrative—building a clinical-grade health intelligence system in public—has an open lane.

Where This System Sits

This is the only system that checks all four boxes: clinical-grade scoring against population benchmarks, a protocol engine that sequences interventions, live wearable data feeding outcome metrics, and proactive agent delivery via scheduled coaching. The thesis is not that each piece is novel—it’s that the integrated loop is what nobody ships.

Why This Approach Works (Historical)

The graveyard validates the architecture:

Lark failed because scripted AI couldn’t adapt. Rule trees pretending to be intelligent. The health-engine provides structured data to an LLM with genuine reasoning—the insights engine generates severity-ranked observations; the LLM decides how to deliver them contextually.

Operator-era companies died on unit economics. Per-user costs scaled linearly with human coaches. This stack’s marginal cost is ~$0.02 per daily coaching session. No human operators at any point.

Noom proved that programs work, then diluted the delivery. Structured behavior change outperforms tracking. The protocols engine delivers at full fidelity for every user—phased progression, evidence-backed habits, outcome metrics closed against real wearable data.

Omada proved that clinical specificity wins. ApoB thresholds from ESC/EAS, fasting glucose from ADA, VO2 max from ACSM—delivered through an agent, not a clinic. Clinical-grade assessment at individual scale, without clinical overhead.

From Dogfood to Production

This is the section the first draft was missing. The intelligence layer works for one user. Here’s how it serves many.

The Protocol Template

The personal protocols running today (sleep optimization, body composition, etc.) are instances of a general template:

Protocol Template — any health domain 
name: "Protocol Name"
domain: sleep | metabolic | cardiovascular | training | dermatologic | hormonal | ...
duration_weeks: 6
phases:
  - name: "Build"
    weeks: [1, 2]
    habits: [max 3 per phase]
    focus: "Establish baseline behaviors"
  - name: "Dial In"
    weeks: [3, 4]
    habits: [progressive additions]
    focus: "Tighten constraints, measure response"
  - name: "Sustain"
    weeks: [5, 6]
    habits: [full stack]
    focus: "Lock in, measure outcomes"
outcome_metrics:
  - metric: "sleep_duration"
    target: 7.5
    source: wearable
  - metric: "hrv_trend"
    direction: "increasing"
    source: wearable

Every protocol—sleep, weight management, cardiovascular risk reduction, stress management—follows this shape. Phased. Habit-tracked. Outcome-measured against real data. The system ships with a starter library of evidence-based protocols. Users can also define custom ones.

Multi-User Architecture

The production system isolates user data while sharing intelligence:

/users/
  /{user_id}/
    config.yaml          # age, sex, targets, preferences
    data/
      weight_log.csv
      meal_log.csv
      daily_habits.csv
      lab_results.json
      garmin_daily.json
      ...
    protocols/
      active/            # user's current protocols
      completed/         # archived with outcomes

What’s shared: The scoring engine, NHANES percentile tables, clinical thresholds, insights rules, protocol templates, coaching personality. This is the intelligence layer—it never holds user data.

What’s isolated: Every user’s measurements, meals, habits, lab results, protocol progress, wearable data. Fully siloed. One user’s data never touches another’s.

The Invite Flow

What onboarding looks like for user N+1:

  1. Receive invite link—opens a conversational onboarding via WhatsApp (or preferred channel)
  2. Profile setup—agent asks: age, sex, goals. 2-minute conversation, not a form
  3. Connect wearable—Garmin OAuth, Apple Health export, or Oura PAT. Agent walks through it
  4. Upload labs (optional)—photograph a lab report, OCR extracts values, scored immediately
  5. Select protocols—agent recommends based on profile and goals: “Based on your age and the fact that you haven’t had a lipid panel, I’d start with the Cardiovascular Baseline protocol and the Sleep Foundation protocol”
  6. First morning briefing—next day at their preferred time, the agent delivers the first coaching message with their initial scores and day-1 protocol habits

Time from invite to first briefing: under 24 hours.

Agent Architecture at Scale

Two viable models, not mutually exclusive:

Model A: Shared agent, per-user context. One LLM agent handles all users. Each conversation loads the user’s data directory and config before responding. The MCP server routes tool calls to the correct user’s data. Lower infrastructure cost. Works well up to ~100 users.

Model B: Agent per user. Each user gets a dedicated agent instance with persistent memory and conversation history. Better personalization—the agent remembers that this user tends to skip meals on Thursdays, or that they respond better to direct nudges than gentle suggestions. Higher infrastructure cost. The right model at scale.

The transition: Start with Model A. The intelligence layer is identical in both—the MCP tools accept a user_id parameter and route to isolated data. The agent layer is the only thing that changes.

The Path Forward

Four phases, grounded in what exists.

Phase 1: Validate the Template — Now through May

User zero is live. The work is proving the template generalizes:

  • Harden multi-protocol support—run 3+ protocols simultaneously, prove the engine handles domain interactions (sleep affects metabolic, training affects recovery)
  • Build the protocol starter library—sleep foundation, cardiovascular baseline, weight management, metabolic health. Evidence-based, ready to assign to any new user
  • Fix data sync—Syncthing bridge between development and agent host
  • Resume content distribution—the build-in-public narrative is the early distribution channel

Phase 2: Second User — May through August

Paul becomes user two. The critical validation:

  • Onboard through the invite flow—not manual setup
  • Paul’s own protocols, scored by the same engine, tracked by the same system
  • Morning briefings delivered via his preferred channel
  • The hypothesis: the system works for someone who isn’t the builder

Phase 3: Controlled Expansion — August through December

10 users. Hand-selected. The goal is stress-testing, not growth:

  • Does the protocol template cover the domains people actually care about?
  • Where does the agent fail? What coaching moments require intervention?
  • What wearable integrations are blocking adoption? (Apple Watch, Oura, WHOOP)
  • What’s the retention curve? Do people still engage at week 6? Week 12?

Phase 4: Product — 2027

With validated templates, proven retention, and multi-user infrastructure:

  • Public invite system with waitlist
  • Protocol marketplace—community-contributed protocols reviewed for clinical validity
  • iOS companion app (Paul’s surface) for capture, dashboards, quick entry
  • API access for third-party surfaces

The Moat

The intelligence layer is the moat. Not the app. Not the agent. Not the channel.

The scoring engine that knows ApoB matters more than LDL-C. The insights engine that connects sleep debt to muscle loss during a deficit. The protocols engine that runs real programs—phased, habit-tracked, outcome-measured—not feature tours.

Five structural constraints that keep the system opinionated:

  • 3 habits max per focus area—a constraint in the protocol schema, not a suggestion
  • Phased progression—protocols don’t front-load. Week 1 builds the routine. Week 3 tightens. Week 5 sustains
  • Clinical thresholds, not vibes—ApoB < 80 mg/dL is the ESC/EAS 2019 target. The system doesn’t soften it
  • Outcome tracking—every protocol defines measurable outcomes checked against real data, not self-report
  • Nudges, not lectures—“Get to bed by 11 tonight” beats “prioritize sleep hygiene”
The agent runs the program. The program is the moat.
