01

Market Overview

Austin's data center market reached approximately 1.54 GW of installed capacity in 2025, growing to an estimated 1.63 GW in 2026 and projected to reach 2.19 GW by 2031 (6.05% CAGR). The Austin-San Antonio corridor now accounts for roughly 1,700 MW of total capacity and has positioned itself as the second-largest U.S. data center market by new construction activity, behind only Northern Virginia.

Between 2023 and 2024, Central Texas construction activity quadrupled, adding 463.5 MW of potential demand under development. That four-fold expansion reflected a structural shift in developer and hyperscaler attention, not a cyclical spike.

Demand Drivers

Primary
AI Compute

Hyperscalers collectively targeting $315-400B in annual CapEx. U.S. data center capacity leasing in Q3 2025 alone exceeded the entirety of 2024. Average AI data center requests run 300 MW at a single site.

Structural
Energy Economics

Austin electricity averages $0.12/kWh, roughly 47% below the national average. At 100 MW scale, that differential represents tens of millions annually versus California or the Northeast.

Overflow
DFW Migration

Dallas-Fort Worth remains dominant by installed capacity, but power constraints, land scarcity, and congestion have pushed project overflow south. Austin offers land at lower cost with comparable power access.

Baseline
Cloud + Enterprise

Austin's 170,000+ technology sector workers generate native enterprise demand independent of the national AI build-out. Regional cloud, financial services, healthcare IT all continue to grow.

The Sub-10 MW Broker Gap

Every major DC broker in Texas is whale hunting: 100+ MW campus deals with hyperscaler tenants. Developers seeking sub-10 MW powered shell deals report being turned away by brokers who won't engage at that scale. This creates an asymmetry: the small operator market is underserved by the existing brokerage infrastructure, which means tenant discovery requires direct outreach, industry networks, and non-traditional channels. For small operators, the gap is both the opportunity (less competition) and the constraint (no broker is bringing you a deal).

02

Supply / Demand Dynamics

The Austin industrial market provides the context against which conversion decisions are made. Two markets, moving in opposite directions.

Industrial / Warehouse
Softening

Vacancy hit 21.9% (up 860 bps YoY). Net absorption of 4.1M sq ft was positive but outpaced by 6.8M sq ft of deliveries. Average asking rent: $13.17/sq ft NNN. Supply has outrun demand.

Data Center
Constrained

Near all-time low vacancy nationally. Colocation asking rates hit $195.94/kW/month (up 6.5% YoY), expected to breach $200/kW in 2026. Lead times for new capacity: 18-36 months.

The Arbitrage

Warehouse values are softer than peak. Data center premiums are near all-time highs. This divergence is the core conversion thesis. The supply of functional data center space is severely constrained relative to demand, and the timeline to bring new supply online is long. This creates a structural window for converting shovel-ready sites.

03

The Conversion Thesis

Why Warehouses

  • Column spacing. Large-bay industrial buildings (40-60 ft spacing) accommodate row-and-aisle server deployments without obstruction.
  • Floor loading. Class A distribution centers routinely specify 125-250 lbs/sq ft. Data centers require 150 lbs/sq ft minimum, 250 preferred. Many modern warehouses already qualify.
  • Power access. Industrial parks are frequently served by 12-35 kV distribution feeders designed for manufacturing loads. Existing utility easements reduce permitting timelines.
  • Site area. Warehouses with significant yard/trailer parking accommodate outdoor cooling towers, generators, switchgear, and battery storage without adding structure.
  • Speed to market. Adaptive reuse can cut 6-12 months off delivery timelines compared to greenfield, with 10-15% cost advantages over new construction.

Key Disqualifier

Ceiling height is the single most commonly cited disqualifier. Pre-2000 warehouse stock with 18-22 ft clear heights limits cooling system design flexibility. 24 ft is the practical minimum; 30 ft provides comfortable overhead mechanical routing.

04

Power Infrastructure

Texas operates an isolated synchronous grid managed by ERCOT. Unlike other U.S. grids, ERCOT is not interconnected with neighboring grids at scale. Power supply and demand must balance in real time with limited external backstop. This isolation is both a feature (Texas-specific pricing and policy) and a risk (no external backup during emergencies).

The Queue Problem

ERCOT's Large Load Interconnection queue has exploded. This is the critical constraint.

Sep 2024
56 GW
Sep 2025
205 GW
Current
225+ GW

225 GW of queued requests represents more than 2.5x the entire current ERCOT system peak demand. Approximately 70-73% are data centers. More than half have not submitted required studies. Realistic timeline from application to energized substation for large new loads (75+ MW): 3-5 years.

The Sub-75 MW Window

For projects below 75 MW, Austin Energy handles interconnection through its own distribution planning process rather than the ERCOT LLI queue. This creates a potential pathway for conversions as small as 2.5 MW that bypasses the worst of the queue delays. Austin Energy's pre-application process can confirm deliverable capacity at a specific site, even at the 2.5 MW floor. Sites near existing substations with available capacity can move from pre-app to energized service on a timeline measured in months, not years. This window is significant for powered shell entrants at every scale below 75 MW.

Senate Bill 6

  • Facilities drawing 75 MW or more must comply with ERCOT curtailment protocols during grid emergencies
  • On-site backup generation serving at least 50% of demand may be directed to deploy during grid events
  • Battery storage is becoming a practical necessity for compliance
  • The 75 MW threshold is adjustable by the Public Utility Commission of Texas

Regulatory + Incentives

Texas Sales Tax Exemption

One of the most generous data center incentive structures in the United States. The program provides a 10-15 year exemption from the 6.25% state sales and use tax on qualifying purchases.

Texas is forgoing approximately $1 billion/year through this program. For a $300M build, the exemption represents approximately $18-22M in avoided sales tax.

Zoning

Austin's LI and GI zoning permits data centers by right or administrative change of use. No full rezoning needed for most warehouse conversions. Outside city limits in Williamson, Hays, Caldwell, or Bastrop County, permitting is generally faster and less costly.

09

Risks

Critical
Power + Interconnection

225+ GW queued at ERCOT. New LLI applications enter a multi-year process. Austin Energy warning of "tremendous strain." The assumption that power will be available on any reasonable timeline requires rigorous due diligence.

Structural
Grid Reliability

Winter Storm Uri killed 200+ Texans when ERCOT ordered 20,000 MW of blackouts. Five years later, structural vulnerabilities remain partially unresolved. Available power could fall 15+ GW short during extreme events.

Market
Overbuilding

225 GW queued is implausible at face value. Many applications are speculative. The risk is timing mismatch: too many projects online simultaneously in 2027-2030 creating a short-term glut.

Cost
Construction Inflation

Data center construction costs rose 47% per sq ft recently. Transformer lead times: 52-100+ weeks. Conversion projects are partially insulated by reusing the shell, but MEP faces the same equipment markets.

Mitigation

Priority-queue sites with existing high-voltage service, legacy manufacturing power agreements, or proximity to substations with demonstrated available capacity. Pre-lease or LOI before breaking ground. Do not start a speculative turnkey buildout without a committed tenant. Powered shell is more defensible as a spec product because capital commitment is lower.

10

Decision Framework

Four Strategic Options

Convert

Owner develops the data center and retains ownership. Requires capital access, long hold horizon, and operational sophistication.

JV / Partner

Owner contributes land/building. Developer contributes capital and expertise. 20-40% promote on stabilized value. Best path for most warehouse holders.

Sell

Sell at data center site premium to a developer. Liquidity now. Expect 3-6x replacement cost for sites with committed power capacity.

Hold

Continue as warehouse. Preserve option value. Best if power or zoning constraints are disqualifying today. Wait for conditions to improve.

Develop DC-Ready

Acquire raw land or an underbuilt industrial site. Develop it as industrial with DC-conversion specs: oversized power (2.5-10 MW via Austin Energy pre-app), structural margins (30 ft+ clear height, 250 lbs/sq ft slab), substation proximity. Operate or lease as industrial while preserving the call option on DC conversion. Dispose to a DC developer at premium, or convert when tenant demand materializes. Maps to the renewable development playbook: pre-construction through disposition. Best path for developers without existing warehouse assets who can raise single-digit millions.

Site Qualification

Step 1
Site Qualification

Does the building meet 4+ of 6 conversion criteria? If not: hold or sell as-is.

Step 2
Power Path

Can the site secure sufficient power within 36 months? If not: option value is speculative.

Step 3
Capital + Expertise

Access to $50M+ and a development platform? If yes: convert or turnkey. $5-10M and development experience? Develop DC-Ready powered shell. Less than $5M? JV or sell.

Step 4
Timing

2025-2027 is the strongest near-term seller's market. After 2027, site premiums may moderate.

Timing Window

The current window (2025-2027) is likely the strongest near-term seller's market for data center sites. Power-constrained markets are repricing land with committed utility capacity dramatically upward. Holding through 2027-2028 without a clear power path carries both opportunity cost and risk of missing the peak.

JV Structure Options

  • Promote structure: Contribute land/building at appraised value. Partner contributes cash and expertise. Owner receives 20-40% promote on stabilized value or sale proceeds.
  • Ground lease: Retain fee interest and lease land under a 50-99 year ground lease. Current income, principal protection, and participation in development upside.
  • Sale-leaseback: Sell at data center premium to institutional buyer who undertakes conversion. Take cash proceeds and exit clean.
