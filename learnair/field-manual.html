Field Manual

Getting Started with AI

A field manual for building your toolkit. The landscape is moving fast, but the path through it is straightforward once you see how the pieces connect.

Built for Mike. Useful for anyone making the transition. This document is also the prototype for the LearnAIR veteran curriculum. Every problem we hit, every solution we find, every lesson we learn here becomes a module in the program. We're the first cohort.

01 — How the Pieces Fit Together
Tier 1 Chat Interfaces
ChatGPT · Claude.ai · Gemini · Perplexity

You type, it responds. Good for questions, brainstorming, writing, research. Limited because it can't see your files, can't run commands, forgets everything between sessions, and each conversation is an island.

Think of it as texting a smart friend. They can help you think, but they can't touch your computer.
Tier 2 Desktop + CLI Tools
Claude Code (CLI) · Claude Cowork (desktop) · Cursor · Windsurf

These tools can read your files, write code, run commands on your machine, and maintain memory across sessions. Claude Code is the CLI version (terminal). Cowork is the visual version. Same brain, different interface. CLI is faster and more powerful once you're comfortable.

The jump from Tier 1 to Tier 2 is like going from walkie-talkies to a full comms system.
Tier 3 Agents
OpenClaw · Custom agents · Autonomous systems

These run 24/7 without you. They monitor, respond, and take action on your behalf. Andrew's health coach on WhatsApp is a Tier 3 system — it pulls Garmin data, sends nudges, coaches users. You define the mission, the agent runs the patrols.

This is where "CEO of agents" becomes real.
Manus Browser Automation

Manus is a browser automation agent. It can click through websites, fill forms, interact with Google Sheets directly. Different from Claude Code (which works on your local machine) and OpenClaw (which runs autonomously).

Think of it as hiring someone to do web tasks for you. Use Manus when the task requires a browser.

03 — Key Concepts
Context Window
How much AI can hold in its head
Claude holds ~500 pages at once. ChatGPT holds less and degrades earlier. More context = better answers.
This is why CLI beats chat. In a browser, you copy-paste snippets. In Claude Code, you point it at your entire project and it reads everything. The context window is the biggest differentiator between "helpful chatbot" and "capable partner." When Claude can see your full codebase, meeting notes, and research at the same time, it connects dots you wouldn't think to ask about.
CLAUDE.md
Standing briefing for every session
A file Claude reads automatically every session. "Here's who I am, here's what this project is, here's what not to do." Write it once, never repeat yourself.
Put a CLAUDE.md in any project folder. Claude reads it before you say anything. Andrew's says things like "never use npm, use pnpm" and "write like texting a smart busy friend." You can put your persona, your preferences, your project context. This is how Claude goes from generic to personal. It's also where your AI style guide lives (see Personalizing Your AI below).
MCP
Model Context Protocol
Connects Claude to external services (Calendar, Sheets, etc.). Powerful when it works. Currently unreliable. CSV export is often better.
MCP is the protocol for plugging Claude into other tools. Google Calendar works well. Google Sheets was a 2-hour nightmare (service accounts, OAuth confusion, config file roulette). The ecosystem is young. Rule of thumb: if an MCP server doesn't work in 10 minutes, use a simpler path (download CSV, use Manus for browser tasks). Don't spend 2 hours on plumbing for a 5-minute task.
Agents vs. Assistants
The key distinction
An assistant waits for you to ask. An agent acts on its own. ChatGPT is an assistant. Andrew's WhatsApp health coach is an agent.
Assistants are reactive: you type, it responds. Agents are proactive: you define the mission, they execute patrols. An agent can pull your Garmin data every 4 hours, send you a morning briefing, nudge you when your sleep score drops. You set the rules once, it runs 24/7. The future of AI work isn't asking questions. It's commanding operations.
Input Speed
Speaking: 150 WPM. Typing: 40. Mouse: 10. Optimize accordingly.
Talk to generate (4x faster). Type to edit. Never click when you can type (10x faster). Voice-first reclaims 2 hours out of every 4 spent with AI tools.
Stanford research: speech is 3x faster than typing with 20% fewer errors. KLM research: terminal commands are 42% faster than GUI clicks. University of Waterloo: developers in terminal 65%+ of the time complete sprints 22% faster with 31% fewer focus breaks. Tools like Monologue turn your voice into text anywhere on your laptop. The workflow: speak your thoughts, let AI process them, type only to refine. Human output bandwidth is ~50 bits/second regardless of method (Paradromics neuroscience). Speaking encodes ~40 of those bits. Typing encodes ~15. The mouse encodes ~5.
Terminal Over GUI
Voice breaks the GUI paradigm. Your computer becomes a conversation, not a control panel.
Voice + CLI: speak an intent, the system acts. No menus, no buttons, no mouse targets. GUI stays for visual tasks (design, spreadsheets, spatial work).
Once you're talking to your computer, clicking through menus is the slow lane. But GUIs win in specific places: visual design (Figma), scanning a color-coded spreadsheet, precision pixel editing. The rule: CLI for producing and commanding. GUI for consuming and arranging. The discoverability problem ("in a GUI I see menus, in a CLI I see nothing") is solved by structure. That's what the hub framework does: five folders that map to five types of work, so you always know what's available without needing a menu bar.
The Hub
Five folders. Five types of work. Your local command center.
research/ · decisions/ · plans/ · meetings/ · execution/. Files are dated markdown. Claude sees everything. Ask "what do we have on Oregon?" and it searches all files.
The hub is at ~/src/hub/. Each folder maps to a type of work. research/ for anything you learn. decisions/ for choices with rationale. plans/ for roadmaps and strategies. meetings/ for session logs and call notes. execution/ for retros and what worked. Files are named YYYY-MM-DD-topic.md. When you open Claude Code inside the hub, it reads the whole structure. "Create a meeting log for today" and it knows the template. "What research do we have on funding?" and it searches across everything. This replaces "where do I click?" with "where does this belong?"
Personalizing Your AI
Make it talk like you, think like you
Feed it your writing. Ask "what do you notice?" Save the analysis as a style guide in CLAUDE.md. Three approaches: (1) let it analyze your patterns, (2) describe your voice in plain language, (3) correct it when it misses.
Out of the box, AI writes like a polite intern. Upload your writing samples and ask "what patterns do you see?" It surfaces things you'd never consciously noticed: how you open, how you structure, what words you reach for. Save that as a style guide. Over time, the AI stops sounding generic and starts sounding like your co-author. This isn't cosmetic. When the AI matches your voice, you spend less time editing and more time thinking. The guide sharpens with every correction.
Persona + Counterbalance Persona
STUB — Teach AI who you are, then fill your gaps
Create a persona prompt that captures your strengths, experience, and style. Then create a counterbalance persona that fills your weaknesses. Together they give AI a comprehensive view of who you are and what you need.
Start by describing yourself: background, skills, how you think, how you communicate. Feed that to Claude or ChatGPT. Then say "study my persona and create a counterbalance that fills all my gaps and weaknesses." Now you have two lenses. The AI knows what you bring and what you need. This came from LearnAIR's training approach and is a core module in the curriculum.
Dynamic UIs: Conversation First, Widgets Second
STUB — The UI of tomorrow pulls up only what you need, when you need it
Static dashboards constrain you. Conversation-first interfaces surface tools, documents, and widgets only when the context calls for them. Then they fold back into your workflow.
Imagine talking through a thesis and the document pulls up automatically. Or reviewing health data and a chart appears mid-conversation, then disappears when you move on. This is the pattern emerging in Claude Code: transient task lists, section-by-section reviews, temporary working files that capture before-and-after. The primary interface is dialogue. Widgets serve the conversation, not the other way around.
