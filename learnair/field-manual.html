Field Manual

Getting Started with AI

A field manual for building your toolkit. The landscape is moving fast, but the path through it is straightforward once you see how the pieces connect.

Built for Mike. Useful for anyone making the transition. This document is also the prototype for the LearnAIR veteran curriculum. Every problem we hit, every solution we find, every lesson we learn here becomes a module in the program. We're the first cohort.

01 — How the Pieces Fit Together
Tier 1 Chat Interfaces
ChatGPT · Claude.ai · Gemini · Perplexity

You type, it responds. Good for questions, brainstorming, writing, research. Limited because it can't see your files, can't run commands, forgets everything between sessions, and each conversation is an island.

Think of it as texting a smart friend. They can help you think, but they can't touch your computer.
Tier 2 Desktop + CLI Tools
Claude Code (CLI) · Claude Cowork (desktop) · Cursor · Windsurf

These tools can read your files, write code, run commands on your machine, and maintain memory across sessions. Claude Code is the CLI version (terminal). Cowork is the visual version. Same brain, different interface. CLI is faster and more powerful once you're comfortable.

The jump from Tier 1 to Tier 2 is like going from walkie-talkies to a full comms system.
Tier 3 Agents
OpenClaw · Custom agents · Autonomous systems

These run 24/7 without you. They monitor, respond, and take action on your behalf. Andrew's health coach on WhatsApp is a Tier 3 system — it pulls Garmin data, sends nudges, coaches users. You define the mission, the agent runs the patrols.

This is where "CEO of agents" becomes real.
Manus Browser Automation

Manus is a browser automation agent. It can click through websites, fill forms, interact with Google Sheets directly. Different from Claude Code (which works on your local machine) and OpenClaw (which runs autonomously).

Think of it as hiring someone to do web tasks for you. Use Manus when the task requires a browser.

03 — Key Concepts
Context Window
How much AI can hold in its head
Claude: 200K tokens (~500 pages). ChatGPT: 128K but degrades earlier. More context = better answers. This is why CLI beats chat — you can feed it your entire project.
CLAUDE.md
Standing briefing for every session
A file you put in any project folder. Claude reads it automatically every session. "Here's who I am, here's what this project is, here's what not to do." Saves you from repeating yourself.
MCP
Model Context Protocol
A way to connect Claude to external services (Google Calendar, Sheets, etc.). Powerful when it works. Currently unreliable for complex setups. CSV export is often the better path for now.
Agents vs. Assistants
The key distinction
An assistant waits for you to ask. An agent acts on its own based on rules you set. ChatGPT is an assistant. Andrew's WhatsApp health coach is an agent. The future is agents.
Input Speed: Why Voice-First Matters
The single biggest productivity unlock
Speaking is 130-170 words per minute. Typing is 35-43. Mouse clicking is 5-15. That's a 4x gap between talking and typing, and a 10x gap between talking and clicking through menus. The implication: the fastest way to interact with AI is to talk to it. Dictate your thoughts, let the AI process them, then type to refine. Stanford research found speech is 3x faster than typing with 20% fewer errors. Tools like Monologue turn your voice into text anywhere on your laptop. The workflow shift: talk to generate, type to edit, never click when you can type. This isn't a nice-to-have. If you're spending 4 hours a day interacting with AI tools, voice-first reclaims 2 of those hours. Optimize your toolchain for speaking first, typing second, mouse last.
Personalizing Your AI
Make it talk like you, think like you
Out of the box, AI writes like a polite intern. You can change that. Feed it examples of your writing and ask "what do you notice?" It builds a theory of your voice: how you start, how you structure, what words you reach for. Save that as a style guide in your CLAUDE.md or ChatGPT custom instructions. Over time, the AI stops sounding generic and starts sounding like your co-author. This isn't cosmetic. When the AI matches your voice, you spend less time editing and more time thinking. Three approaches: (1) upload your writing and let it analyze your patterns, (2) describe your voice in plain language ("direct, short sentences, no fluff, military-adjacent"), (3) correct it when it misses ("I wouldn't say it that way, I'd say it like this"). The guide sharpens over time. Think of it like training a new team member on how you communicate.
