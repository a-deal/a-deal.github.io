01

The arc of conversation

Good conversations have a shape. You start light, build energy around shared excitement, then use that momentum to get into the real questions. Think of it as climbing a hill together. The hard-hitting questions come near the top, when people are open and engaged, not at the start when everyone's still warming up.

First 10 minutes: Build momentum

Start with the excitement. This is a massive opportunity and you both feel it. Share what's been energizing you. The competitive landscape is wide open. Nobody is doing what you're envisioning. There are policy tailwinds (Raimondo's workforce AI initiatives, VET TEC 2.0 coming). Andrew is attending a Stanford seminar with Brynjolfsson, the leading AI labor economist, on Monday. You've been going through the LearnAIR curriculum yourself and seeing both its strengths and where veterans need something more.

The point of this phase: get Trisha talking about what excites her. People reveal what they care about when they're excited. Let her show you.

Middle 10 minutes: Explore competencies

Shift naturally from excitement to substance. There are real competencies this venture needs: curriculum design, employer outreach, veteran recruiting, sales, program management. You don't have to list them. Instead, talk about the challenges you see and watch where she leans in.

For example: "One thing I keep coming back to is, how do we know employers will actually hire for this role? That feels like the thing we need to answer first." See if she has a take. Does she name specific employers? Does she know how hiring managers think about AI roles? Or does she stay general?

Another angle: "The LearnAIR curriculum is strong for individual productivity. The gap I see is organizational coordination. When 50 people each have their own AI setup, who governs that?" See if she can speak to this with any depth.

You're not testing her. You're having a real conversation about real problems. Her responses will naturally reveal what she brings.

Last 10 minutes: Direct questions

By now you've built rapport and shared energy. This is where the frank questions feel natural, not confrontational.

How do you see LearnAIR's strategy for the veteran program? This is the big one. If she has a clear, specific answer, she's been thinking deeply. If she defers to Justin or stays high-level ("we're still figuring it out"), that tells you where she is. Both are fine. You just need to know.

What's your arrangement with LearnAIR right now? Volunteer? Consultant? On payroll? This is a fair question between potential partners. Ask it directly and warmly.

How do you see the April 7 call going? What does she think the outcome should be? This tells you whether she's thinking in terms of commitments or more conversation.

02

What to say, what to hold back

Say this

"Since our last group call, Andrew and I have been putting real work into this. We've built a comprehensive thesis, a competitive landscape analysis, a curriculum framework, and employer readiness targets. We're excited to bring that to the April 7 conversation."

That single statement does three things: it signals depth without showing your hand, it positions April 7 as the real moment, and it invites her to match your energy with her own preparation.

Hold back: the hub, the concept brief, the full competitive landscape, the curriculum gap analysis. These are for Justin and Teresa. You can talk to the work at wave-top level. Don't hand over documents yet.

Context from today's call: Trisha was likely brought back into the fold only after Justin met you. She's newly activated, not someone who failed to execute for 5 months. Give her room to show what she's got. The priority failure was timing and bandwidth on LearnAIR's side, not incapability.

03

What you're listening for

Does she describe real problems or abstract roles?

If she talks about the difficulty of getting employers to understand AI supervision roles, or the challenge of vetting which veterans are ready for this, she's thinking about the actual work. If she talks about "strategic alignment" and "idea management," she's thinking about process.

Can she speak to employer dynamics with any specificity?

Her L&D background at Consumer Cellular and her Easter Seals HVRP work mean she should have opinions here. If she can name companies, describe hiring patterns, or talk about how training budgets work, that's additive to what you and Andrew bring.

Is she additive or duplicative?

Best case: she brings the employer network, the veteran org connections (Easter Seals, L&D community), and the operational program management that you and Andrew don't have. That's a real team. If she's claiming strategy and curriculum design, those overlap with what you already have. Overlap isn't bad, but it changes how the partnership works.

What's her energy around commitment?

Is she talking about what she's going to do this week? Or is she talking about what the group should discuss next time? Forward motion vs. more meetings. Both tell you something.

Remember: Thursday is positioning for April 7. You're not closing anything. You're building a relationship and gathering intel. Walk in curious, walk out informed. That's a win.

You've got this. Trust the arc. Trust your read on people. Call Andrew after if you want to debrief.
