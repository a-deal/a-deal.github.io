Framing note: in the loop vs on the loop

2. The Vision: From Hierarchy to Intelligence

Source: Jack Dorsey + Roelof Botha (Sequoia), March 31, 2026

Corporate hierarchy is a 2,000-year-old information routing protocol. It exists because one human can only manage 3-8 people. Every attempted alternative (Spotify squads, Zappos holacracy, Valve flat structure) reverted to hierarchy because nothing else could handle coordination at scale.

Dorsey's claim: AI breaks that constraint. For the first time, you can maintain a continuously updated model of an entire business and coordinate work without humans relaying information through management layers.

"What does your company understand that is genuinely hard to understand, and is that understanding getting deeper every day?"

If the answer is nothing, AI is just cost optimization. If the answer is deep, AI reveals what your company actually is.

3. The Structural Problem: Adversarial Collaboration

Source: MIT CSAIL, arXiv 2602.19141, February 2026

MIT researchers proved mathematically that sycophantic chatbots cause users to spiral into false beliefs through a self-reinforcing feedback loop. The bot doesn't lie. It selects which true facts to present based on what the user wants to hear. Each round, the user becomes more confident in the wrong direction.

Even perfectly rational reasoners spiral. This isn't a bug in human thinking. It's a structural property of the interaction.

Both obvious fixes fail:

  • Making bots factual only: spiraling persists at sycophancy levels as low as 10%
  • Warning users about sycophancy: reduces but doesn't eliminate spiraling

The Dorsey Paradox

Dorsey proposes replacing human coordination with AI coordination at the exact moment we have mathematical proof that AI coordination has a structural honesty problem.

If the AI intelligence layer tells each person what they want to hear, you get something worse than hierarchy: the illusion of alignment. Everyone thinks the team agrees because the AI told each of them what they wanted to hear. Nobody discovers the disagreement until the project fails.

The skill that matters most in AI-augmented organizations isn't prompt engineering. It's adversarial collaboration with AI. Knowing when to push back. Knowing how to detect when the model is selecting facts instead of presenting them.

That's a curriculum. That's teachable. And almost nobody is teaching it because the entire industry is optimizing for making AI agreeable.

v8 refinement for Beat 2

The supervision gap isn't just "can you evaluate AI output." It's "can you maintain truth in a system that's structurally incentivized to agree with you." The Matt Schwartz example (v7) is exactly this. The physicist didn't just check output. He adversarially interrogated it because he knew where it would fail.

v8 refinement for Track 2, Week 3

"Live AI Agent Supervision Exercises" should include adversarial collaboration training. Not just error detection. Active truth maintenance.

5. The Cautionary Tale

Source: Medvi / NYT, April 2, 2026

One person built a $401M/yr telehealth GLP-1 business with AI tools and 2 employees. Revenue is real. But: FDA warning letter for misbranding. Fabricated doctor personas. AI-generated deepfake marketing. Customer service chatbot that hallucinated drug prices and product lines.

This is what happens when you teach AI leverage without teaching structural honesty.

AI gives individuals enormous power. That power can build systems that help people or systems that exploit them. The difference is integrity of the system. As the thing that separates graduates employers want to hire from the ones that end up with FDA warning letters.

7. Field Data: AI Agents in Professional Production

Source: Stanford DEL Seminar, April 6, 2026

A researcher from the University of Chicago Booth School of Business (Finance and Applied AI, formerly Microsoft and Google, PhD Applied Math/CS Harvard) presented findings from Cursor, an AI-assisted coding platform, covering 100,000 professional users across 1,000 firms. This is the first large-scale empirical study of AI agents in professional production environments.

The Shift from Support to Delegation

AI agents change the nature of work. Autocomplete (the prior paradigm) helps workers finish tasks they've already started. Agents let workers delegate entire tasks. The skill set shifts from implementation to specifying intent, evaluating output, and aligning with the AI before acting.

40% of professional developers on Cursor no longer type code manually into their editors. Agent usage has become the dominant mode of software production on the platform in under a year.

39% increase in weekly output (measured by code merges) after agent release, with no measured increase in bug rates or reverts in the sample period.

The task distribution for agent users: writing intent specifications, making plans, evaluating outputs, and gathering information. These are fundamentally different skills than typing code. This is the job description for the role LearnAIR is training.

The Experience Gradient Reversal

This is the finding that maps most directly to the veteran thesis.

Prior AI research consistently found that junior workers benefit more from AI tools. Autocomplete helps people who don't know what to type next. That pattern reversed with agents.

Experienced workers are better at AI delegation. They plan more in initial messages, ask fewer questions about the codebase, and have higher acceptance rates for agent output. The mechanism: tacit knowledge enables clearer intent specification and more accurate evaluation.

The study found larger output gains for firms with more experienced workers and more non-engineers (designers, product managers). Workers in roles with more verifiable outputs used agents more frequently: designers averaged 80+ agent interactions per week versus 55-60 for software engineers.

Direct validation of Beat 3

The exact skills that predict agent effectiveness, planning before acting, specifying intent clearly, evaluating output accurately, pattern recognition with incomplete information, are the skills veterans trained for years. The "experience-based operating management mindset" isn't a metaphor. It's what the data shows produces better outcomes with AI delegation tools.

Verifiability Predicts Adoption

Agent delegation works best when you can tell whether the output matches your intent. Tasks with more verifiable outputs (UI, API integration) see higher agent usage than tasks with complex dependencies (architecture, DevOps). This has direct implications for curriculum design: training people to verify AI output is training the bottleneck skill.

Direct validation of Beat 2 + Track 2 Week 3

The supervision gap is empirically confirmed. The MIT sycophancy research (Section 3) shows AI systems are structurally incentivized to agree. This field data shows verification skill is the differentiator between effective and ineffective AI delegation. Together: the ability to maintain truth while evaluating AI output is the core competency.

Where This Goes Next

The researcher introduced the concept of "syntactic regularities": domains with formal rules, structured outputs, and verifiable results will see agent-driven productivity gains similar to software engineering. The candidates: mathematics, accounting, contract law, and formal compliance environments.

LearnAIR's target sectors (healthcare, financial services, government, logistics) are exactly these formal-rule domains. The research predicts they are next in line for the delegation shift that has already transformed software production.
