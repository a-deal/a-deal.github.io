1. Oregon Veteran Population

Statewide

  • ~266,000 veterans in Oregon (ODVA official figure)
  • Oregon ranks ~20th nationally in veteran population
  • ~6.3% of adult population are veterans (above national average of ~5.6%)
  • Oregon has no active-duty bases — veteran population is Guard/Reserve + post-service relocators drawn by quality of life

Age Distribution (Derived from Service Period Data)

  • Vietnam era dominates Oregon’s veteran population (~88,251 statewide) — now 70–85 years old, largely retired
  • Gulf War 2001+ (~53,464) — ages 35–55, the prime placement cohort for AI ecosystem roles
  • Gulf War 1990s (~35,021) — ages 48–60, senior roles
  • Post-9/11 + younger — growing cohort, most tech-ready but smallest in number
Key insight: The 35–55 age bracket (post-9/11 and Gulf War veterans) is the sweet spot — experienced enough to supervise, young enough to retrain. Roughly ~90,000 veterans statewide in this window.

Veteran Employment in Oregon

  • Oregon veteran unemployment rate tracks close to national average (~3.5–4.0% for post-9/11 veterans)
  • Underemployment is the bigger issue: Many veterans in jobs below their capability — security, warehouse, logistics roles that don’t leverage leadership training
  • Most common post-military career paths: healthcare, public administration, construction, transportation/logistics, law enforcement
  • Median veteran household income in Oregon: ~$65,000–75,000 (below non-veteran peers with similar education in many cases)
  • WorkSource Bend has been nationally recognized (NASWA award) for veteran services

Veteran Infrastructure Already in Place

  • COVO (Central Oregon Veterans Outreach): 200+ veterans/month, Bend
  • East Cascades Works: WIOA workforce board for Central Oregon (10 counties), HQ in Bend
  • COCC STRIVE Program: Free 8-week veteran entrepreneurship (Syracuse IVMF partnership) — Justin won through this
  • OSU-Cascades: CS with AI option, career programs, Bend campus
  • 541VETS: Digital resource library for rural Oregon veterans
  • Easterseals Oregon: HVRP across 8 cities
  • Hire Heroes USA: Expanding into PNW, partners with General Assembly (Project Forge)

2. AI Adoption in Oregon Businesses

National Context (Oregon Extrapolation)

  • 28% of workers currently use generative AI at work (Salesforce survey, 14,000 workers, Oct 2023)
  • More than half use AI without employer approval — the “shadow AI” problem
  • 70% of workers have never received AI training from their employer
  • 87% of healthcare workers say their employer lacks clear AI policies
  • 64% of workers have presented AI-generated work as their own
The gap is real: Individual productivity up, organizational process absent. This is the exact thesis.

Oregon–NVIDIA $10M AI Partnership

  • Signed April 2025 by Gov. Kotek + Jensen Huang
  • Programs funded: AI Ambassador Program (campus), K–12 AI curriculum, campus infrastructure
  • Participating institutions: Oregon State (first multidisciplinary AI grad program), PCC (AI Lab opened Jan 2026, $1.7M teaching cleanroom), University of Oregon, COCC
The Opening The NVIDIA partnership focuses on education/awareness, not on business operations or deployment supervision. No “AI operations” track exists. That’s the gap.

  • Intel (Hillsboro): 22,300 employees, largest private employer. Deep into AI chip development. Would need internal AI coordinators at scale.

  • Health Elements AI (Bend): Actual AI company doing clinical data abstraction. BVC finalist.

3. Which Businesses Feel the Pain Most

Individual workers bought ChatGPT and got 10x more productive at their tasks. But the organization didn’t change its processes, workflows, approval chains, or quality controls. Now you have powerful individuals operating in an organizational vacuum. Someone needs to supervise the AI ecosystem. That’s the AI Ecosystem Supervisor.

Pain Categories Ranked by Urgency

Tier 1 — Acute Pain
Bought AI, don’t know how to manage it

Companies with ChatGPT Team/Enterprise licenses but no governance, no workflows, no quality control.

  • SMBs that engaged LearnAIR-style training: Got excited, bought licenses, did a workshop... now what? The trainer left.
  • Professional services firms (accounting, law, consulting): Partners using AI for drafts, junior staff using it unsupervised, no quality gate
  • Healthcare organizations: 87% lack AI policies. HIPAA + AI = liability bomb without supervision
  • Real estate/mortgage/insurance: High-volume document processing, AI creeping in without controls
Tier 2 — Structural Pain
High process complexity + AI adoption pressure

Complex workflows where unsupervised AI creates risk.

  • Manufacturing: Quality control, supply chain, safety documentation. BASX-type companies with hyperscale clients demanding process rigor.
  • Healthcare systems: St. Charles Health System (~4,500 employees, 4 hospitals). Clinical documentation, billing, patient communications all AI-adjacent.
  • Construction/engineering: Permitting, project management, safety compliance.
  • Logistics/transportation: Les Schwab’s 500+ locations managing AI across distributed operations.
Tier 3 — Growth Pain
Scaling with AI but struggling with coordination

Companies in growth mode where AI amplifies both speed and chaos.

  • Tech startups (Bend ecosystem): Dutchie, Health Elements AI, Kollective, Amplion — small teams using AI heavily but no dedicated AI ops role
  • Data center adjacent: Companies serving Meta/Apple Prineville campuses
  • E-commerce/DTC brands: AI for marketing, customer service, product descriptions — inconsistent quality
Tier 4 — Succession Pain
Baby boomer handoff + AI transition

SMBs in the $100K–5M revenue range where the founder is retiring and the next generation needs to modernize.

  • Central Oregon trades: HVAC, plumbing, electrical contractors. Baby boomer owners, millennial managers eager for AI but no framework.
  • Professional practices: Dental offices, veterinary clinics, small law firms. Solo practitioner retiring, buyer wants to run leaner with AI.
  • Agriculture/ranching: Precision ag is AI-ready but no one to implement.
  • Hospitality/tourism: Brasada Ranch, Sunriver Resort, Mt. Bachelor. Seasonal workforce + AI = scheduling chaos.

5. Veteran-to-Employer Matching Framework

Tier 1 — E4–E6 (Tactical NCOs)
Corporals through Staff Sergeants

Military skill translation: Team leadership (4–12 people), process execution, quality control, SOP adherence, training subordinates, equipment maintenance protocols

Best employer fit:

  • SMBs (10–100 employees) as the hands-on AI coordinator
  • Manufacturing floor (BASX, Medline Renewal) — ensuring AI tools are used correctly in production
  • Healthcare (St. Charles individual departments) — clinical AI tool deployment and monitoring
  • Retail operations (Les Schwab regional) — rolling out AI tools across 10–30 locations
Salary range: $55,000–75,000  |  Title: “AI Operations Coordinator” / “AI Tools Administrator”
Tier 2 — E7–E9 + O1–O3 (Senior NCOs + Junior Officers)
Program Management Level

Military skill translation: Program management, cross-functional coordination, policy development, training program design, risk assessment, 50–200 person organizations

Best employer fit:

  • Mid-market companies (100–1,000 employees) as the AI Program Manager
  • Healthcare systems (St. Charles, PeaceHealth) — enterprise-wide AI governance
  • Manufacturing (BASX, Daimler, Precision Castparts) — AI quality and compliance programs
  • Professional services (Perkins & Co, law firms) — firm-wide AI policy and training
Salary range: $80,000–120,000  |  Title: “AI Ecosystem Manager” / “AI Program Manager”
Tier 3 — O4+ (Senior Officers — Majors and above)
Enterprise Strategy Level

Military skill translation: Organizational strategy, cross-departmental governance, change management, executive communication, large-scale program oversight (500+ people)

Best employer fit:

  • Large enterprises (1,000+ employees) as VP/Director of AI Operations
  • Intel, Nike, Daimler — enterprise AI governance and strategy
  • Healthcare systems as Chief AI Officer / VP Digital Transformation
  • Federal/defense (Bonneville Power, Northrop) — AI governance with clearance
Salary range: $130,000–200,000+  |  Title: “Chief AI Officer” / “VP of AI Operations”
The Naming Advantage No one has standardized what to call this role. The program that names it owns the category. The military rank mapping gives employers an instant credibility signal: “This person managed 200-person operations in high-stakes environments. They can manage your AI ecosystem.”

6. The Consultancy Play

Engagement Model

Phase 1: AI Readiness Assessment (1–2 weeks)

  • Audit current AI tool usage (licensed, shadow, free-tier)
  • Map workflows where AI is being used vs. should be used
  • Interview 5–10 key staff across departments
  • Deliverable: AI Ecosystem Report Card + Priority Roadmap
  • Price: $5,000–10,000 (flat fee)

Phase 2: AI Ecosystem Build-Out (4–8 weeks)

  • Design AI governance policies (acceptable use, data handling, quality standards)
  • Build workflow templates for top 3–5 use cases
  • Set up monitoring and reporting dashboards
  • Train internal champion(s) to maintain the ecosystem
  • Price: $15,000–30,000 (project-based)

Phase 3: Ongoing Retainer (monthly)

  • Monthly ecosystem health check, new use case development
  • Staff training for new hires, policy updates as AI tools evolve
  • Quarterly executive briefing
  • Price: $3,000–5,000/month

Phase 4: Veteran Placement (the pipeline)

  • Recommend hiring a full-time AI Ecosystem Supervisor
  • Source from the veteran program, provide 90-day onboarding support
  • Price: Placement fee (15–20% of first-year salary) + transition retainer
