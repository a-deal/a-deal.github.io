Gina Raimondo — Former Secretary of Commerce

Current Roles (Post-Commerce, as of Jan 2025)

  • Distinguished Fellow, Council on Foreign Relations — co-chairing new CFR Task Force on economic security
  • Commissioner, BPC Commission on the American Workforce (alongside former Govs Haslam + Deval Patrick)
  • Costco board of directors
  • Aspen Institute Economic Strategy Group
  • Publicly considering a 2028 presidential run (Boston Globe, Jan 2026)

Her Core Thesis

The US is about to repeat the mistakes of deindustrialization unless it acts deliberately on AI workforce transition. Personal: her father lost his 30-year job when the Bulova watch factory in Providence closed.

Key Quotes

“Because I had a front-row seat in my family and my state to that suffering, I’m so worried we’re about to make the same mistake again with AI.”

“Millions of Americans — from white collar to blue collar, entry level to executive — may soon find themselves jobless and without prospects.” (NYT op-ed, Mar 6 2026)

“America cannot withstand the economic shock that’s coming.”

“Slowing AI isn’t the answer and believing the market will sort it out is a mistake we’ve made before.”

“It’s a waste of money to train people for jobs that don’t exist.”

“These things only work if businesses step up from day one and commit to hiring.”

“The way I’m telling this to governors is: be an AI-ready state. Don’t be a state that doesn’t have a plan and winds up with sky-high unemployment.”

Her Policy Framework — The “Grand Bargain”

Three pillars:

  1. Employers define skills, commit to hiring, coordinate industry-by-industry
  2. Government funds training tied to outcomes (not enrollment/seat-time), builds safety nets, creates modular education
  3. Education transforms into lifelong, modular, job-linked credentialing

Outcomes-Based Funding (Her Signature Issue)

  • Rhode Island Governor: Real Jobs RI — employer-driven, demand-side. Trained 3,000+ with direct placement.
  • Commerce Secretary: Good Jobs Challenge — $525M, 40 regions, 12,000+ workers trained and placed. Funding via “funding orders” (small tranches, not large grants). Measures employment and earnings outcomes.
  • NYT op-ed: “Public funding based on labor-market results instead of enrollment.”
  • BPC Commission: “Rewarding results instead of promises. Moving beyond seat-time requirements.”

NYT Op-Ed: “America Cannot Withstand the Economic Shock That’s Coming” (Mar 6, 2026)

  • Calls for “new grand bargain” between public and private sectors
  • Modular higher education: short, affordable, job-linked credits
  • Public funding based on labor-market results
  • Employers must describe needed skills and create pathways
  • “Be an AI-ready state”
  • Source: NYT via RealClearMarkets

State-Level Unevenness

  • Brookings: 30 metro areas account for 2/3 of all AI job postings
  • Anthropic Economic Index: per capita AI usage ranges from 3.82x expected (DC) to 0.21x expected (Mississippi)
  • Her framing: “Don’t be a state that doesn’t have a plan”

Programs She Built at Commerce

  • US AI Safety Institute (within NIST)
  • Good Jobs Challenge: $525M, outcomes-based
  • CHIPS Act workforce training: 50+ community colleges, HBCU network
  • Commerce Workforce Council
  • LEARN AI Act: gave Commerce Secretary 6 months to develop national AI literacy strategy

China vs US

“We’re ahead of China. We stay ahead of China.”

“Everybody who cares about US national security better get religion.”

Implied contrast: China embeds AI aggressively across economy; US risks falling behind not on tech but on workforce readiness.

Singapore Connection

  • Held AI roundtable in Singapore as Commerce Secretary (Jun 2024)
  • Her policy proposals (modular education, lifelong training credits, outcomes-based funding) closely parallel Singapore’s SkillsFuture model
  • Singapore mandated AI literacy for all public servants (Oct 2025)

Veteran Programs

Gap identified: No specific Raimondo initiative targeting veteran workforce transition in AI context. This is a genuine whitespace — the LearnAIR veteran program could position itself directly in her framework.

Erik Brynjolfsson — Stanford Digital Economy Lab

Position

  • Jerry Yang and Akiko Yamazaki Professor, Stanford
  • Senior Fellow, Stanford HAI
  • Director, Stanford Digital Economy Lab
  • Co-author/contributor, Stanford AI Index Report

His Core Research (LearnAIR-Relevant)

“Canaries in the Coal Mine” (Nov 2025) — THE KEY PAPER

  • Uses ADP payroll data (25M+ US workers) to measure AI’s real-time labor impact
  • 13% relative employment decline for ages 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations since ChatGPT launch
  • Software developer hiring (22–25): down ~20%
  • Call center hiring: down ~15%
  • Older workers: stable or growing (6–9%)
  • Critical finding: automative AI usage drives decline; augmentative AI usage does not
  • Uses both GPT-4 Exposure index and Anthropic Economic Index
  • Source: Stanford DEL (PDF)

“Generative AI at Work” (QJE, May 2025)

  • 5,179 customer support agents, staggered AI rollout
  • 14% average productivity increase
  • 34% improvement for novice/low-skilled workers
  • Minimal impact on experienced workers
  • AI disseminates best practices from top performers to everyone

“Minimum Wages and Rise of the Robots” (NBER, Feb 2026)

  • 10% minimum wage increase → 8% increase in industrial robot adoption
  • Two-pronged squeeze: AI from top (white-collar), automation from bottom (blue-collar)

The Productivity J-Curve

  • GPTs suppress measured productivity during investment phase, then enter harvest phase
  • US productivity jumped ~2.7% in 2025 (nearly 2x the 10-year average)
  • Brynjolfsson argues we’re transitioning from investment to harvest

Key Concepts

Task Automation Doubling Rate

  • METR research (cited in AI Index): 50% task completion threshold doubles every ~7 months (accelerating to ~4 months in 2024–25)
  • Within a decade, AI agents could independently complete tasks taking humans days/weeks

Nowcasting vs Forecasting

  • Traditional: forecast which jobs AI will affect using models
  • Brynjolfsson: nowcast using real-time ADP payroll microdata to measure what’s actually happening

“I feel like we’re kind of flying blind... not putting in place the infrastructure we need.”

Advocates “sense and respond” vs “plan then implement.”

The Jagged Frontier

  • AI’s capability landscape is uneven — tasks that seem similar in difficulty may fall on opposite sides of AI’s capability
  • Within same workflow, some tasks are easy for AI, others are beyond current AI
  • You cannot predict from task difficulty alone — must test empirically

The Turing Trap

  • Excessive focus on AI that mimics humans (substitution) vs AI that augments humans → workers lose bargaining power
  • Solution: eliminate excess incentives for automation over augmentation; equalize taxation of capital and labor

Jevons Paradox — “This Time Is Different”

  • Jevons (efficiency increases demand) only applies if workers get more productive AND productivity translates to lower prices AND consumer demand explodes
  • Does NOT apply if AI can fully automate every task in a job
  • Position: Jevons holds for some occupations (augmentation) but not others (full automation)

What Works in Workforce Transition

  • Focus on skill adjacencies (minimal retraining, not starting over)
  • Short, achievable training with clear ROI
  • Management skills — managing AI agents will be as important as managing people
  • Use AI to learn (augmentation), not just to automate
  • Identify bottleneck tasks that cannot be automated — focus value there

What Doesn’t Work

  • Long, expensive retraining with poor completion

“We didn’t do a very good job of that” (trade displacement) — “it would be a catastrophe” to repeat with AI.

The Whitespace

Neither Raimondo nor Brynjolfsson has a veteran-specific AI workforce program in their portfolio. This is a genuine gap. A program that:

  1. Uses Raimondo’s outcomes-based framework
  2. Is backed by Brynjolfsson’s research
  3. Targets the veteran population
  4. Measures placement, not attendance

…would be the first of its kind and directly aligned with the policy conversation both are driving.
