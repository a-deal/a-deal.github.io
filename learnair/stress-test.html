Strategic Stress Test

Opposition arguments, path to success, and honest assessment

March 22, 2026

Strongman Opposition

The best version of every argument against this working. If the thesis survives these, it survives anything.

1 “You don’t have a product yet. You have a thesis.”

The brief is impressive. The research is deep. But nobody has gone through the training. No veteran has been taught. No employer has been asked “would you hire this person?” The gap between a published thesis and a working program is where most ideas die.

Counter

Dogfooding matters. You and Mike going through the learning path IS the product development. But the opposition would say: you’re two technical-adjacent guys training yourselves. A 22-year-old E-5 with a GED coming off active duty has a completely different starting point.

2 “AI operations supervisor isn’t a job title anyone is hiring for.”

Search any job board. Zero results. You’re creating a role that doesn’t exist in the market yet. Employers don’t have budget lines for positions they haven’t imagined. You’d be selling them something they don’t know they need, which is the hardest sale there is.

Counter

“Data scientist” wasn’t a title in 2008. “DevOps engineer” didn’t exist in 2005. The Brynjolfsson research shows the need is real. But you need to translate “AI operations supervisor” into language employers already use. Maybe “AI-enabled project manager” or “automation team lead” or something that maps to existing budget lines.

3 “LearnAIR already tried veteran outreach and it failed.”

Justin said directly: Easter Seals wasn’t ready. SEAL orgs weren’t ready. Other vet groups were hesitant. 40 organizations identified, minimal traction. Why would your approach be different?

Counter

LearnAIR pitched “AI literacy training for veterans.” Solution looking for a problem. The thesis reframes it as “here’s how to make your veterans the most hirable people in the room.” Different pitch, different energy. But the underlying resistance (vet orgs not ready for AI) is real and won’t disappear because you have better slides.

4 “You’re two guys with no track record in workforce development.”

Neither has run a training program. Neither has placed a veteran in an AI role. Neither has managed DOL grant compliance. The people with operational chops (Trisha) are the ones you’re arm’s-length from.

Counter

Valid. This is why the Trisha relationship matters regardless of the LearnAIR decision. And it’s why starting with a pilot (5 veterans, 1 employer partner) is smarter than a program launch.

5 “The funding path is unclear.”

DOL grants require 501(c)(3) or partnership. GI Bill requires accreditation (years-long). SkillBridge requires DoD approval. Every funding path has 6–18 month lead time.

Counter

Run a pilot for near-zero cost. Training tools = Claude Code. Curriculum = what you’re building. Venue = Zoom. Real question: can you get 5 veterans and 1 employer to do a free pilot that produces a case study? That’s fundable.

Path to Success: Zero to One

Phase 0 — Now through April
Dogfooding

You and Mike ARE the curriculum. Document everything. The field manual is the prototype.

Milestone: Both of you can independently use Claude Code for real work, articulate the three-tier landscape, and explain why AI ops supervision is a distinct skill from AI literacy.
Phase 1 — April through May
Find 1 Employer

You don’t need 50. You need ONE who says: “Yes, I would hire a veteran who can supervise AI operations.”

  • Tech companies in Bend/Portland deploying AI internally
  • Defense contractors with Oregon presence
  • Federal agencies doing AI modernization
  • SMBs who just bought ChatGPT Team and have no idea what to do (LearnAIR’s client base)
The pitch: “We’re not asking you to hire anyone yet. If a veteran showed up who could manage your AI tools, validate outputs, and run human-AI workflows, would that be valuable? What would that role look like?” Discovery interview, not a sales call.
Phase 2 — May through June
Find 5 Veterans

Not through organizations (they already said no). Through personal networks.

  • Mike’s Marine network. Text 10 buddies.
  • Dad’s network in Bend. Veterans know veterans.
  • Derek’s contacts (expressed interest)
  • Breathwork Northwest community (some are veterans)
  • LinkedIn: Mike posts about the thesis, tags veteran hashtags
  • COCC veteran students (Justin won STRIVE there)
The pitch: “Free. Four weeks. You’ll learn to use AI tools that make you 10x more productive. At the end, we connect you with an employer who wants this skill. No commitment, no cost. We just need honest feedback.”
Phase 3 — June
Run the Pilot

5 veterans. 4 weeks. The curriculum you’ve been dogfooding. One employer partner for a mock project or interviews at the end.

Success looks like:

  • 5 veterans complete the program
  • At least 3 can independently use Claude Code
  • At least 1 gets a job offer, interview, or gig connected to AI skills
  • Video testimonials, before/after skill assessments, employer quote
  • A case study you can put in front of Raimondo, Brynjolfsson, or any funder

Kill Criteria

  • Can’t find 5 veterans willing to do a free program (no demand)
  • Veterans drop out because content isn’t relevant (wrong curriculum)
  • Employer partner says “interesting but I wouldn’t hire them” (no market fit)
  • You and Mike burn out or can’t sustain time investment (wrong team)
Phase 4 — July+
Case Study to Scale

If the pilot works, you have proof. Proof opens every door:

  • Easter Seals will listen (you have outcomes data)
  • Brynjolfsson will validate (you’ve tested his framework)
  • Raimondo will care (you’re a concrete implementation of her grand bargain)
  • Funders will engage (case study, not a deck)

Honest Assessment

Biggest Risk

Building supply (trained veterans) before confirming demand (employers who’ll hire them). Most workforce programs fail here. Antidote: find the employer partner BEFORE running the pilot.

Biggest Advantage

Speed. LearnAIR spent 5 months and got a landing page. You built a full thesis, competitive landscape, policy alignment, and field manual in 4 days.

The thing nobody’s saying: This might work better as content than as a program. If Mike starts posting about “mission command to AI supervision” on LinkedIn and it resonates, the audience becomes the distribution. The program follows the audience, not the other way around.
