Strategic Clarity

April 1, 2026. Read this first, not the 10 other files.

The Hypothesis

Employers will pay for veterans trained in AI operations supervision.

Not "veterans can learn AI." Not "mission command maps to AI supervision." Not "there's a gap in the competitive landscape." Those are supporting arguments. The load-bearing question is whether someone writes a check.

We have zero employer conversations. All work to date has been supply-side: what to teach, who teaches it, how it maps to military ranks, what the landscape looks like. The thesis is strong. The proof is missing.

The One Proof Point

3 employer discovery conversations before April 7.

Warm discovery with people Mike already knows: Break Line, Joshua Rajo (Volterra), Deloitte contacts.

The question isn't "would you hire an AI operations supervisor?" It's:

  1. Where are you at with AI adoption?
  2. What's working, what's not?
  3. Where's the gap between what your team can do and what you need them to do?
  4. If that gap had a name, what would you call it?
  5. What would it be worth to close it?

If three different employers describe a problem that sounds like "my people are using AI but nobody's governing the outputs," we've validated demand. If they describe something different, the thesis adapts.

The Consultancy Framing

We are not employees. We are not volunteers. We are a consultancy that owns its IP.

  • Protects the IP we've already built
  • Two-way doors with LearnAIR instead of one-way doors (employment)
  • Allows us to take the same IP to other partners (Deloitte, Break Line, direct to employers)
  • De-risks the relationship while we're still in discovery
  • Positions us as partners, not hires

Next step: Form an LLC. 10 minutes. Do it before April 7.

The Arc for Stakeholder Conversations

Every conversation with LearnAIR stakeholders follows the same shape:

Build momentum (first third)

Share the excitement. The opportunity is massive, the competitive landscape is open, policy tailwinds are real. Wave-top references to our work. Let them feel the energy.

Explore competencies (middle third)

Talk about the real challenges. Curriculum design, employer outreach, sales, program management. Watch where people lean in. What they engage with reveals what they bring.

Direct questions (final third)

After rapport is built, ask the hard ones. What's your commitment? What are you going to build? What does success look like to you? People open up more at the top of the hill than at the base.
