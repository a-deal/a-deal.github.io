Connecting with Erik Brynjolfsson — Stanford Digital Economy Lab

March 2026

Who He Is

Erik Brynjolfsson is the Jerry Yang and Akiko Yamazaki Professor at Stanford, Senior Fellow at the Stanford Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence (HAI), and Director of the Stanford Digital Economy Lab (DEL). He is one of the most cited researchers on AI’s impact on labor markets and productivity.

His landmark “Canaries in the Coal Mine” paper (Nov 2025) used ADP payroll data from 25M+ US workers to show a 13% relative employment decline for ages 22–25 in AI-exposed occupations since ChatGPT launched — while older, experienced workers remained stable or grew 6–9%. His core finding: augmentative AI usage does not cause job loss; only automative AI does.

Key concepts from his research:

  • The Jagged Frontier — AI capability is uneven across tasks; you cannot predict from difficulty alone
  • The Productivity J-Curve — GPTs suppress measured productivity during investment phase, then enter a harvest phase (US productivity jumped ~2.7% in 2025)
  • The Turing Trap — excessive focus on AI that replaces humans vs. AI that augments them erodes worker bargaining power
  • Nowcasting vs. Forecasting — measure real-time labor data instead of building models about the future

His finding that augmentative AI doesn’t cause job loss is the research backbone of our thesis: train veterans to supervise AI (augmentation), not be replaced by it.

Why He Matters for LearnAIR

Nobody has applied Brynjolfsson’s augmentation/automation framework to veterans specifically. This is genuine whitespace — in both his research portfolio and the broader academic literature.

Our thesis is a concrete implementation of his research:

  • Veterans aren’t juniors — they have the tacit knowledge and judgment that makes workers AI-resistant
  • Military mission command maps directly to AI operations supervision (augmentation, not automation)
  • His “Generative AI at Work” paper showed +34% productivity for novice workers — veterans are “novices” to AI but experienced in judgment, making them the highest-leverage learners
  • His emphasis on skill adjacencies over full retraining aligns perfectly — military skills are a short bridge to AI ops, not a long detour
The bridge to Raimondo: Brynjolfsson is an AESG member alongside Gina Raimondo. Building a relationship with him first creates the warm introduction to her through the Aspen Economic Strategy Group. This is the most natural path.

The Play — DEL Monday Seminars

The Stanford Digital Economy Lab hosts free, weekly seminars every Monday from noon to 1pm at Gates Computer Science Building, Room 119 on the Stanford campus. Brynjolfsson hosts these personally.

This is a room-sized event, not an auditorium. The format allows for real interaction — show up early, sit near the front, stay after for conversation. The intimacy is the advantage.

Andrew attended the March 17 Hoover talk where Brynjolfsson was on Panel 1 (“Augmentation or Automation?”) alongside representatives from LinkedIn and Anthropic. This gives a natural touchpoint: reference the Hoover discussion when introducing yourself at DEL.
