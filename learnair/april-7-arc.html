Beat 2: The Job

2 MINUTES

Before the framework, show the job. Make them picture it.

Lead with one workflow example: A healthcare company deploys an AI to review insurance claims and pre-approve routine cases. It works. But someone has to define the approval criteria, catch false denials, audit the system's decision patterns, and adapt the rules when regulations change. That someone isn't a developer. They're not a data scientist. They're an operations supervisor who thinks in terms of intent, delegation, exception handling, and feedback loops.

That role is emerging across every sector right now:

  • Federal: AI triages FOIA requests. Supervisor sets rules of engagement for what's auto-approved vs. flagged.
  • Logistics: AI manages inventory forecasting. Supervisor intervenes during novel conditions (supply disruptions, demand spikes).
  • Business services: AI flags risk clauses in contracts. Supervisor validates critical calls and decides when to override.

Companies are deploying these systems and discovering nobody knows how to manage them. Brynjolfsson at Stanford is measuring it: 20-30% productivity gains, but only when humans stay in the loop as supervisors, not operators.

Spoken version

Pick ONE workflow (healthcare is the clearest). Walk through it in 60 seconds. Then: "This pattern is showing up in federal, logistics, business services, everywhere AI is being deployed. The question is: who do you train for this role?"

Beat 3: The Bridge

2 MINUTES

Now reveal why veterans. And not just officers. The full military structure.

That supervisor role requires a specific cognitive framework: set intent clearly, delegate execution, validate outputs, adapt when conditions change. That's not a new skill set. It's mission command. And it exists at every level of the military, not just the top.

Mike mapped it across the full military ladder:

Military Role AI Ecosystem Equivalent Core Translation
Field Grade Officers (O4+) AI Strategy & Architecture System design, organizational AI vision
Junior Grade Officers (O1-O3) AI Ecosystem Orchestrator Workflow design, decision authority, cross-functional coordination
Staff NCOs (E6-E9) AI Operations & Reliability Lead System stability, QA frameworks, workforce execution
NCOs (E4-E5) AI Workflow Operator & Team Lead Task orchestration, direct supervision, prompt engineering

The "AI Platoon Model" maps it clean:

  • Intent (Strategy Layer): Business objective. Field grade territory.
  • Planning (JO Layer): Workflow design, tool selection, resource allocation.
  • Execution (SNCO/NCO Layer): AI + human task execution, quality control, tempo.
  • Feedback (Shared): QA, iteration, improvement loops at every level.

This isn't a metaphor. Marines, soldiers, and airmen already operate in mission command environments: decentralized execution under commander's intent, where human judgment governs semi-autonomous systems under uncertainty. They just haven't applied it to AI ecosystems yet.

Spoken version

"That healthcare supervisor we described? Set the intent, delegate, validate, adapt. The military calls that mission command. It exists at every level, from NCOs managing execution to officers designing strategy. We mapped the full ladder from E4 to O4+. Each level has a distinct AI role. We're not teaching veterans a new way to think. We're building a bridge from what they already know."

Show 2-3 rows of the table. Let the AI Platoon Model visual land. Don't over-explain.

Beat 4: The Roadmap

3 MINUTES

One pipeline. Three tracks. Each serves a different audience, a different funding source, and a different outcome. Self-selecting, not prescriptive.

Track 1: SEPS/TAPS Feeder (Government Funded)

Audience:

Transitioning service members, any grade, during their separation week.

Format: 2-hour AI ecosystem introduction embedded in existing SEPS/TAPS programming.

Outcome: Certification + capstone project in target market sector. Separates them on a resume. Also serves as the hook: "If this resonated, here's the next program."

Funding: Branch of service (already pays for SEPS/TAPS content).

Why it's first: Lowest cost to validate. LearnAIR already has curriculum. Government already funds it. Captures people at the transition moment.

Track 2: Transitioning Vet Program (L&D Network Funded)

Audience:

Veterans already out, in career transition. Break Line members, BetterUp alumni, job seekers.

Format: 4-week intensive. Project-based, not lecture-based.

  • Weeks 1-2: Map military experience to AI supervision framework. Tools in context.
  • Weeks 3-4: Live capstone project for a real employer in their target sector.

Outcome: Certification + capstone + job placement tracking.

Funding: L&D social network partnerships (Break Line, BetterUp, potentially LearnAIR).

Why it matters: This is the outcomes-based track. Placement data is the moat.

Track 3: In-Employer Upskilling (Employer Funded)

Audience:

Veterans already employed who want to move into AI operations roles within their current company.

Format: 4-week part-time program alongside current role.

Outcome: Certification + employer-executed capstone project (real problem, real deliverable).

Funding: Employer-sponsored (training budget, professional development).

Why it matters: No job placement needed. The employer already has the person. They just need the capability uplift.

Sequencing

"We start with Track 1 because it's the lowest cost to validate and the widest net. Tracks 2 and 3 activate once we have signal from the feeder. The feeder also vets who's ready for the intensive programs."

The Capstone (across all tracks)

The capstone is not a homework assignment. It's a live project for a real employer. In the intake form before the program starts, we ask: "What are the top 3-5 employers you'd want to work for?" We build a bottoms-up map of demand from day one. The employer evaluates the capstone output. That's the ultimate signal: did the graduate produce something the employer would hire for?

Spoken version

"We see one pipeline with three tracks, each serving a different audience and a different funding source. Track 1 is a SEPS/TAPS feeder: low cost, government funded, captures people at the transition moment. Track 2 is a 4-week intensive for transitioners with job placement. Track 3 is employer-funded upskilling for vets already working. We start with Track 1 because it validates cheapest. The capstone in every track is a live project for a real employer, not a classroom exercise. How does this map to what you're seeing?"

Defense (if they try to collapse tracks): "Each track has a different audience, funder, and outcome. Collapsing them waters down the value prop for each. The feeder is wide net. The intensive is the differentiated product. The employer track is the revenue engine."

K Framework: The Underlying Pattern

Mike's work maps to Komoroske's rubber bands model. The Marine Corps doesn't use rigid control or no control. It uses elastic constraints: commander's intent pulls units toward alignment while allowing autonomous execution. That's the AI supervision model in one image.

The line cooks vs. chefs distinction is the tier gap. LearnAIR's current curriculum trains line cooks (follow prompts, use tools). Mike's framework trains chefs (set intent, design systems, evaluate outputs). The April 7 pitch needs to make this distinction without insulting what LearnAIR already does. Frame it as: "You've built the foundation. We're proposing the next floor."

Optimization ratchet warning: If LearnAIR optimizes for "get veterans jobs fast" (Trisha confirmed revenue is the primary driver), they'll build a placement pipeline that measures speed and volume. That caps the program at tactical. The tension between revenue motivation and program quality is the load-bearing risk.
