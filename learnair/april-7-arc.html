Beat 2: The Job

2-3 MINUTES

Before the framework, it's important to see the job.

Your students are already seeing results. The woman who built an HR compliance hub in a week. The individual who completed four months of work in a single week. That's AI literacy. That's real.

Now shift the lens.

Imagine she's not just using AI for her own tasks. She's managing a system of AI agents operating across her entire function. She defines the intent. Sets the approval criteria. Reviews outputs. Catches errors. Adapts the system as conditions change.

That's a different role entirely. That's the role we're training for.

And it's not isolated to one industry. This role is emerging across every sector:

  • Healthcare and life sciences
  • Government and public sector
  • Financial services and insurance
  • Logistics and manufacturing
  • Technology
  • Business services
  • Small to mid-sized businesses

The gap is specific. Organizations are adopting AI, but workforce redesign is lagging and there's no clear leader training the people responsible for supervising it. Current employees are struggling because they don't have management training in an AI ecosystem. They're not used to context switching, setting up systems and rules, or evaluating whether something that they haven't worked on themselves is any good.

And the requirement is not deep technical skill. It's operational judgment.

The individual doesn't need to build the system. They need to command it.

Can you define clear intent, evaluate whether the output meets the standard, and intervene when it doesn't? That's the qualification.

Supporting evidence (depth if questioned)

Matt Schwartz at Harvard supervised Claude through a publishable theoretical physics paper in 2 weeks vs. the typical 1 year. Claude's output looked professional but was fundamentally wrong: it adjusted parameters to match expected plots, invented coefficients, asserted results without derivation. Schwartz caught every error because he'd done the grunt work decades ago. That's the supervision gap we're filling.

Sources: Karamanis, "The Machines Are Fine," ergosphere.blog, March 2026; Schwartz, "Vibe Physics," Anthropic Science Blog, March 2026.

Beat 3: The Bridge

2-3 MINUTES

Now, why veterans.

That supervisor role requires a specific cognitive approach: set clear intent, delegate execution, validate outputs, and adapt as conditions change.

That's not a new skill set. The military has been training it for decades.

What veterans bring is not just a framework, but an experience-based operating management mindset:

  • Structured execution frameworks: Mission command, MCPP, BAMCIS, OSMEAC. Proven approaches to planning and executing under uncertainty.
  • Decentralized execution philosophy: Commander's intent drives action. You don't micromanage, you define the outcome and trust the execution layer.
  • Tacit knowledge from ambiguity: Pattern recognition with incomplete information. Prioritization under constraint. Managing friction across people, systems, and time.
  • Transferable leadership and soft skills: Coaching, communication across levels, rapid alignment, and leading without deep domain expertise.
  • Ecosystem thinking: Operating in systems of systems. Understanding inputs, outputs, dependencies, and feedback loops.

We mapped this across the full military leadership ladder:

Military Role AI Ecosystem Equivalent Core Translation
Field Grade Officers (O4-O6) AI Ecosystem Director / Systems Leader Enterprise-level orchestration, multi-system integration, strategic intent setting, cross-organizational alignment
Junior Grade Officers (O1-O3) AI Ecosystem Orchestrator Workflow design, decision authority, cross-functional coordination
Staff NCOs (E6-E9) AI Operations & Reliability Lead System stability, QA frameworks, workforce execution
NCOs (E4-E5) AI Workflow Operator & Team Lead Task orchestration, direct supervision, prompt execution

This isn't a metaphor. Service members already operate in environments where human judgment directs semi-autonomous systems under uncertainty.

They just haven't applied it to AI ecosystems yet.

And this initiative addresses a long-standing challenge: not transitioning veterans into the workforce, but translating them. Translating their identity, their frameworks, and their capabilities into an environment where they have a natural advantage.

Spoken version

"That supervisor role we described, set intent, delegate, validate, adapt, the military calls that mission command. But it's more than a framework. It's an experience-based operating management mindset. The philosophies, the tacit knowledge from operating in ambiguity, the leadership skills, and the experience of managing complex systems. Veterans bring the full package. We mapped this from NCOs through field grade officers, and each level aligns directly to a role in an AI ecosystem. We're not teaching veterans a new way to think. We're building a bridge from what they already know to where the demand is."

Beat 4: The Roadmap

3-4 MINUTES

One pipeline. Three tracks. Each serves a different audience, a different funding source, and a different outcome. They are not sequential. They can operate independently.

Track 1: SEPS/TAPS Feeder (Government Funded)

Audience:

Transitioning service members, any grade, during separation.

Format: A 2-hour introduction embedded within SEPS/TAPS. Focused on exposing the opportunity: what AI-enabled roles look like, where they exist across sectors, and how their existing experience translates.

Outcome: Awareness and activation. They leave with a clear understanding of the opportunity and begin to see themselves in an AI ecosystem role. It functions as a filter: who is interested, who has aptitude, who wants to pursue it further.

Funding: Government-funded through existing SEPS/TAPS programming.

Why it comes first: Lowest cost, fastest signal. Captures individuals at the moment of transition. We have the network introductions ready to initiate this track. It creates pull into the deeper tracks, but does not depend on them.

Track 2: Transitioning Veteran Program (L&D Network or Government Funded)

Audience:

Veterans in transition. Job seekers, L&D network members, and SEPS/TAPS follow-on participants.

Format: 4-week intensive. Project-based.

  • Weeks 1-2: Translate military experience into AI supervision. Tools in context. Build AI agent capability set.
  • Weeks 3-4: Live capstone with a real employer.

Outcome: Certification, capstone, and a configured set of AI agents ready for deployment. Job placement tracking tied to outcomes.

Funding: L&D partnerships or government extension.

Why it matters: This is the proof layer. Placement data becomes the differentiator.

Track 3: In-Employer Upskilling (Employer Funded)

Audience:

Veterans already employed who want to move into AI operations roles within their current company.

Format: 4-week part-time program alongside current role.

Outcome: Certification, employer-executed capstone, and a configured set of AI agents for their specific employer context.

Funding: Employer-sponsored (training budget, professional development).

Why it matters: No placement needed. The employer already has the person. They want the capability uplift.

Sequencing

The tracks operate independently. Track 1 creates pull. Track 2 proves outcomes. Track 3 drives revenue.

The Identity Shift (all tracks)

The most important outcome isn't the certification or the capstone. It's that veterans see themselves as capable of leading in AI-enabled ecosystems. Not just AI literate, but AI capable. They walk away with the confidence, grounded in demonstrated competency, that they can command these systems. That's the identity shift. Don't forget the human part.

We have the network introductions ready to get this started. Track 2 is a four-week intensive for transitioning veterans. Defense (if they try to collapse tracks): "Each track has a different audience, funder, and outcome. Track 1 creates pull. Track 2 proves outcomes. Track 3 drives revenue."

K Framework: The Underlying Pattern

Mike's work maps to Komoroske's rubber bands model. The Marine Corps doesn't use rigid control or no control. It uses elastic constraints: commander's intent pulls units toward alignment while allowing autonomous execution. That's the AI supervision model in one image.

The line cooks vs. chefs distinction is the tier gap. LearnAIR's current curriculum trains line cooks (follow prompts, use tools). Mike's framework trains chefs (set intent, design systems, evaluate outputs). The April 7 pitch needs to make this distinction without insulting what LearnAIR already does. Frame it as: "You've built the foundation. We're proposing the next floor."

Optimization ratchet warning: If LearnAIR optimizes for "get veterans jobs fast" (Trisha confirmed revenue is the primary driver), they'll build a placement pipeline that measures speed and volume. That caps the program at tactical. The tension between revenue motivation and program quality is the load-bearing risk.
