Beat 2: The Job

2-3 MINUTES

Before the framework, show the job. Make them picture it. Anchor on what LearnAIR students have already accomplished, then show the gap our thesis fills.

Start with the familiar: Your students are already seeing results. The woman who built an HR compliance hub in one week. The guy who completed four months of work in a single week. That's AI literacy. That's real. Now imagine she's not just using AI for her own tasks. She's managing a team of AI agents doing that for her entire department. Defining the approval criteria. Catching errors the AI misses. Adapting the system when regulations change. That's a different role entirely. That's what we're training for.

The workflow example (healthcare): A healthcare company deploys an AI to review insurance claims and pre-approve routine cases. It works. But someone has to define the approval criteria, catch false denials, audit the system's decision patterns, and adapt the rules when regulations change. That someone isn't a developer. It also may not be a healthcare professional. They're an operations supervisor who thinks in terms of intent, delegation, exception handling, and feedback loops.

That role is emerging across every sector:

  • Healthcare: AI reviews claims. Supervisor defines criteria and catches exceptions.
  • Federal: AI triages FOIA requests. Supervisor sets rules for what's auto-approved vs. flagged.
  • Logistics & supply chain: AI manages inventory forecasting. Supervisor intervenes during novel conditions.
  • Business services: AI flags risk clauses in contracts. Supervisor validates critical calls.
  • SMBs & private sector broadly: AI handles customer service, scheduling, invoicing. Supervisor manages quality and escalation.

The gap is clear and the demand is growing. Brynjolfsson at Stanford is measuring it: 20-30% productivity gains, but only when humans stay in the loop as supervisors, not just operators. Raimondo at Commerce is asking the question at the policy level. The competitive landscape confirms it: 23 organizations in this space, and not one of them trains for the supervision role.

The technical sophistication question: What level of technical skill does this require? Less than you'd think, if the tools are pre-configured. The veteran doesn't need to build the AI system. They need to command it. Can you write a clear intent, evaluate whether the output is good enough, and intervene when it's not? That's the qualification. That's the rifle marksmanship equivalent.

Spoken version

Start with LearnAIR's own success stories. "Your students are already seeing these results at the individual level. What we're building is the next layer: people who can manage AI at the team and organizational level. Here's what that looks like in healthcare..." Walk through one workflow in 60 seconds. Then: "This pattern is showing up across healthcare, federal, logistics, business services, SMBs. The demand is documented. The gap is that nobody is training for the supervision role. That's what we do."

Beat 3: The Bridge

2-3 MINUTES

Now reveal why veterans. Not just the military framework, but the full package: philosophies, dynamics of operating in ambiguous ecosystems, tacit knowledge, soft skills, and the lived experience of commanding in uncertainty.

That supervisor role requires a specific cognitive framework: set intent clearly, delegate execution, validate outputs, adapt when conditions change. That's not a new skill set. Veterans already possess it.

What veterans bring (the full picture)

  • Military frameworks: Mission command (MCPP), BAMCIS, OSMEAC. Structured approaches to planning and executing under uncertainty.
  • Philosophy of decentralized execution: Commander's intent as the governing principle. You don't micromanage, you set the intent and trust the execution layer.
  • Tacit knowledge from ambiguous ecosystems: Pattern recognition under incomplete information. Prioritization under constraint. Managing friction across people, systems, and time pressure.
  • Soft skills that transfer directly: Coaching and mentorship. Communication up and down chain. Building alignment quickly. Leading without deep domain expertise. Emotional regulation in chaos.
  • Ecosystem dynamics experience: Operating in systems of systems. Understanding inputs, processes, outputs, feedback loops. Managing dependencies and logistics.

Mike mapped these across the full military ladder:

Military Role AI Ecosystem Equivalent Core Translation
Junior Grade Officers (O1-O3) AI Ecosystem Orchestrator Workflow design, decision authority, cross-functional coordination
Staff NCOs (E6-E9) AI Operations & Reliability Lead System stability, QA frameworks, workforce execution
NCOs (E4-E5) AI Workflow Operator & Team Lead Task orchestration, direct supervision, prompt engineering

The "AI Platoon Model" maps it clean:

  • Intent (Strategy Layer): Business objective. Comes from the employer.
  • Planning (Staff NCOs + JOs): Workflow design, tool selection, resource allocation.
  • Execution (NCOs + Staff NCOs + JOs): AI + human task execution, quality control, tempo.
  • Feedback (All levels): QA, iteration, improvement loops.

This isn't a metaphor. Marines, soldiers, sailors, and airmen already operate in mission command dynamic environments. Decentralized execution under commander's intent, where human judgment governs semi-autonomous systems under uncertainty. They just haven't applied it to an AI ecosystem yet.

And this initiative is answering the age-old challenge of translating service members into the civilian workforce. Not transitioning. Translating. Translating the identity, the frameworks, and the capabilities into a new ecosystem where they have a natural advantage.

Spoken version

"That healthcare supervisor we described? Set the intent, delegate, validate, adapt. The military calls that mission command. But it's not just the framework. It's the philosophies, the tacit knowledge from operating in ambiguous environments, the soft skills, the experience of managing complex ecosystems. Veterans bring the full package. We mapped it from NCOs through junior grade officers. Each level has a distinct AI role. We're not teaching veterans a new way to think. We're building a bridge from what they already know to where the demand is."

Beat 4: The Roadmap

3-4 MINUTES

One pipeline. Three tracks. Each serves a different audience, a different funding source, and a different outcome. Self-selecting by circumstance, not prescriptive by rank.

Track 1: SEPS/TAPS Feeder (Government Funded)

Audience:

Transitioning service members, any grade, during their separation week.

Format: 2-hour AI ecosystem introduction embedded in existing SEPS/TAPS programming. Approachable agentic work at the squad level: here are 3-4 AI agents, here's a real task, command the squad to deliver the output.

Outcome: Not a capstone. The outcome is the hook. They experience commanding AI agents firsthand, they see the power, and they want the follow-on curriculum. It also serves as a vetting function: who lights up, who has aptitude, who wants more.

Funding: Branch of service (already pays for SEPS/TAPS content).

Why it's first: Lowest cost to validate. LearnAIR already has curriculum foundation. Government already funds it. Captures people at the transition moment. But it doesn't necessarily inform Tracks 2 and 3. Those can activate independently.

Track 2: Transitioning Vet Program (L&D Network or Government Funded)

Audience:

Veterans already out, in career transition. Break Line members, BetterUp alumni, job seekers. Also potentially a government-funded extension to SEPS/TAPS for those who want to continue after Track 1.

Format: 4-week intensive. Project-based, not lecture-based.

  • Weeks 1-2: Map military experience to AI supervision framework. Tools in context. Build your AI agent rolodex.
  • Weeks 3-4: Live capstone project for a real employer in their target sector.

Outcome: Certification + capstone + AI agent rolodex (they leave with a configured squad of agents, ready to deploy at an employer) + job placement tracking.

Funding: L&D social network partnerships (Break Line, BetterUp) OR government-funded as SEPS/TAPS follow-on.

Why it matters: This is the outcomes-based track. Placement data is the moat. The graduate doesn't just have a certificate. They have a working fire team of AI agents and a capstone that proves they can command it.

Track 3: In-Employer Upskilling (Employer Funded)

Audience:

Veterans already employed who want to move into AI operations roles within their current company.

Format: 4-week part-time program alongside current role.

Outcome: Certification + employer-executed capstone project (real problem, real deliverable) + AI agent/digital employee rolodex configured for their specific employer context.

Funding: Employer-sponsored (training budget, professional development).

Why it matters: No job placement needed. The employer already has the person. They want the capability uplift. And the graduate comes back to work with a squad of AI agents ready to deploy.

Sequencing

The three tracks can activate independently. Track 1 is the lightest lift and captures people at the transition moment. Tracks 2 and 3 don't depend on Track 1, but Track 1 can serve as a hook that feeds into Track 2 for transitioning vets who want more.

The Capstone (Tracks 2 and 3)

The capstone is not a homework assignment. It's a live project for a real employer. In the intake form before the program starts, we ask: "What are the top 3-5 employers you'd want to work for?" We build a bottoms-up map of demand from day one.

The Identity Shift (all tracks)

The most important outcome isn't the capstone or the certification. It's that veterans see themselves as AI capable, not just AI literate. They walk away with the confidence, grounded in demonstrated competency, that they can command AI-enabled systems. They see themselves as leaders in a new ecosystem, not just translators from an old one. This maps directly to your tagline: don't forget the human part.

Spoken version

"We see one pipeline with three tracks. Track 1 is a SEPS/TAPS feeder: two hours, government funded, hands-on experience commanding a squad of AI agents. It's the hook. Track 2 is a four-week intensive for transitioners: capstone project, job placement, and they leave with a configured AI agent rolodex ready to deploy. Track 3 is employer-funded upskilling for vets already working. All three tracks can activate independently. We start with Track 1 because it validates cheapest. But the real product is what happens after: veterans who don't just know about AI, but see themselves as leaders in AI-enabled ecosystems. That's the identity shift. Don't forget the human part."

Defense (if they try to collapse tracks): "Each track has a different audience, funder, and outcome. They can run independently. The feeder is the hook. The intensive is the product. The employer track is the revenue engine."

K Framework: The Underlying Pattern

Mike's work maps to Komoroske's rubber bands model. The Marine Corps doesn't use rigid control or no control. It uses elastic constraints: commander's intent pulls units toward alignment while allowing autonomous execution. That's the AI supervision model in one image.

The line cooks vs. chefs distinction is the tier gap. LearnAIR's current curriculum trains line cooks (follow prompts, use tools). Mike's framework trains chefs (set intent, design systems, evaluate outputs). The April 7 pitch needs to make this distinction without insulting what LearnAIR already does. Frame it as: "You've built the foundation. We're proposing the next floor."

Optimization ratchet warning: If LearnAIR optimizes for "get veterans jobs fast" (Trisha confirmed revenue is the primary driver), they'll build a placement pipeline that measures speed and volume. That caps the program at tactical. The tension between revenue motivation and program quality is the load-bearing risk.
