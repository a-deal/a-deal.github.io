Beat 1: The Bet

30 SECONDS

Employers will pay for veterans trained in AI operations supervision.

That's the hypothesis. Not "veterans can learn AI." Not "mission command is cool." The question is whether someone writes a check for this specific thing. We believe they will. We haven't proven it yet. Everything we're about to show you is the case for why the bet is worth making, and the plan for how we test it.

Spoken version

Beat 2: The Job

2 MINUTES

Before the framework, show the job. Make them picture it.

Lead with one workflow example: A healthcare company deploys an AI to review insurance claims and pre-approve routine cases. It works. But someone has to define the approval criteria, catch false denials, audit the system's decision patterns, and adapt the rules when regulations change. That someone isn't a developer. They're not a data scientist. They're an operations supervisor who thinks in terms of intent, delegation, exception handling, and feedback loops.

That role is emerging across every sector right now:

  • Federal: AI triages FOIA requests. Supervisor sets rules of engagement for what's auto-approved vs. flagged.
  • Logistics: AI manages inventory forecasting. Supervisor intervenes during novel conditions (supply disruptions, demand spikes).
  • Business services: AI flags risk clauses in contracts. Supervisor validates critical calls and decides when to override.

Companies are deploying these systems and discovering nobody knows how to manage them. Gina Raimondo at Commerce is asking the question at the policy level. Brynjolfsson at Stanford is measuring it: 20-30% productivity gains, but only when humans stay in the loop as supervisors, not operators.

Spoken version

Pick ONE workflow (healthcare is the clearest). Walk through it in 60 seconds. Then: "This pattern is showing up in federal, logistics, business services, everywhere AI is being deployed. The question is: who do you train for this role?"

Beat 3: The Bridge

2 MINUTES

Now reveal why veterans.

That supervisor role requires a specific cognitive framework: set intent clearly, delegate execution, validate outputs, adapt when conditions change. That's not a new skill set. It's mission command.

Mike mapped it term by term:

Marine Corps AI Supervision
Commander's Intent Outcome definition + constraints
Mission Tactics Delegated AI task execution
Feedback Loop Output validation + refinement

This isn't a metaphor. A Marine field grade officer trained in MCPP already has the cognitive framework for AI supervision. They've spent years converting ambiguous reality into clear intent, integrated plans, disciplined execution, and adaptive feedback. They just haven't applied it to this domain yet.

Veterans don't need to learn how to think in systems. They need a bridge that translates what they already know into AI.

Spoken version

"That healthcare supervisor we just described? Set the intent, delegate, validate, adapt. Marines call that mission command. It's the same cognitive framework. We're not teaching veterans a new way to think. We're giving them a bridge to apply what they already know."

Show 2-3 rows of the table. Let it land. Don't over-explain.

Beat 4: The Roadmap

3 MINUTES

Two prongs. Not competing. Sequential.

Prong 1: AI Literacy (LearnAIR's strength)

Adapt your existing curriculum for a military audience. Delivered through TAPS or a 1-week follow-on. Teaches tool proficiency. This is where enlisted grades and junior officers start. It's also a vetting function: who's engaged, who has aptitude, who wants more. This is revenue now with what you've already built.

Prong 2: AI Supervision (new IP)

A 4-week intensive for Marine Corps officers, O1-O3. Project-based, not lecture-based.

  • Week 1: Map military experience to the AI supervision framework
  • Week 2: Tools in context (judgment, not proficiency)
  • Week 3: Live employer project, real ambiguity
  • Week 4: Portfolio, case study, placement prep

The phased approach to outcomes:

  • Phase 1: Put out the curriculum. Train Marines. Get testimonials. Prove the model works.
  • Phase 2: Track participants into jobs. Measure outcomes. Build the case studies.
  • Phase 3: Outcome data makes this a different category from every other program. That's the moat.

We don't need to be outcome-based on day one. But the roadmap gets us there. And the curriculum design comes after employer validation, not before. We need to hear from the companies who would hire our graduates before we finalize what we teach.

Spoken version

"We see two tracks. Track one is adapting what you've already built for a military audience. That's revenue now. Track two is a purpose-built program for AI supervision, designed for officers, grounded in mission command. We think track two is the bigger opportunity. How are you thinking about the relationship between those two?"

Two-track defense (rehearse this): If they try to merge the prongs: "Both are valuable. They're different work with different timelines. Prong 1 is revenue now. Prong 2 needs its own track."

K Framework: The Underlying Pattern

Mike's work maps to Komoroske's rubber bands model. The Marine Corps doesn't use rigid control or no control. It uses elastic constraints: commander's intent pulls units toward alignment while allowing autonomous execution. That's the AI supervision model in one image.

The line cooks vs. chefs distinction is the tier gap. LearnAIR's current curriculum trains line cooks (follow prompts, use tools). Mike's framework trains chefs (set intent, design systems, evaluate outputs). The April 7 pitch needs to make this distinction without insulting what LearnAIR already does. Frame it as: "You've built the foundation. We're proposing the next floor."

Optimization ratchet warning: If LearnAIR optimizes for "get veterans jobs fast" (Trisha confirmed revenue is the primary driver), they'll build a placement pipeline that measures speed and volume. That caps the program at tactical. The tension between revenue motivation and program quality is the load-bearing risk.
