Veterans + AI Workforce Initiative

Veterans are built to command AI. We train them to do it.

AI has given every individual 10x leverage. Every organization that can’t capture it will be outpaced by one that can. This isn’t a rupture. 01

Why This Gap Exists

Every enterprise deploying AI agents hits the same bottleneck: who supervises the machines? Not who builds them. Not who prompts them. Who sets the intent, monitors execution, catches errors, and maintains accountability when automated systems make decisions that affect real operations?

That role does not have a training pipeline. Veterans already possess the operating habits it requires.

What exists today

  • Deloitte AI Academy teaches prompt engineering -- how to talk to AI tools. It does not teach how to manage the operations those tools run.
  • Code Platoon teaches coding -- how to build software. It does not teach how to supervise AI systems that are already built.
  • SkillBridge teaches existing job skills -- placing veterans into roles that already exist. It does not create the new role that AI deployment demands.
  • OpenAI Veterans Forge teaches tool literacy -- a 4-hour sprint on ChatGPT. It does not teach workflow command.

Nobody teaches the person who sits between the AI system and the business outcome and makes sure it actually works.

That is what mission command trained you for -- managing complex systems under uncertainty, validating information before it moves up the chain, maintaining human accountability when execution is delegated.

Military mission command is decentralized execution under commander's intent. Service members learn to define objectives, set left and right limits, delegate execution, monitor outcomes, and escalate exceptions. That is a direct translation of AI workflow supervision: set the goal, configure the guardrails, review outputs, intervene when the system drifts.

  • Process discipline under pressure. Veterans validate information before passing it up, catch errors in workflows, and maintain quality when stakes are high.
  • Judgment under ambiguity. Current AI systems still require human interpretation, contextual reasoning, and accountable decision-making, especially in messy operational environments.
  • Trust and clearances. Many veterans hold active security clearances and bring credibility in government, healthcare, and defense-adjacent sectors where AI oversight demands high-trust operators.
"The AI operations supervisor is the most important role nobody is hiring for yet."

What LearnAIR does

Short, applied training that produces AI operations supervisors. Not tool literacy. Not prompt engineering. Workflow command. Participants graduate with a portfolio of real artifacts: workflow redesigns, guardrail documents, productivity analyses, and before-and-after operational cases that prove competence to employers.

The pitch

The market has AI literacy programs. It has veteran career programs. It has no veteran AI leadership program. LearnAIR fills that gap. The window is 12-18 months before Accenture, VET TEC 2.0, and big tech close it.

What we are testing

  • Will employers pay for this pipeline, or does the "AI supervisor" role fold into existing management without a distinct hiring signal?
  • Does the mission command framing land with civilian hiring managers, or is it only compelling to veterans?
  • Can a short-format program (4 weeks) produce graduates that employers rate as "would hire"?

Pilot Design
04

First Cohort: 4-Week Pilot

Sector: Federal contracting. Cohort: 15 participants. Format: 4 weeks part-time (10-12 hrs/week). First cohort cost: $19,657 ($1,310/student). Subsequent cohorts: $13,370.

Tools

Claude Pro, ChatGPT Plus, Microsoft Copilot, n8n (workflow automation), Miro (collaborative design).

Week 1
AI Landscape + Mission Command Mapping

Ground truth: where AI is deployed in federal operations today, what it does well, where it fails. Map the military mission command framework to civilian AI supervision: intent = objective, left/right limits = guardrails, delegated execution = agent autonomy, monitoring = human-in-the-loop.

  • Survey current AI deployments across government and defense contractors
  • Translate mission command vocabulary into civilian operations language
  • Identify the "AI supervisor" role across three federal workflow examples
  • Deliverable: personal mission command translation document
Week 2
Hands-on Workflow Design

Build it. Participants design AI-augmented workflows for real operational scenarios. Introduce the RACI-AI matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed + AI role). Hands-on with n8n for workflow automation.

  • RACI-AI matrix for a FOIA processing workflow
  • Build a working n8n automation for document intake and classification
  • Identify where human oversight is required vs. optional vs. unnecessary
  • Deliverable: workflow case study with before/after process maps
Week 3
Supervision Practicum

The core differentiator. Live AI agent supervision exercises. Participants manage running AI systems, catch errors, handle exceptions, and build guardrail engineering skills.

  • Supervised AI output review: identify hallucinations, bias, and quality drift
  • Guardrail engineering: design constraints that prevent common failure modes
  • Exception handling: when and how to override automated decisions
  • Deliverable: guardrail document + productivity analysis
Week 4
Portfolio Build + Capstone

Synthesize everything into employer-ready artifacts. Capstone presentations to an employer panel.

  • Compile portfolio: workflow case study, guardrail document, productivity analysis, mission command brief
  • Write AI competency statement (the civilian equivalent of a military evaluation)
  • Present capstone to employer panel for live feedback
  • Deliverable: complete portfolio + AI competency statement

Kill criteria

  • Week 2: If attendance drops below 70%, content engagement is flat, or participants consistently say "I don't see how this is different from a Google cert," diagnose immediately. The problem could be format (too much lecture), difficulty (too abstract), or relevance (wrong sector focus).

Applied Curriculum
05

Sector Workflows

Four sector-specific AI supervision workflows that form the applied curriculum. Each shows exactly what the "AI supervisor" role looks like in practice: not building models, not writing prompts, but running operations where AI handles the first pass and a human maintains command.

1. Federal/Public: FOIA Request Processing

AI-supervised pipeline from citizen request intake through response drafting. The supervisor sets classification criteria, reviews AI redaction recommendations, and approves final responses.

Step 1
Intake Classification
AI triages requests by complexity, agency, and document type
Step 2
Document Review
AI identifies responsive documents and flags sensitive content
Step 3
Redaction Recommendation
AI proposes redactions under exemption categories
Step 4
Human Review
Supervisor validates, overrides, and approves
Step 5
Response Drafting
AI generates response letter; supervisor signs off

Outcome: 40-60% reduction in processing time per request. The AI supervisor role is the quality gate between automated document review and public release.

2. Healthcare Admin: Prior Authorization + Denial Management

AI handles the administrative burden of insurance authorization and denial appeals. The supervisor monitors payer criteria matching, reviews appeal strategies, and tracks pattern detection for systemic issues.

Step 1
Auth Prep
AI assembles clinical documentation and prior auth requirements
Step 2
Payer Matching
AI matches criteria to specific payer rules and policies
Step 3
Denial Triage
AI categorizes denials by type, appealability, and urgency
Step 4
Appeal Letter
AI drafts appeal; supervisor reviews clinical accuracy
Step 5
Pattern Detection
AI identifies systemic denial patterns across payers

Outcome: Denial rate reduced from 15% to 10%. Appeal preparation time from 60 minutes to 20 minutes. The supervisor catches clinical nuances AI misses and owns the escalation decisions.

3. Logistics: Warehouse Exception Management

AI monitors warehouse operations, detects anomalies, recommends resolutions. The supervisor handles complex exceptions, validates AI root-cause analysis, and manages reporting to stakeholders.

Step 1
Detection
AI flags inventory discrepancies, shipping delays, quality holds
Step 2
Root Cause
AI analyzes patterns and proposes root cause
Step 3
Resolution
AI recommends corrective action from playbook
Step 4
Execution
Supervisor approves and dispatches resolution
Step 5
Reporting
AI compiles pattern reports for leadership

Outcome: 64% reduction in exception resolution time. The supervisor validates root-cause accuracy and makes judgment calls AI cannot: vendor relationships, customer impact, safety thresholds.

4. Business Services: Contract Review + Client Reporting

AI handles data aggregation, report generation, and compliance monitoring. The supervisor ensures accuracy, manages client relationships, and directs the AI knowledge base.

Step 1
Aggregation
AI pulls data from CRM, billing, project tools
Step 2
Report Gen
AI drafts client reports and QBR decks
Step 3
Compliance
AI flags contract terms, SLA status, risk items
Step 4
QBR Prep
Supervisor reviews, adds context, prepares narrative
Step 5
Knowledge Base
AI updates institutional knowledge from interactions

Outcome: Ops team freed from 60% to 25% admin time. The supervisor owns the client relationship, narrative framing, and strategic decisions that AI cannot make.

Validation Playbook
07

Interview Scripts

Three scripts for 25-35 minute conversations. The goal is signal extraction, not selling. Record every call (with permission) and debrief with notes within one hour.

Script A: Employer / Hiring Manager

Target: VP of Operations, Program Manager, or HR Director at a federal contractor or healthcare system with 500+ employees. Prioritize orgs with existing veteran hiring commitments.

"Thanks for making time. We're exploring a workforce training concept focused on veterans transitioning into AI-adjacent roles -- not building AI models, but supervising AI systems in operational settings: reviewing outputs, catching errors, managing workflows where AI does the first pass and a human makes the final call. We're early -- we haven't built anything yet -- and we're talking to people like you to understand whether this solves a real hiring problem. Nothing to buy, no pitch. I just want to learn from your experience."
Q1. Where is AI showing up in your operations today, and where do you expect it in 12-18 months? Listen for: specific use cases vs. vague "we're exploring." Concrete = real need.
Q2. When you think about the people who sit between the AI system and the final decision, what does that role look like? Listen for: whether they have thought about this at all. If they jump to "data scientist," the supervision framing has not landed.
Q3. How hard has it been to hire for roles requiring judgment under ambiguity -- people who follow a process but also flag when something is off? Listen for: specific pain. "Three reqs open for six months" beats "yeah, hiring is tough."
Q4. Have you hired a veteran specifically for their operational background? How did that work out? Listen for: whether the military-to-ops pipeline is a proven pattern or a new idea.
Q5. If a program produced candidates who could supervise AI-assisted workflows -- and those candidates were veterans with security clearances -- what would that pipeline be worth per hire? Listen for: a number, a range, or "we'd pay a recruiter fee." If they deflect entirely, willingness to pay is low.
Q6. What would you need to see before considering hiring from a program like this? Listen for: what drives hiring decisions. This shapes curriculum outputs.
Commitment test (Q7): "If we ran a pilot cohort of 10-15 veterans this summer, trained specifically on AI workflow supervision with a capstone project, would you be willing to interview the top graduates -- no obligation to hire, just review their work and give us feedback on readiness?" A "yes" here is real signal.
Green flags
  • Names a specific AI use case in production
  • Describes "human in the loop" problem unprompted
  • Puts a dollar figure on hiring cost
  • Volunteers to introduce you to a colleague
  • Asks when the first cohort graduates
Red flags
  • "AI is still a few years out for us"
  • Only talks about needing engineers or PhDs
  • Enthusiastic but will not commit to reviewing candidates
  • "We don't think about veteran hiring specifically"
  • Defers to hiring freeze or budget cycle

Script B: VSO / Workforce Leader

Target: Program Director at Hiring Our Heroes, BreakLine, a state workforce board, or a VSO with career transition programs. They control access to veteran cohorts and institutional credibility.

"Appreciate you taking this. We're designing a training program called LearnAIR that prepares transitioning service members for AI workflow supervision roles -- jobs where a person reviews, validates, and manages AI system outputs in fields like healthcare operations, government services, and logistics. We're not trying to turn veterans into software engineers. We're focused on the oversight and judgment layer. Before we build anything, we want to learn from organizations like yours."
Q1. What are the top three job categories your transitioning veterans land in? Where do they struggle most? Listen for: whether "operations" or "process management" shows up naturally.
Q2. When employers ask for veteran candidates, what skills do they request most? Has that changed recently? Listen for: whether AI, automation, or "tech-adjacent" is on employers' radar.
Q3. How do you evaluate new training programs before recommending them? What makes you trust a program enough to put your name behind it? Listen for: the actual bar. This tells you exactly what you need to earn credibility.
Q4. Have you seen AI-specific training programs targeting veterans? What worked, what fell short? Listen for: awareness of the landscape. If they name programs, ask what those programs got wrong.
Q5. If 70%+ of graduates landed relevant roles within 90 days, what would a partnership look like from your side? Listen for: what "partnership" means to them. Co-marketing? TAP class access? A slot in their calendar?
Commitment test (Q6): "If we designed a 4-week pilot this summer and needed 10-15 veteran participants, would your organization help recruit them -- even informally? And would you be open to a follow-up once we have a curriculum outline?"
Green flags
  • Describes gaps in existing tech-transition programs
  • "We could include this in our next cohort cycle"
  • Offers to introduce you to employer partners
  • Mentions GI Bill, VR&E, or WIOA unprompted
Red flags
  • "We already have an AI program" (not interested)
  • Only willing to "stay in touch"
  • Can not describe how they would recruit participants
  • Decision timeline is 6+ months for any new partnership

Script C: Transitioning Veteran

Target: E-5 to O-4, 6-15 years of service, within 12 months of transition (or recently separated within 24 months). Any branch, especially operations, intelligence, logistics, medical, or maintenance backgrounds.

"Thanks for your time -- I know transition is a busy period. We're building a training program for veterans that focuses on AI workflow supervision. That means: jobs where AI does a first draft or a first pass, and a human reviews it, catches mistakes, and makes the final call. Think quality assurance for AI systems, not programming them. It maps pretty directly to the kind of process discipline and judgment you've been using in the military. We haven't launched yet -- I want to hear whether this sounds useful, or whether I'm off base."
Q1. What is your plan for your next career step? What roles are you targeting? Listen for: "project management, operations, maybe tech" = core audience. Locked into specific track (nursing, cyber cert) = may not need this.
Q2. When you hear "AI," what comes to mind? Is it relevant to the work you want to do? Listen for: fear ("I'll be replaced"), curiosity ("I want to understand it"), or dismissal ("that's for engineers").
Q3. In your military role, how often did you review someone else's work, validate information, or catch errors in a process? Listen for: whether they describe AI-supervision-adjacent skills without realizing it.
Q4. If a program could help you land a $65K-$85K role in 90 days, where you supervise AI tools rather than build them, what would it need to look like for you to invest 4-6 weeks? Listen for: format preferences, salary expectations, what "invest" means to them.
Q5. What have you tried for career transition -- TAP, SkillBridge, bootcamps, certifications? What worked and what did not? Listen for: specific complaints. Past experience shapes how they evaluate your program.
Q6. If this cost $2,000 out of pocket -- or was free via GI Bill or workforce grant -- which path would you pursue? Listen for: willingness to pay (strongest signal) or hard resistance ("only if free" = different payer model).
Commitment test (Q7): "We're planning a pilot cohort this summer. If you qualified, would you apply? And -- separate question -- can you think of 2-3 people in your network who would also be interested? Would you share it with them?"
Green flags
  • Immediately connects military role to AI-supervision framing
  • Asks specific questions about the program
  • Names people they would refer
  • "I'd pay for this" or "I'd use my GI Bill"
Red flags
  • "Sounds cool" but no questions or follow-up
  • Only interested if completely free and zero time commitment
  • Sees AI as irrelevant to their future work
  • Cannot describe any process-oversight experience from service

GapHow to frame itAction to cite
No instructional designer "Our pilot curriculum is being developed in partnership with [advisor] who has designed workforce programs for [organization]." Name a specific advisor. A 30-minute conversation with an ID professional at a community college is enough to cite.
No AI/ML technical depth "Our technical curriculum is informed by [advisor] who leads AI implementation at [company]. We focus on applied judgment, not engineering." Reframe as intentional scoping. Still need one credible technical voice on the advisory board.
No workforce track record "Our team brings [X years] of combined military training and professional development experience." Military training IS workforce development. An NCO who ran training for 30 soldiers is closer to instructional design than most realize.
No veteran on team Be honest. Recruit a veteran advisor immediately. Do not launch without at least one veteran in a named, visible role. Non-negotiable. A program training veterans without veteran leadership will face justified skepticism from every stakeholder.

What funders actually evaluate

  • Specificity over superlatives. "10-year Army officer who managed 50-person teams" beats "decorated military leader with extensive experience."
  • Connection to the mission. "I watched 6 people in my platoon struggle to translate their skills" is more compelling than "I identified a market opportunity."
  • Awareness of what you don't know. Teams that acknowledge gaps and show plans to fill them are more credible than teams that pretend everything is figured out.
  • Evidence of traction. "We've conducted 20 validation interviews with employers" beats any credential.
  • Diversity of perspective. Three Army officers is less credible for a cross-service program than Army, Navy, and a civilian with workforce development experience.

"AI supervisor" role does not emerge as distinct job category High Medium Frame as skill overlay, not standalone job title. "AI operations leadership" is additive to project management, operations management, etc.
Employer willingness to pay is zero High Medium Validate early via commitment tests in interviews. If employers won't pay, pivot to government funding only (WIOA, HVRP, VET TEC).
Veterans see this as "just another bootcamp" High Medium Mission command framing must be authentic, not marketing. Curriculum must feel different on day one. Portfolio artifacts must be distinct from bootcamp projects.
Accenture or HOH launches competing program High High Speed advantage: 12-18 month window. Build curriculum IP and employer relationships that create switching costs. Pursue co-branding to turn competitors into partners.
Pilot cohort cannot be recruited in time Medium Medium Multiple recruitment channels: Easter Seals HVRP, VSO partners, SkillBridge pipeline, direct outreach to transitioning service members.
Funding delays (ETPL, VET TEC, GI Bill) Medium High Employer-pays model runs independently of government funding. Easter Seals HVRP as near-term DOL channel. Stack multiple applications to avoid single-point-of-failure.
Curriculum does not translate to employer readiness High Low Employer panel reviews capstones during pilot. Iterate curriculum based on real feedback. Portfolio artifacts designed to prove competence, not check boxes.
AI tools change faster than curriculum Low High Curriculum teaches judgment and process, not specific tools. Tools are interchangeable; the supervision framework is durable.
