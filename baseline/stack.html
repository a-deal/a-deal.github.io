The Intelligence Layer

Scoring Engine engine/scoring/

  • 20 health metrics across 2 tiers (Foundation + Enhanced)
  • NHANES 2017–2020 Pre-Pandemic continuous percentiles, survey-weighted, stratified by age bucket and sex
  • Clinical zone assessment (Optimal / Healthy / Borderline / Elevated) sourced from:
  • AHA/ACC 2017 (blood pressure)
  • ESC/EAS 2019 (ApoB)
  • ADA 2023 (glucose, HbA1c)
  • ACSM (VO2 max, age/sex stratified)
  • AHA/CDC (hs-CRP)
  • Endocrine Society (TSH, Vitamin D)
  • NHLBI/AHA (waist circumference)
  • Freshness decay—a lipid panel from 18 months ago gets ~33% credit, not full weight
  • Reliability multipliers—single BP readings count less than 7-day protocol averages
  • Weighted percentile composite using standing weights (Lp(a) downweighted since genetically fixed)
  • Gap analysis ranked by coverage leverage with cost-to-close for every missing metric

Insights Engine engine/insights/

  • Threshold-based coaching rules loaded from rules.yaml (configurable, not hardcoded)
  • Compound signals that connect metrics across domains:
  • Sleep debt accumulation (weekly tracking against target)
  • Deficit sustainability (weight loss rate × HRV × RHR interaction)
  • Sleep-deficit interaction—flags muscle loss risk citing Nedeltcheva 2010 (60% more muscle lost under sleep restriction at same deficit)
  • Taper readiness (when to start reverse diet based on proximity to target)
  • Nutrition deviation (unplanned surplus detection, late-meal sleep impact)
  • 90-day trend computation for HRV and RHR with directional interpretation
  • Four severity levels: critical, warning, positive, neutral—the agent uses these to prioritize what to surface

Protocols Engine engine/coaching/protocols.py + protocols/

  • YAML-defined phased protocols with habit tracking and outcome metrics
  • Active protocols today:
  • Sleep Stack—6-week progressive protocol with 9 habits, 3 phases, nicotine taper integrated, outcome tracking against Garmin sleep duration and regularity
  • Hair Recovery—finasteride/minoxidil protocol with phase tracking
  • Skincare—morning/evening routines with product-specific tracking
  • Each protocol computes: day number, week number, current phase, last night’s habit completion, phase average completion rate, top nudge (highest-priority missed habit), outcome metric status vs targets
  • Nudges are opinionated: “Hit your cutoff time,” not “consider your caffeine intake”

The Graveyard Lesson, Applied

Your graveyard analysis identified four failure modes. Here’s how the running stack addresses each one.

Lark failed because scripted AI couldn’t adapt

Lark’s conversational AI was rule trees pretending to be intelligent. When a user went off-script, it broke.

The health-engine doesn’t script conversations. It provides structured data—NHANES percentiles, clinical zones, compound coaching signals—to an LLM that has genuine reasoning capability. The insights engine generates severity-ranked observations; the LLM decides how to deliver them based on context. The coaching voice in CLAUDE.md sets the personality (“direct, warm but not soft, like a trainer who knows your numbers”), but the conversation itself is emergent.

The difference: Lark had to anticipate every user path. The health-engine provides ground truth; the LLM navigates.

Operator-era companies died on unit economics

Every “AI health coach” from 2015–2020 that put a human in the loop eventually hit the same wall: per-user costs scaled linearly with users. Noom’s coaches handle 200+ users each, diluting the coaching to the point of irrelevance.

The health-engine’s marginal cost per user is approximately zero. The intelligence layer runs locally. The LLM call is the only variable cost, and at current Sonnet pricing, a full daily coaching session costs roughly $0.02. There are no human operators in the loop.

The difference: MCP tools + LLM replaces the human operator entirely. The program runs the coaching; the LLM runs the conversation.

Noom proved that programs work

Noom’s insight was correct: structured behavior change programs outperform pure tracking. Their problem was delivery—watered-down coaching at scale.

The protocols engine is opinionated by design. The sleep stack has 9 habits with evidence-based rationale for each, 3 phases that progressively tighten constraints, and outcome metrics that close the loop against Garmin data. It doesn’t ask what you want to do. It tells you what to do this week and tracks whether you did it.

The difference: Same programmatic structure as Noom, zero dilution in delivery. The protocol runs at full fidelity for every user.

Omada proved that specificity wins

Omada’s diabetes prevention program worked because it was clinically specific—targeted population, validated protocol, measurable outcomes. But it required clinical staff and health plan integration to operate.

The scoring engine is built on the same clinical foundations. ApoB thresholds from ESC/EAS 2019. Fasting glucose zones from ADA 2023. VO2 max stratified by age and sex from ACSM. The gap analysis doesn’t just say “get a blood test”—it says “a $30 lipid panel with ApoB add-on would close your highest-leverage coverage gap.” Clinical specificity without clinical overhead.

The difference: Clinical-grade assessment delivered through an agent, not a clinic. Population health infrastructure at individual scale.

This is the architectural bet: build the intelligence once, deliver it through whatever surface the user actually lives in.

Market Signal

On March 18, 2026—the same day I shipped the agent stack—Brit Morin (Offline Ventures founding partner) publicly posted her OpenClaw agent configuration on LinkedIn. Daily briefings, health monitoring, financial tracking. She described the same infrastructure pattern: agent on always-on hardware, tools for domain-specific intelligence, proactive scheduling via cron.

This is not a coincidence. The agent-as-personal-staff pattern is emerging among technically sophisticated early adopters. Health is the deepest vertical for this pattern because:

  1. Data density—health generates more heterogeneous data streams than any other personal domain (wearables, labs, meals, habits, medications, symptoms, biometrics)
  2. Longitudinal value—health data compounds over months and years in ways that financial or productivity data doesn’t
  3. Coaching leverage—the gap between knowing what to do and doing it is largest in health, which is exactly where a proactive agent adds value
  4. Clinical grounding—health has established population benchmarks (NHANES) and clinical thresholds (AHA, ADA) that give the intelligence layer real authority

The question isn’t whether agents will run health coaching. It’s who builds the intelligence layer that makes them good at it.

The One Thing That Cannot Change

Your closing is right, and I want to echo it with the same emphasis.

The program must stay opinionated.

The protocols engine enforces this structurally:

  • 3 habits max per focus area—not a suggestion, a constraint in the protocol YAML
  • Phased progression—the sleep stack doesn’t give you all 9 habits on day 1. Weeks 1–2 build the routine. Weeks 3–4 dial it in. Weeks 5–6 sustain
  • Clinical thresholds, not vibes—ApoB < 80 mg/dL is the ESC/EAS 2019 target. We don’t soften it
  • Outcome tracking—every protocol defines measurable outcomes and checks them against real data. The sleep stack targets 7.5hr duration and < 30min regularity stddev, measured by Garmin, not self-report
  • Nudges, not lectures—“Get to bed by 11 tonight” beats “prioritize sleep hygiene”

The intelligence layer is the moat. Not the app. Not the agent. Not the channel. The scoring engine that knows ApoB matters more than LDL-C. The insights engine that connects sleep debt to muscle loss during a deficit. The protocols engine that runs a real program, not a feature tour.

The agent runs the program. The program is the moat.
