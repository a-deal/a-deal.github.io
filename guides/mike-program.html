Return to Strength

4 sessions a week. Compound lifts at the gym, conditioning at home. Includes an optional onramp for Nikki.

The Setup

44, 195 lbs, 6'0". Four months off. Newborn at home. Commitment level: 9/10.

We're running strength and conditioning concurrently. Not strength first, then cardio. Both, every week, with conditioning getting the early bias because your VO2 max is the fastest thing to recover.

Target: 3 gym days + 1 home day. You said you can do 2 gym days. That's the floor. Push for 3 when you can. The fallback is below if a week only allows 2.

You don't need to earn the right to train hard. You just need to start smart.

The Program

Big compound lifts, low volume to start, conditioning finisher every session. Weeks 1-2 should feel easy. That's by design.

How to Pick Your Weights

Weeks 1-2: Pick a weight you could do for 10 reps. Do sets of 5. Finish every set thinking "I had 3-4 more." RPE 6-7.

Weeks 3-4: Add 5-10% to your working weights. RPE 7-8.

Week 5: Cut volume in half, keep weights moderate. Deload, then start Mesocycle 2.

Postpartum First

Clear with her doctor. Once cleared, start with home conditioning and bodyweight movements for 1-2 weeks before adding external load. Core and pelvic floor come first: dead bugs, bird dogs, diaphragmatic breathing before planks and squats. If anything feels off, scale back. A pelvic floor PT is worth the visit.

The Rules

RPE governs everything. If the weight feels heavy, it is heavy. Drop 10% and keep moving. Ego lifting is how you get hurt at 44.
Never miss two in a row. Miss one session, that's life with a newborn. Miss two, you're building a habit of not showing up.
Sleep beats everything. If the baby had you up all night, swap to a conditioning day. Don't skip.
30 minutes is a full session. Get in, hit the compounds, get out. Consistency beats duration.
Track your weights. Write down what you lifted. Next session, try to beat it. That's the whole system.

Addendum: The Thinking Behind the Plan

You don't need to read any of this to follow the program. This is for the curious.

To drive meaningful strength adaptations, you need roughly 6-10 hard sets per muscle group per week (Minimum Effective Volume through Maximum Adaptive Volume). Cramming that into two sessions means either long, grinding workouts or leaving sets on the table. Three sessions lets you spread the volume, recover between sessions, and actually progress week to week.

At two gym days, you're capped at about 60-70% of the weekly stimulus you could get from three. Over a month, that gap compounds. Israetel's volume research and Nippard's practical application both point to the same conclusion: frequency matters more than session duration for returning lifters.

Prilepin's table was originally built for Olympic lifters, but the principles hold for compound strength work. There's an optimal number of reps at each intensity that drives adaptation without burying you.

IntensityReps / SetTotal RepsYour Weeks
65-75%4-618-24Weeks 1-2 (reacquisition)
70-80%3-512-20Weeks 3-4 (loading)
50-60%3-510-15Week 5 (deload)

You don't have current maxes, and we're not testing them in week one. The "pick a weight you could do for 10, do sets of 5" heuristic lands you at roughly 65-70%. The stimulus for a detrained lifter comes from novelty, not from grinding.

MEV is Minimum Effective Volume: the least you can do and still grow. MAV is Maximum Adaptive Volume: the sweet spot. You start at MEV and build toward MAV over the mesocycle.

Volume increases by adding 1-2 sets per muscle group every 1-2 weeks. If recovery drops (sleep wrecked, soreness 3+ days, performance dips), hold volume or pull back.

April: Weeks 1-5 (You Are Here)

Reacquisition phase. Moderate weights, build conditioning base. RPE 6-8. Focus on showing up. End with a deload week.

May: Mesocycle 2

Push toward MAV. Add a 4th exercise to gym days. RPE 8-9 on top sets. Conditioning intervals shift to 45s on / 60s off. Body comp starts shifting.

June: Specialization

Bias toward weak points. If conditioning is solid, shift more volume to strength. If you're still gassing out, keep the conditioning bias.

At 195 lbs, 20 lb DBs are ~10% of your bodyweight. Not enough for progressive overload.

Priority upgrades, in order:

1. Adjustable Dumbbells (50-70 lb)

Single highest-impact purchase. Unlocks goblet squats, DB bench, rows, RDLs, lunges at real load. PowerBlocks or Bowflex SelectTechs, $300-400.

2. Pull-up Bar ($25-35)

Doorframe mount. Chin-ups, pull-ups, hanging leg raises. High stimulus, zero footprint.

3. Kettlebell (35-53 lb, $50-80)

Swings, Turkish get-ups, single-arm work your 15 lb bells can't touch.

4. Barbell + Squat Stand ($400-600)

End game. Eliminates the gym entirely. Get the DBs first, see if consistency holds, then invest.

With adjustable DBs up to 50-70 lbs, you can run a full strength program from home. Not a watered-down version. An actual program with progressive overload.
