Back & Leg Recovery Notes

Given the September car accident and the back pain / left leg numbness, here's how we adjust the program to keep building strength while protecting your spine.

Squat Adjustments

Wear a belt for every working set. This isn't a crutch. It increases intra-abdominal pressure and stabilizes your spine under load. Get a basic leather lifting belt if you don't have one.

Use a box or bench. Box squats control your depth and teach you to sit back, which takes pressure off your lower back. Sit back to the box, pause one second, then drive up. Same weight, same progression.

If numbness flares up mid-set: stop, drop weight 20%, try again. If it persists, switch to leg press for that session. Don't push through nerve symptoms.

Deadlift Adjustments

Swap conventional deadlift for dumbbell RDLs. Heavy dumbbells, 3x8. Hits your entire posterior chain without the spinal compression of a barbell off the floor. Same progression: add 5 lbs when all 3 sets feel controlled.

When your back is feeling strong: you can work back to conventional deadlifts. Start light (50% of what you'd normally pull) and build from there. No rush.

Spinal Stability Warm-Up (Every Session)

Add these before your regular warm-up. The McGill Big 3: gold standard for back rehab.

1 min Bird Dogs (10 each side, 5 sec hold)
1 min McGill Curl-Ups (10 reps, hands under lower back)
1 min Side Planks (20 sec each side)

Important

See a physical therapist if you haven't already. Numbness in the leg after an accident usually means a nerve is getting compressed (disc herniation, piriformis, etc.). Getting imaging and a PT evaluation before loading heavier is worth it. The program will still be here. Your spine needs to be right first.

Getting Lean and Muscular

You don't need to bulk and cut. Eat enough protein (150g+ a day) and keep lifting heavier. Your body recomps naturally when the training signal is strong and protein is high. Especially if you're not super lean yet, you can build muscle and lose fat at the same time.

Technique Tips

Keep 1-2 reps in reserve. Finish each set thinking "I could have done 1-2 more." Going to failure every set burns you out. Save it for the last set if you want to push.

Control the lowering phase. Take 2-3 seconds on the way down. Muscle responds more to the stretch under load. Don't bounce out of the bottom.

Want more size? Add one set of 8-12 reps at lighter weight after your 3x5 on bench and squat. That extra volume is where hypertrophy lives.

Warm-Up & Cooldown

5 minutes each, every session. Non-negotiable.

Before You Lift

2 min Jump rope or jog in place
30s Arm circles each direction
1 min 10 bodyweight squats
1 min 10 hip hinges
1 min Warm-up set at 50% weight

After You Lift

90s Couch stretch (each leg)
60s Doorway chest stretch
60s Hang from bar or doorframe
30s 5 deep, slow breaths

For your wife: Same exact program. Same lifts, same progression, same warm-up and cooldown. Start lighter, add 5 lbs per session. Compound lifts work the same for everyone. She'll build functional strength and lean out. There is no such thing as a separate "women's program."

The Rules

Never miss two in a row. Miss one, that's life. Miss two, you're building a habit of not showing up.
150g protein a day. Chicken, beef, eggs, shakes. This is the one nutrition thing that matters.
Add 5 lbs every session. Small, consistent progression. Light to heavy without ever feeling overwhelmed.
Sleep > supplements. 7+ hours. Nothing you can buy will outwork bad sleep.
Train together. You and your wife lifting is the accountability that works. Your kid sees parents who take care of themselves.

Nutrition: Keep It Simple

You don't need a meal plan. You need one rule: 150g+ protein a day. That's the single thing that drives muscle growth and body recomp. Everything else is secondary.

The easiest way to hit it: front-load breakfast with 30-40g protein. That puts you on pace for the day without thinking about it.

The Math

Breakfast: 30-40g. Lunch: 40-50g. Dinner: 40-50g. Snack or shake: 20-30g. That's 150g+ without counting a single calorie. If you're short, add a protein shake after your workout.
