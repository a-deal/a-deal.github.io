01

The Structural Force

The US regulatory-commercial system cannot produce definitive answers about non-patentable therapeutic compounds. Not approval. Not rejection. Silence. This is not a market gap. It is a systemic failure to generate information.

This isn't to say nobody has tried. Thymosin Beta-4 has had 17 clinical trials. RegeneRx's Phase 3 (RGN-259 eye drops) just missed its primary endpoint. Ipamorelin was tested by Helsinn in Phase 2 and failed on efficacy. But these are individual efforts on individual compounds. For most non-GLP-1 peptides, the system produces nothing. Three forces sustain the void:

Patent economics

Drug development costs $2.6B per approved compound. Patent exclusivity is the ROI mechanism. Peptides the body naturally produces (BPC-157, GLP-1, TB-500) cannot be patented in their native form. No patent = no ROI = no investment. Semaglutide required just one amino acid swap + a fatty acid chain to become patentable. That minimal modification made Novo Nordisk briefly Europe's most valuable company.

Regulatory enforcement tightening

FDA removed BPC-157 and TB-500 from the 503B bulk compounding list in 2024. The semaglutide compounding crackdown was the catalyst. "Research chemical" labeling does not create legal protection when products are packaged in dosage forms and sold to individuals. The gray market is larger now because legal channels got shut down.

Evidence vacuum

BPC-157 has only 2 registered clinical trials (one cancelled Phase 1 that never published results, one Phase 2 recruiting). 80% of the 212 published papers come from a single Croatian research group (Sikiric/Seiwerth). No identified receptor. No well-characterized mechanism of action. This is not "GLP-1 in the 1990s." GLP-1 had 2,158 papers from dozens of independent groups and a cloned receptor before pharma modified it. BPC-157 is far earlier and far less understood.

But the question isn't whether BPC-157 works. It's whether the system can produce a definitive answer either way. Right now, it can't. Millions of Americans make decisions in that void.

Silence, Not Rejection

The system doesn't produce approval or rejection for most non-GLP-1 peptides. It produces silence. A public evidence infrastructure could produce clarity. Maybe the answer for BPC-157 is "worth developing." Maybe it's "doesn't work." Either answer is better than what we have now, which is millions of people making decisions based on Reddit threads and one lab's publications. Even a definitive "no" is a public health contribution.

Signal: Someone Is Betting

Hudson Biotech has Phase 2 trials recruiting for BPC-157 (hamstring repair), TB-500 (cardiovascular biomarkers), and GHK-Cu (wound healing). This is the first entity running clinical development on multiple gray market peptides simultaneously. It validates that at least one institutional actor sees the therapeutic potential. But one company running three trials is not a system producing answers at the scale the market demands.

02

The Problem (Four Problems, Not One)

Start from the user. A person considering or currently taking peptides faces four unsolved problems:

Problem 1: "What am I actually injecting?"

Supply chain provenance. Gray market peptides from Chinese synthesis labs. Inconsistent dosing, unknown purity, no chain of custody.

  • Dosage accuracy is the crisis, not purity. Finnrick (6,195 samples, 185 vendors) found BPC-157 dosage deviates up to +/-80% from labeled amounts. A vial labeled "10mg" might contain 2-18mg. Purity is generally fine (>98%). You just don't know how much you're injecting.
  • 57% of BPC-157 vendors score "Poor" or worse. Only 32% of all vendors earn A or B grades.
  • 8% of samples show bacterial endotoxin contamination (MIT Technology Review, BBC, The Guardian reported this finding).
  • Finnrick tests purity but doesn't connect testing to tracking or outcomes. Nobody tells you: "This vendor scored 3/10 across 47 samples. Here are the outcomes from users who bought from them."

Problem 2: "What's my risk?"

Pre-protocol risk assessment. Before taking BPC-157: what are the known risks? What's your personal risk based on your health data?

  • BPC-157 promotes angiogenesis. If you have family cancer history or undetected lesions, that's a specific, quantifiable risk factor.
  • The biohacker community says "the body makes it, so it's safe." The body also makes cortisol, adrenaline, and insulin. Exogenous administration disrupts feedback loops. Exogenous testosterone is bioidentical but still suppresses natural production.
  • No tool connects your health record to peptide risk profiles. Zero.

Problem 3: "Is it working?"

Outcome monitoring. During and after a protocol: are biomarkers moving? Is wearable data showing improvement? Is self-reported outcome tracking against the protocol timeline?

  • Did the BPC-157 heal the tendon, or did 8 weeks of rest heal it? Attribution requires structured before/during/after comparison.
  • Emerging apps (PeptPro, PepMetrics) attempt this but neither is launched at scale.

Problem 4: "What works at scale?"

Aggregate intelligence. Across thousands of users: what protocols produce the best outcomes? What doses? What stacking? What vendors have the best quality track record?

  • This is the dataset that doesn't exist anywhere. Not in pharma (no funding). Not in academia (no structured data). Not in the biohacker community (anecdotal only).
What Already Exists (And Why It's Not Enough)

Expert-level peptide guidance exists. Platforms like peptide.courses offer curated protocols drawn from 65K+ academic papers, 5K+ evidence-based and anecdotal protocols, and referral networks of 125+ practitioners globally. Practitioners advise on terrain optimization (lifestyle, nutrition, sleep) before peptide use, personalized protocols for sensitive populations, and dosing adjustments. This is real, valuable expertise.

But it's consultative, not scalable. It serves people who can afford a personal peptide advisor. It doesn't serve the millions making decisions on Reddit threads. The knowledge is locked in expert relationships and literature reviews, not in structured, queryable, population-scale outcome data. The gap isn't "nobody knows anything about peptides." The gap is that what's known doesn't scale.

The Real Gap

The market has 15-20 apps that let you log injections and a handful of expert-driven consultative platforms. Zero that connect supply chain quality to protocols to outcomes to aggregate intelligence at scale. The logging layer is commoditized. The expert layer is valuable but doesn't scale. The data-driven intelligence layer is unbuilt.

The Product (Four Layers, Built Sequentially)

Layer 1 // Wedge
Supply Chain Intelligence

Ship first. Vendor quality database.

Layer 2
Risk + Protocol Tracking

Health record substrate. Structured logging.

Layer 3
Outcome Monitoring

Before/during/after biomarker comparison.

Layer 4 // Moat
Aggregate Intelligence

Community-sourced. Statistical rigor. The dataset.

Layer 1: Supply Chain Intelligence (ship first)

  • Vendor quality database built on third-party testing data (Finnrick partnership or own testing).
  • "Before you buy from this vendor, here's their purity track record across N samples."
  • Batch COA verification. QR-code lookup.
  • This is a content/data product. No app required. Web resource. Launchable in 30 days.
  • Revenue: free tier drives traffic, premium vendor reports, B2B licensing to clinics.

Layer 2: Risk Assessment + Protocol Tracking

  • Import your health record (labs, wearable data, family history).
  • Get a pre-protocol risk assessment: "Given your health profile, here are the specific risk factors for BPC-157."
  • Structured protocol logging: peptide, dose, frequency, injection site, cycle length, stacking, source vendor (linked to Layer 1 quality data).
  • This is where the Baseline architecture applies: parsing, normalization, health record as substrate.

Layer 3: Outcome Monitoring

  • Before/during/after biomarker comparison against your own baseline.
  • Wearable data correlation (HRV, recovery, sleep) mapped to protocol timeline.
  • Self-reported outcomes (pain, mobility, energy, cognition) structured and timestamped.
  • The "is it working?" answer, personalized to your data.

Layer 4: Aggregate Intelligence (requires scale)

  • Community-sourced protocol data across 500+ users minimum.
  • Statistical attribution: propensity score matching, interrupted time series, Bayesian methods.
  • "217 users ran BPC-157 at 250mcg 2x/day for rotator cuff. Median recovery improvement: X%. Users who used Vendor A (97% avg purity) saw Y% better outcomes than Vendor B (82% avg purity)."
  • This is the dataset. This is the moat. This is what pharma companies would acquire.
Technical Discipline

Use AI for data structuring (parsing, normalization). Use proper statistics for attribution. The attribution engine requires a biostatistician, not an LLM. Don't let the LLM do both.

05

The GLP-1 Precedent

Why semaglutide matters as a case study.

Timeline

Year Event Significance
1983 GLP-1 discovered Natural peptide, 2-minute half-life. Completely impractical as a drug.
2005 Exenatide (Byetta) approved First GLP-1 receptor agonist. Derived from Gila monster venom.
2010 Liraglutide (Victoza) approved 97% homology to human GLP-1. Half-life: ~13 hours.
2017 Semaglutide (Ozempic) approved for T2D Half-life: ~7 days (weekly injection).
2021 Semaglutide (Wegovy) approved for obesity Same molecule, new indication. Massive market expansion.
2026 GLP-1 market exceeds $50B 30M+ Americans on GLP-1s.

The modification strategy

Natural GLP-1 has a 2-minute half-life. Completely impractical as a drug. Semaglutide: Aib substitution at position 8 (DPP-4 resistance) + C18 fatty diacid chain at position 26. Two changes. $50B market.

What this tells us about other peptides

  • Natural peptides have terrible pharmacokinetic profiles. Modification is required for practical therapeutics.
  • The modification is what creates the patent. The patent is what funds the trials.
  • BPC-157 in its native form will never get FDA approval. Not because it doesn't work. Because nobody will spend $1-2B on trials for an unpatentable molecule.
  • Real-world outcome data at scale could do what trials can't: create an evidence base that informs regulatory frameworks, clinical practice, and eventually pharma investment in modified analogs.
The Overton Window

30M+ Americans now inject a peptide weekly. The Overton window is open. The question is whether the rest of the peptide market follows GLP-1 into rigor or stays in the gray market indefinitely.

The ARPA-H Angle (Why This Is Infrastructure)

The peptide data gap is not a consumer app problem. It is a health infrastructure gap with national security dimensions.

Dimension 1
Strategic Dependency

US peptide supply chain runs through Chinese synthesis labs. No domestic pharmaceutical-grade peptide manufacturing at scale for non-GLP-1 compounds. Same supply chain vulnerability pattern as semiconductors and rare earth minerals.

Dimension 2
Innovation Blind Spot

The patent system creates a structural blind spot for non-patentable therapeutics. An entire class of compounds with therapeutic potential is invisible to the pharmaceutical R&D engine.

Dimension 3
Evidence Vacuum

Millions of Americans are running an uncontrolled experiment on themselves. The outcomes are not being captured. This is both a public health risk and a wasted data opportunity.

Dimension 4
Regulatory Gap

FDA's framework handles patentable drugs (approval pathway) and dietary supplements (DSHEA). Non-patentable therapeutic peptides fit neither category. No framework exists.

Clarity, Not Advocacy

The goal of evidence infrastructure is not to validate peptides. It is to produce definitive answers. Some compounds may prove therapeutically relevant, identifying candidates worth developing patentable analogs of (the semaglutide path). Others may prove ineffective, giving the millions of current users information they don't have. Either outcome is better than silence. ARPA-H exists to fund research the market won't. The peptide evidence gap is a textbook case: no commercial path to the data, population-scale experiment already underway, and the system producing neither approval nor rejection.

Risks + Kill Criteria

Risk matrix

Critical
Adverse Event Association

If a user experiences a serious adverse event while using the platform, reputational and legal risk is real even if you didn't recommend the protocol. Requires legal structure and medical advisory board before launch.

Critical
Supply Chain Data Quality

If gray market peptides have inconsistent purity, outcome data based on those protocols is noise, not signal. Layer 1 (supply chain verification) must precede Layer 3 (outcome monitoring) or the dataset is worthless.

Structural
Regulatory Trajectory (In Flux)

The regulatory picture is moving in two directions simultaneously. FDA removed BPC-157 and TB-500 from the 503B compounding list in 2024. On February 27, 2026, HHS Secretary Kennedy announced on Joe Rogan (#2461) that ~14 of 19 Category 2 peptides would move back to Category 1, re-legalizing compounding access. No formal FDA rule has changed yet, but the direction is clear. Meanwhile, Dr. Eric Topol (Scripps Research) called the original ban "appropriate" and peptide promotion "grifter" territory, demanding "show me the data." If access expands without evidence infrastructure, we're reopening the door without turning on the lights. The opportunity shifts from "evidence for a gray market" to "evidence for a newly re-legalized market." The second is larger, cleaner, and more fundable.

Market
PeptPro First Mover

PeptPro (EU-based) is attempting the full stack: marketplace + tracking + biomarker analysis + wearable integration. If they execute and scale, the intelligence gap may close before you enter. Their conflict of interest (selling peptides + rating outcomes) is a structural weakness but doesn't guarantee failure.

Statistical
Data Density Requirements

Need n=200+ per peptide-condition pair to distinguish a 20% effect from noise. At 5 peptides x 5 conditions = 25 cohorts x 200 users = 5,000 users minimum for meaningful aggregate intelligence. May take 12-18 months.

Engineering
Attribution Complexity

"Did BPC-157 heal the tendon or did 8 weeks of rest?" requires controlled comparison methodology, not pattern matching. Requires a biostatistician. Cannot be approximated by an LLM.

Kill criteria

Walk Away If

1. If you can't get 500 users tracking protocols within 6 months, the dataset thesis doesn't reach critical mass. Pivot to supply chain intelligence only (Layer 1 as standalone business).

2. If users won't share anonymized protocol data, you have individual tracking (commodity) not community intelligence (moat). Test willingness to share before building aggregation.

3. If you can't find a biostatistician co-founder or advisor within 60 days, don't build the attribution engine. Ship Layers 1-3 only and be explicit that you don't make outcome claims.

4. If Finnrick or PeptPro ships aggregate outcome intelligence before you reach scale, evaluate whether to compete or partner. Don't fight a data network effect from behind.

5. If a major adverse event in the peptide community triggers FDA enforcement that shrinks the market by 50%+, the consumer thesis contracts but the B2B/research thesis strengthens (more need for evidence, not less).
