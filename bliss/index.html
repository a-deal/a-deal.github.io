01

The Precedent

GLP-1 receptor agonists are a $45B+ market. Semaglutide produces 15-20% body weight loss. But this success required 80 years of publicly funded foundational science before pharma invested a dollar.

Three things had to be true before any company spent $2.6B: a known receptor (GLP-1R, identified 1986), a known mechanism (insulin secretion, appetite suppression), and decades of independent validation (2,158 papers from dozens of labs). Pharma entered at the last mile. Everything before that was public science.

The Template

GLP-1 is not an isolated success. It is a template: foundational science (public) → mechanism understood → rational modification (pharma) → patent → blockbuster drug. The question is what other compounds are stuck at an earlier stage, not because the science is impossible, but because the funding structure doesn't reach them.

03

Why the Evidence Doesn't Exist

Patent law (35 U.S.C. § 101) prohibits patenting products of nature. Most therapeutic peptides are naturally occurring amino acid sequences. No patent means no ROI on clinical trials. No ROI means no investment. No investment means no evidence. The system produces silence.

Question 1
What's in them?

Finnrick tests 6,195 samples: +/-80% dosage variance, 8% endotoxin contamination. 57% of BPC-157 vendors score Poor or worse. Partially answered by one private company.

Question 2
Do they work?

BPC-157: no identified receptor, no known mechanism, 80% of papers from one Croatian lab, zero Phase 1 trials. For most non-GLP-1 peptides: we genuinely don't know.

Question 3
For whom, toward what?

Zero structured outcome tracking. Millions of users generating data that evaporates. A population-scale uncontrolled experiment with no observation.

The instinct is to fund foundational science first: identify receptors, map mechanisms, then decide. That takes 5-10 years per compound, and there are dozens of compounds. Meanwhile, millions of people are injecting them today. Epidemiology existed before molecular biology. John Snow mapped cholera without knowing what a bacterium was. Observe first. Investigate what the observations justify.

But observation at scale raises an immediate question: observe which compounds first? The list of non-patentable peptides in active use is long. A credible program cannot pick arbitrarily. It needs a framework.

04

The Selection Framework

Before any cohort runs, candidate compounds are scored on three axes. The framework itself is the first public deliverable. The top-ranked compound becomes the first cohort. If the framework's weights are wrong, the argument is about the weights, not the compound.

The three axes

Axis 1 // Demand
Who is already using it?

Monthly search volume. Prescription and gray-market use estimates. Paper velocity (papers per year, year-over-year growth). Cultural gravity (podcast mentions, Reddit density). The question ARPA-H cares about: is this a population-scale phenomenon or a niche?

Axis 2 // Benefit
Could evidence move the needle?

Mechanistic plausibility: known receptor, known pathway. Existing human data: any trials, any registry. Claimed indications: narrow vs. broad, measurable vs. subjective. Comparable approved analogs that inform priors. Weaker mechanism = stronger need for observational data, but harder to interpret a null result.

Axis 3 // Risk
How urgent is the evidence?

Known adverse events. Theoretical risks (angiogenesis, receptor desensitization, HPA suppression). Supply chain contamination failure rates. Vulnerable populations using it. Higher risk means the evidence gap is more urgent, not less. Compounds with known concerns move up the list.

Candidate scoring (preliminary)

Each compound is scored 1-5 on each axis. Scores below are preliminary and are intended to be refined with expert panel review (ARPA-H program staff, academic advisors, practitioner input) before Phase 1 deliverables are finalized.

Reading the Matrix

BPC-157 emerges as the leading Phase 2 candidate on preliminary scoring: highest demand signal, highest risk signal (angiogenesis concern, worst supply chain), moderate benefit potential. The combination of "millions of users" and "zero evidence and real theoretical risk" is exactly the profile ARPA-H is mandated to address.

CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin is the secondary candidate: known receptor and mechanism make the benefit axis easier to evaluate, but lower risk means the urgency argument is weaker.

The scoring is not the thesis. The framework is the thesis. Phase 1 refines both the scoring criteria and the weights before any cohort begins.

Why a framework beats a pick

  • Defensibility. "Why this compound?" has a reproducible answer: because the framework ranked it first. The argument moves from personal conviction to methodology.
  • Generalizability. Once the framework is public, it applies to future compounds, future peptide classes, and adjacent non-patentable therapeutic categories.
  • Expert alignment. The framework gives ARPA-H program staff, academic advisors, and practitioner networks a shared artifact to debate before any money is spent on a cohort.
  • Optionality. If the top-ranked compound proves infeasible for cohort design (IRB blockers, sourcing issues, low enrollment), the second-ranked compound is already defended.
05

The Program

The program is structured in two phases with an exercisable option between them. Phase 1 delivers the selection framework, refined scoring, and full cohort design. Phase 2 is the prospective observational cohort itself. ARPA-H exercises Phase 2 based on Phase 1 deliverables, not on a blind commitment upfront.

Phase 1 // Months 0-6
Framework + Cohort Design
$500K  ·  6 months  ·  Independently deliverable
  • Refined selection framework with expert panel review (ARPA-H staff, academic advisors, practitioners)
  • Scoring of 10-15 candidate compounds with documented rationale
  • Full Phase 2 cohort design: enrollment criteria, biomarker panels, comparison group, IRB submission, pre-registered hypotheses
  • Supply chain verification partnership (Finnrick or equivalent) finalized
  • Biostatistician onboarded; attribution methodology published
  • Public methodology paper on observational evidence generation for non-patentable therapeutics
Phase 2 // Months 7-18 (optional)
Prospective Cohort
$2.5M  ·  12 months  ·  Exercisable based on Phase 1
  • 500-user enrollment on top-ranked compound from Phase 1 framework
  • Standardized biomarker panels at months 0, 6, and 12
  • Supply chain verification for all enrolled user sources
  • Matched comparison group (non-users from similar demographic profile)
  • Interim analysis at month 6 with pre-specified decision thresholds
  • Peer-reviewable outcome publication by month 18
Why Phased

Phase 1 is independently valuable. Even if Phase 2 never runs, ARPA-H gets a public methodology for compound prioritization and cohort design that generalizes across non-patentable therapeutic categories.

Phase 2 is de-risked by Phase 1. No blind commitment to a compound or cohort design. The exercise decision is made with the framework, the scoring, the IRB path, and the biostatistical plan already in hand.

Structured like a DARPA program. Exercisable options reduce funder commitment and create natural checkpoints. Phase 1 deliverables are the gate for Phase 2 capital.

Kill criteria (structural options)

A serious program names the conditions under which it stops. The program includes an explicit kill criterion, to be selected jointly with ARPA-H program staff during Phase 1 contracting. Four structural options are on the table:

Option Trigger Response Tradeoff
A // Soft Pivot Enrollment < 250 at month 6 Pivot to supply chain intelligence as standalone deliverable Lowest risk to ARPA-H. Weakest credibility signal (“we’ll find something to publish”).
B // Hard Stop Enrollment < 250 at month 6 Program closes. Unspent funds returned. Strongest credibility signal. Loses framework value if Phase 2 stalls.
C // Signal-Based Interim analysis shows no biomarker shift above pre-specified noise threshold Cohort discontinues. Remaining budget redirects to second-ranked compound. Most scientifically rigorous. Requires pre-specified analysis plan from biostatistician during Phase 1.
D // Two-Phase Kill Phase 1 deliverables unaccepted, or Phase 2 enrollment < 250 at month 6 Phase 2 not exercised, or Phase 2 closes with funds returned Cleanest ARPA-H structure. Two independent go/no-go gates. Natural off-ramp between phases.
Recommendation

Option D (Two-Phase Kill) is structurally aligned with how ARPA and DARPA programs typically operate. It gives the funder two independent exit points, independently delivers value at Phase 1, and creates explicit quality gates before Phase 2 capital deploys. Options B and C are substitutes or add-ons inside Phase 2, not alternatives to the phased structure itself.

Final selection happens during Phase 1 contracting with ARPA-H program staff.

What each outcome produces

Outcome What It Enables
Signal detected Justifies government funding for foundational science (receptor identification, mechanism mapping) on the specific compound. Tells NIH and ARPA-H where to invest next.
No signal Saves billions in research chasing compounds that don't work. Gives consumers data instead of anecdote. Equally valuable as a public health contribution.
Safety signal Informs FDA regulatory action with evidence. ~14 compounds are being re-legalized in 2026 without it. A safety finding is the fastest path to evidence-based rulemaking.
Framework-only (Phase 1 completes, Phase 2 not exercised) Public methodology for observational evidence generation across any non-patentable therapeutic category. Reusable by future researchers, future funders, and adjacent compound classes.

Why this, why now

  • The cohort already exists. 10 million monthly searches. Millions of active users. They're looking for answers. An evidence program with a credible framework has a built-in enrollment population.
  • The timing is now. Regulatory whiplash (FDA ban in 2024, RFK re-legalization in 2026) means compounds are re-entering the market without evidence to guide prescribing, dosing, or safety. The framework and cohort need to exist before the next wave of adoption, not after it.
  • The cost is asymmetric. $500K for Phase 1 framework. $3M total if Phase 2 exercises. Compared to $2.6B and 10+ years for a single compound through traditional FDA trials. This is the cheapest defensible way to answer the question "do any of these work?"
  • No one else is building this. 8-10 peptide apps launched in the last year. All are logging tools. None have a compound selection framework. None connect supply chain to outcomes. None are designed to produce publishable evidence. The program is the missing piece of infrastructure between a $100B consumer market and the scientific establishment.
The Ask

An ARPA-H partnership to fund Phase 1 ($500K, 6 months) to produce a public selection framework and full Phase 2 cohort design. Phase 2 ($2.5M, 12 months) exercisable based on Phase 1 deliverables.

Total program, if fully exercised: $3M over 18 months. First systematic outcome data on any non-patentable therapeutic peptide. A public methodology that generalizes to other non-patentable compound classes. Two independent go/no-go gates that return unspent funds at either phase.
