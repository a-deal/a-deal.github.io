01

The Problem

GLP-1 proved peptides work

Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) is a modified version of a peptide the human body already produces. One amino acid substitution plus a fatty acid chain made it patentable, injectable weekly instead of daily, and commercially viable. Novo Nordisk briefly became Europe's most valuable company on this single compound. The GLP-1 market crossed $50B in 2026.

That success opened the Overton window for peptide therapeutics broadly. Dozens of other peptides now circulate in the health optimization community: BPC-157 for tissue repair, TB-500 for inflammation, CJC-1295 for growth hormone secretion, GHK-Cu for skin and wound healing, Semax for cognitive function. Many show real promise in preclinical research.

The data vacuum

The problem is simple. For non-GLP-1 peptides, there is zero rigorous human outcome data at scale. The evidence base is rodent studies, cell cultures, Soviet-era research papers, and biohacker anecdotes on Reddit.

  • No systematic tracking of what peptide, what dose, what duration, what biomarker changes, what outcomes.
  • No correlation data linking specific protocols to measurable health improvements in humans.
  • No quality-controlled supply chain ensuring that the compound being tracked is actually what it claims to be at the stated purity.
  • No statistical infrastructure to distinguish real effects from placebo, regression to the mean, or confounding variables.

People are already using these compounds. Millions of them. They're just doing it with no data infrastructure, no outcome tracking, and no way to learn from each other's experience at scale.

02

The Structural Force

Why pharma won't build this

Drug development costs $2.6B per approved compound on average. The ROI equation requires patent exclusivity to recoup that investment over a 12-15 year window. You cannot patent what the body already makes.

BPC-157 is a fragment of a protein found in human gastric juice. GLP-1 is produced by L-cells in the gut after eating. TB-500 is a synthetic fragment of thymosin beta-4, which exists in every human cell. The body already manufactures these compounds.

Semaglutide required a specific molecular modification (one amino acid swap plus a fatty acid chain attachment) to become patentable. Without that kind of novel chemical entity, there is no IP moat, and without an IP moat, no pharma company will spend $2.6B on a Phase III trial.

Structural Barrier
Patent economics

Natural peptides can't be patented. No patent means no exclusivity period. No exclusivity means no way to recoup $2.6B in development costs. The incentive structure is broken for these compounds.

Regulatory Shift
FDA crackdown on compounding

FDA's 2023-2024 enforcement actions against compounding pharmacies pushed peptide supply further into the gray market. Quality control is getting worse, not better. Users are sourcing from unregulated overseas labs.

The supply chain reality

The FDA crackdown on compounding pharmacies (particularly around semaglutide compounding) had cascading effects across the broader peptide market. Pharmacies that previously compounded BPC-157, TB-500, and other research peptides either shut down or stopped carrying them. Users migrated to gray market sources, primarily Chinese peptide synthesis labs selling direct to consumers.

This created a quality control crisis. Purity claims are unverified. Dosing is inconsistent. Third-party testing is rare. The compounds people are tracking outcomes for may not be what they think they are.

Core Thesis

The structural opportunity isn't peptides themselves. It's the gap between pharmaceutical-grade compound supply and consumer demand that GLP-1 created. Whoever solves quality control and proper dosing protocols captures a market currently served by gray market Chinese labs.

The Dataset Thesis

The asset that doesn't exist

The first entity with 10,000+ tracked peptide protocols correlated to biomarker outcomes has something that doesn't exist anywhere. Not in any pharma company's clinical trial database. Not in any academic research institution. Not in any biohacker forum's self-reported threads.

The dataset is the business. Not the tracking tool, not the coaching layer, not the community. Those are distribution mechanisms for building the dataset. The dataset itself is the defensible asset.

The Lilly/Insilico parallel

Eli Lilly paid $2.75B for Insilico Medicine's AI drug discovery platform. The value wasn't in any single molecule. It was the dataset, the training data, and the predictive engine built on top of it. A peptide outcome dataset with 50,000+ tracked protocols is the same asset class: structured real-world evidence that no one else has, with compounding network effects.

The Flywheel

Every user who tracks a protocol adds to the intelligence. Every correlation discovered makes the next recommendation better. Every new biomarker panel connected increases the resolution of the dataset. That's the flywheel. It compounds.

04

Product Architecture

Three layers, built sequentially

Each layer depends on the previous one reaching critical mass. Ship in order. Don't skip ahead.

Layer 1 // Ship First
Protocol Tracking

Structured logging of peptide protocols. Dose, frequency, source, duration, stacking. No health claims, no outcome attribution. Pure data capture. This is the foundation. If nobody uses this, nothing else matters.

Layer 2 // Requires Layer 1 Data
Biomarker Correlation

Connect protocols to blood panels (Function Health, InsideTracker), wearable data (HRV, recovery, sleep), and self-reported outcomes. Show before/during/after comparisons. Descriptive statistics only. No causal claims.

Layer 3 // Requires Biostatistician
Outcome Attribution

Community-sourced protocol intelligence. "217 users ran BPC-157 at 250mcg 2x/day for rotator cuff. Median recovery improvement: 34%. 95% CI: 22-46%." Statistical rigor. Propensity score matching. Interrupted time series. Not LLM pattern matching.

Output Format
Protocol Reports

Individual: your protocol, your biomarkers, your trajectory. Community: what worked for people like you, with statistical confidence intervals. The individual report is the hook. The community intelligence is the retention.

Technical Discipline

Use AI for data structuring (parsing lab reports, normalizing dosage formats, extracting entities from unstructured text). Use proper statistics for attribution (propensity score matching, interrupted time series, Bayesian hierarchical models). Don't let the LLM do both. The temptation to let GPT-4 "find patterns" in outcome data is strong. Resist it. Statistical rigor is the entire value proposition.

05

The Monitoring Argument

Steelmanned

Justin Mares' argument: continuous glucose monitors, quarterly biomarker panels, annual Prenuvo scans, and wearable data mean we can take "more risk on" with experimental compounds because we'll catch problems early. The monitoring infrastructure of 2026 is qualitatively different from what existed even five years ago.

What IS true about this: modern monitoring tools create a feedback loop that didn't exist a decade ago. If you can track comprehensive biomarkers before, during, and after a peptide protocol, you have outcome data. Real data. Not anecdotes, not forum posts. Structured, timestamped, biomarker-correlated outcome data. That's not a safety argument. It's a data argument. And the data argument is legitimate.

Critiqued

Detection is not prevention. A Prenuvo MRI detects a tumor. It does not prevent BPC-157 from promoting angiogenesis in a pre-existing lesion you didn't know about. You monitor because the risk exists, not because monitoring eliminates it. The monitoring reduces the consequence of certain risks (catching problems earlier), but it does not reduce the probability of those risks occurring.

The "Natural" Fallacy

The "your body already makes it" frame is a marketing argument, not a safety argument. The body also makes cortisol, adrenaline, and insulin. Exogenous administration of any hormone or peptide disrupts endogenous feedback loops. Exogenous testosterone is bioidentical to what the testes produce, but it still suppresses the HPT axis and shuts down natural production. "Natural" tells you nothing about the safety profile of supraphysiological dosing.

The Real Opportunity

The monitoring infrastructure argument works not because it makes peptide use safe, but because it makes peptide use measurable. Measurable use generates data. Data at scale generates knowledge. Knowledge creates a defensible business. That's the thesis. Not "peptides are safe because we can monitor." Rather: "monitoring generates the dataset that nobody else is building."

07

Prerequisites (Non-Negotiable)

These are not nice-to-haves. They are the difference between building knowledge infrastructure and building Medvi 2.0 (a peptide telehealth marketplace that adds no lasting value).

Hire #1
Biostatistician

Co-founder or senior advisor. The attribution engine requires propensity score matching, Bayesian hierarchical models, interrupted time series analysis, and careful handling of confounders. This cannot be built by prompting an LLM. If you can't find this person within 60 days, don't build Layer 3.

Day One
Legal Counsel

Language matters. "Our data shows BPC-157 improves recovery" is an FDA-regulated health claim. "Here's what 217 users reported about their experience" is user-generated content. The line between them is thin, and getting it wrong is potentially terminal. Need counsel experienced in FDA, FTC, and DSHEA.

Credibility
Medical Advisor

Not a biohacker MD selling peptides on Instagram. Someone published in peer-reviewed journals, willing to put their professional reputation next to your product. This person exists in the longevity medicine space but needs to be vetted carefully. Their presence is your credibility signal to both users and potential B2B partners.

Data Quality
Supply Chain Quality Layer

If users track outcomes on garbage peptides with inconsistent dosing from unverified sources, your dataset is noise, not signal. Need batch testing, COA validation, or pharmaceutical-grade supplier partnership. Without this, every correlation in your data is suspect.

Non-Negotiable

If a single adverse event gets publicly linked to your platform, the regulatory and reputational risk may be terminal. It doesn't matter whether you recommended the protocol or not. The association is the liability. Build the advisory structure, the legal framework, and the disclaimers before launch. Not after.

08

Risks + Kill Criteria

Risk matrix

Critical
Adverse Event Linked to Platform

Even if you didn't recommend the protocol, even if the user sourced the peptide independently, a hospitalization or death linked to "that peptide tracking app" is front-page news. The liability surface is real and potentially company-ending.

Structural
Gray Market Supply Chain

Inconsistent dosing, unknown purity, mislabeled compounds. If the input data is garbage, the output correlations are garbage. Your dataset may be measuring noise, not signal. This is the hardest risk to mitigate without controlling the supply chain directly.

Regulatory
FDA Trajectory Unclear

Mares' thesis assumes "legalization is coming." Current FDA enforcement actions suggest otherwise. The 503A/503B compounding pathway remains under active restriction. Regulatory clarity is 3-5 years out, not imminent. Build for the current regime, not a hoped-for future one.

Statistical
Data Density Requirements

Need n=200+ per protocol to distinguish a 20% effect size from noise at 80% power. For rare peptides or unusual stacking combinations, reaching statistical significance may take 12-18 months. Users may churn before the dataset reaches actionable density.

Competitive
Incumbents Could Build Faster

Whoop (14M+ users), Function Health (growing biomarker database), or a pharma company with existing clinical trial infrastructure could build this faster with their existing user base and capital. Your advantage is focus and speed. Their advantage is distribution and trust.

Behavioral
Data Sharing Reluctance

Users may track protocols individually but refuse to share anonymized data with the community. If sharing rates are low, you have individual tracking (low value, commodity) instead of community intelligence (high value, defensible). The social proof and incentive design must be right from day one.

Kill criteria

Walk Away If

1. You can't get 500 users actively tracking protocols within 6 months of launch. Below this threshold, the dataset doesn't reach critical mass for any single peptide, and the flywheel never starts.

2. Users won't share anonymized data. If sharing rates are below 40%, you have individual tracking (commodity, low defensibility) not community intelligence (high value, compounding returns).

3. You can't find a biostatistician co-founder or senior advisor within 60 days. Without this person, don't build the attribution engine. You'll ship something that looks like science but isn't, and that's worse than shipping nothing.

4. Synthetic data tests show the attribution algorithm can't distinguish a 20% effect size at n=200. If the statistical methods don't work on clean synthetic data, they won't work on noisy real-world data. You need 10x more users before making any claims.

The earning window

Regulatory clarity for non-GLP-1 peptides is 3-5 years out. When regulation arrives, whoever has the outcome data becomes the reference. The pharma companies entering this space will need real-world evidence. The FDA will want safety data. The only entity that can provide either is the one that has been systematically tracking outcomes.

The window is now. Not because regulation is imminent, but because the dataset takes years to build to actionable density. Starting later means arriving after the regulatory framework has already been shaped by whoever started first.
