01

The Problem

Health data is everywhere and nowhere. Lab results arrive as email attachments. Wearable data lives in siloed apps. Doctor notes sit in patient portals behind five-step logins. Supplement lists live in random notes. Prescriptions are in pharmacy apps. Blood pressure readings are on scraps of paper.

Nobody has a complete, structured, queryable picture of your health. Not you. Not your doctor. Not any AI. The person most motivated to understand their health, the person actually paying for labs, wearables, and supplements, has the worst data infrastructure of anyone in the chain.

The problem is not storage. Google Drive can hold every lab PDF you will ever generate. The problem is that every tool meant to help you is flying blind. Your doctor sees a 15-minute snapshot. Your wearable sees sleep and steps. Your health coach sees whatever you remember to tell them. Claude sees whatever you paste into a chat window.

Why Google Drive + Claude doesn't work

  • PDFs are opaque to LLMs. A lab report PDF is a rendered image of a table. Claude can OCR it, but the extraction is lossy, inconsistent, and non-repeatable. Every conversation starts from scratch.
  • No temporal awareness. Dropping three lab reports into a folder gives you no trend lines, no deltas, no "your LDL went from 142 to 118 over six months." You get point-in-time snapshots with no connective tissue.
  • No semantic normalization. LabCorp calls it "Cholesterol, Total." Quest calls it "Total Cholesterol." Both map to LOINC 2093-3, but an LLM has no way to know that without a normalization layer. Cross-lab comparisons are unreliable.
  • No cross-source correlation. Your resting heart rate trend from your wearable, your fasting glucose from labs, and your sleep data from your watch all bear on metabolic health. No tool connects them into a single view.
The Fundamental Gap

Health-forward people are spending $500/yr on Function Health labs, $300/yr on Whoop, $100/yr on supplements, and $20/mo on ChatGPT. They are generating more health data than at any point in human history. And they still cannot answer the question: "How do I know if I'm healthy?"

The Thesis

Build the observation layer. Not coaching. Not intervention. Not action. Structured health data that makes everything downstream better. The health record that does not exist today.

Every health company picks an altitude and tries to own the full stack from there. We pick one altitude, nail it, and let everyone else build on top of it.

03

The Coverage Score

The lead differentiator. Nobody tells you what is missing.

Function Health tells you your LDL is high. Apple Health shows your steps. Whoop tells you your HRV. Nobody says: "You have strong blood work coverage but zero cardiovascular monitoring. Connecting a wearable would take you from 55% to 70%."

The Coverage Score does two things:

  • Coverage (0-100%). How much of the health picture do we actually have data for? Weighted by evidence strength. A lipid panel contributes more to coverage than a step count because the clinical evidence base is deeper.
  • Standing (~Xth percentile). Where do you fall relative to the population? Benchmarked against NHANES (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey), the gold standard for population health data in the United States.

Why weights, not counts

A lipid panel with five biomarkers tells you more about cardiovascular risk than connecting a wearable that tracks steps. Evidence strength matters. A study linking ApoB to all-cause mortality carries more weight than a correlation between daily step count and mood. The scoring system reflects this hierarchy.

Critical Framing

Average is not healthy. The average American is typical for a population where 96M are prediabetic, 47% have hypertension, and 42% are obese. "You're in the 50th percentile" is not reassuring. It means you look like the median member of a population with epidemic-level chronic disease. The Coverage Score benchmarks you against reality, not against an aspirational ideal.

04

Record + Loop

The Record

A structured, portable, user-owned health record. Labs, vitals, wearable data, and health history normalized into a single queryable index. Not a PDF folder. Not a patient portal. A living data structure that any AI can read via MCP (Model Context Protocol).

  • Labs: Parsed from PDFs, normalized to LOINC codes, stored with timestamps, reference ranges, and lab-specific metadata.
  • Vitals: Blood pressure, resting heart rate, weight, body composition. From devices, manual entry, or wearable sync.
  • Wearable data: Sleep, HRV, activity, recovery scores. Imported from Apple Health, Oura, Whoop, Garmin.
  • History: Medications, supplements, conditions, family history. Manual entry, structured and queryable.

The Loop

The post-score action sequence. You get your Coverage Score. It tells you what is missing. The Loop tells you what to do about it: "Track your blood pressure for two weeks. Order an ApoB test. Connect your wearable." Each action feeds data back into the Record. The Record gets richer. The Coverage Score updates. Coaching from any downstream tool gets more contextual. Value compounds.

Validate
Score works?

Users complete intake. Coverage Score is meaningful and actionable. Users understand what they have and what is missing.

Validate
Loop works?

Users take the recommended action. They order a test, connect a wearable, or log a measurement. Data flows back into the Record.

Validate
Record is deep?

After 30 days, does the average user have 5+ data sources? Is the Record meaningfully richer than day one?

Validate
Platform works?

Can a downstream tool (Claude, a coach, a doctor) query the Record via MCP and get useful, structured context?

The Interdependence

Neither works alone. Record without Loop is a filing cabinet. You built a beautiful schema and nobody puts data into it. Loop without Record is a coaching app with no data moat. You tell people to track their blood pressure but you have nowhere to put the readings. The two are co-dependent. Both must work for either to have value.

05

Architecture

Local-first. SQLite + MCP server. No server, no cloud initially. Your health data lives on your machine, queryable by any AI that speaks MCP. This is a deliberate architectural choice: privacy by default, zero infrastructure cost at launch, and alignment with the "your data, your control" thesis.

Phase Roadmap

Phase 1: Desktop
CLI + Local MCP

Lab PDF parsing (LabCorp, Quest, Function Health). Apple Health XML import. Manual entry for vitals, medications, supplements. Coverage scoring engine. Local MCP server exposing the Record to Claude Desktop, Cursor, or any MCP-compatible client.

Phase 2: Mobile
iOS App + Cloud MCP

Share Sheet capture for lab PDFs directly from email. Encrypted cloud endpoint for mobile-to-Record sync. iCloud sync for cross-device access. Push notifications for Loop actions (reminder to log BP, test results ready to parse).

Phase 3: Platform
Watch for Apple MCP

If Apple ships MCP support in iOS/macOS, the local architecture becomes native. If not, the cloud relay from Phase 2 serves as the bridge. Either way, the Record schema and MCP interface are stable. Third-party integrations: lab ordering, telehealth, pharmacy APIs.

Regulatory
MDDS Classification

Medical Device Data System. We display and structure data. We do not interpret, diagnose, or recommend treatment. FDA deregulated MDDS software as of 2022. Zero FDA regulatory burden. No 510(k). No clinical trials. This is intentional: the observation layer stays at observation.

Architectural Advantage

Local-first means zero cloud infrastructure cost at launch. A SQLite database and a Python MCP server. The entire product runs on the user's machine. This is not a limitation. It is a feature. Privacy-sensitive health data never leaves the device. The business model does not depend on storing user data in the cloud.

Competitive Landscape

Every competitor owns a slice of health data. None of them structure ALL of it, make it portable, make it AI-queryable, or tell you what is missing.

Platform
Apple Health

Best wearable data aggregation. But platform-locked (iOS only), no AI queryability, no lab integration, no coverage scoring. Apple Health is a data sink, not a data platform. You can put data in. You cannot get structured data out.

Abandoned
Google Health

Pivoted twice, abandoned. Google Fit is maintenance mode. Google Health API was shut down. Even with infinite resources, health data aggregation is hard enough that big tech companies walk away from it.

Testing Only
Function Health

$500/yr for 100+ biomarker tests. Excellent at generating data. Zero integration with wearables, no MCP, no external data sources, no portability. Your Function Health data lives in Function Health.

Doctor-Facing
Epic MyChart

The dominant EHR. But designed for clinical workflows, not patient empowerment. Data export is painful (FHIR is technically possible, practically unusable). The patient portal is a view into the doctor's system, not a patient-owned record.

Measurement
Whoop / Oura

Excellent at what they measure: HRV, sleep, recovery, strain. But they only see their own data. Whoop does not know your cholesterol. Oura does not know your blood pressure. Siloed measurement, no cross-source intelligence.

Generic AI
ChatGPT Health

Can answer health questions with generic context. But has no structured access to YOUR health data. Every conversation starts from zero. No persistence, no normalization, no temporal awareness.

The Moat: Parsing + Normalization

"Cholesterol, Total" (LabCorp) = "Total Cholesterol" (Quest) = "Cholesterol" (Function Health) = LOINC 2093-3. Done correctly across thousands of biomarkers, hundreds of lab formats, and dozens of naming conventions. This is tedious, unglamorous work that compounds with every format parsed. Every new lab format supported makes the Record more valuable. Every edge case handled makes the parsing engine more reliable. This is not a feature. It is the moat.

B2B Angle: Infrastructure

The parsing and normalization engine has standalone value. Health AI companies (coaching apps, telehealth platforms, clinical decision support tools) all need structured health data. Selling the engine as an API or SDK creates a second revenue stream independent of the consumer product.

Two Businesses

The consumer business (subscription, $50-100/yr) is a lifestyle business. It can sustain a small team indefinitely at 10K+ subscribers. The infrastructure business (parsing/normalization API sold to health AI companies) is a venture business. It scales with the number of companies building on health data. You can run both. The consumer product generates the parsing volume that makes the infrastructure product better.

The 1Password Comparison

1Password charged $50/yr. Bootstrapped for 14 years. Built a loyal, paying user base without VC funding. Eventually raised at a $6.8B valuation when the market was ready. The playbook: solve a real problem for a willing-to-pay audience, compound value over time, stay profitable, raise money only when it accelerates an already-working business.

Risks + Kill Criteria

Risk Matrix

Critical
Apple Ships Health + MCP

If Apple ships native MCP integration with Apple Health in iOS 27 or macOS 27, the local-first health record becomes a platform feature. The window closes. You become a feature, not a product. Probability: low-medium in 2026, rising in 2027-2028.

Structural
Parsing Accuracy Trust

Parsing accuracy below 98% destroys trust permanently. One wrong lab value, one mismatched unit, one swapped biomarker name. Health data has zero tolerance for errors. Users will not forgive "your cholesterol was displayed as 42 instead of 242."

Market
Willingness to Pay

The health-forward, data-literate segment may not pay for an invisible observation layer. They pay for tests (tangible). They pay for wearables (tangible). Will they pay for structured data infrastructure? The value is real but abstract.

Technical
iOS Sandbox Blocks MCP

iOS sandboxing may block local MCP server architecture on mobile. The mobile story depends on either a cloud relay (privacy trade-off) or Apple shipping native MCP support (dependency on Apple's roadmap).

Competitive
ChatGPT Health Gets Good

If ChatGPT or Claude ships persistent health memory with structured data support, generic AI becomes "good enough" for most users. The observation layer value proposition shrinks to power users who need clinical-grade normalization.

Operational
Lab Format Fragmentation

Thousands of labs, hundreds of PDF formats, constant format changes. The parsing engine is a treadmill. If the rate of format change exceeds the rate of parser development, coverage degrades over time instead of compounding.

Kill Criteria

When to Stop

1. If Apple ships Health + MCP integration, evaluate pivot to B2B infrastructure. The consumer product may be obsolete, but the parsing/normalization engine is not.

2. If 80% of users stop after their initial Coverage Score, the Loop does not work. Without the Loop, the Record never gets deep enough to be valuable.

3. If parsing accuracy cannot hit 98% on the top 5 lab formats (LabCorp, Quest, Function Health, Labcorp Oncology, hospital systems) within 2 weeks of focused work, the technical foundation is not there.

4. If users will not pay $50/yr after 90 days of free use, the 1Password model fails. Consider pivoting to pure B2B infrastructure.
