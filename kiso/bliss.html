01

The Structural Force

The US regulatory-commercial system cannot produce definitive answers about non-patentable therapeutic compounds. Not approval. Not rejection. Silence. This follows the same misallocation pattern Drew Endy has identified in biotech R&D: a system that overfunds applications and underfunds foundational tools. In therapeutics, the system generates extensive evidence for patentable molecules and none for public-domain compounds.

This isn't to say nobody has tried. Thymosin Beta-4 has had 17 clinical trials. RegeneRx's Phase 3 just missed its primary endpoint. Ipamorelin was tested in Phase 2 and failed on efficacy. But these are individual efforts on individual compounds. For most non-GLP-1 peptides, the system produces nothing. Three forces sustain the void:

Patent economics

Drug development costs $2.6B per approved compound. Patent exclusivity is the ROI mechanism. Peptides the body naturally produces (BPC-157, GLP-1, TB-500) cannot be patented in their native form. No patent, no ROI, no investment.

Semaglutide required just one amino acid swap and a fatty acid chain attachment to become patentable. That minimal modification created a $50B+ market and briefly made Novo Nordisk Europe's most valuable company. The natural GLP-1 peptide it derives from has a 2-minute half-life and will never be developed as a drug, despite being therapeutically relevant.

Regulatory tightening

FDA removed BPC-157 and TB-500 from the 503B bulk compounding list in 2024, driven by the semaglutide compounding crackdown. Legal compounding channels are closing. The gray market is expanding to fill the void, with supply chains running primarily through Chinese synthesis labs with no FDA oversight.

Data vacuum

BPC-157 has zero Phase 1 human trials. TB-500 has zero Phase 1 human trials. The evidence base for the most widely used non-GLP-1 peptides consists of rodent studies, cell culture experiments, Soviet-era clinical data that does not meet FDA evidentiary standards, and millions of unstructured anecdotal reports.

Nobody is systematically tracking: what peptide, what dose, what duration, what source, what biomarker changes, what outcomes.

The Vacuum

Millions of Americans self-administer peptides sourced from Chinese synthesis labs with zero systematic outcome data. The data infrastructure to evaluate these compounds does not exist. The pharmaceutical market will not build it. The patent system ensures this.

02

The Evidence Vacuum

The absence of systematic evidence creates four compounding problems. Each makes the next worse.

Supply chain opacity

The crisis is dosage accuracy, not purity. The largest independent testing dataset (Finnrick, Austin TX, 6,195 samples across 185 vendors) found BPC-157 dosage deviates up to +/-80% from labeled amounts. A vial labeled "10mg" might contain anywhere from 2mg to 18mg. 57% of BPC-157 vendors score "Poor" or worse. Only 32% of all tested vendors earn A or B grades. 8% of samples show bacterial endotoxin contamination (reported by MIT Technology Review, BBC, The Guardian). This is the entirety of the verification infrastructure for a market serving millions of Americans.

No risk stratification

BPC-157 promotes angiogenesis. For a user with family cancer history or undetected pre-malignant lesions, this is a specific, quantifiable risk factor. No tool connects individual health data to peptide risk profiles. Users make dosing decisions based on Reddit threads and influencer endorsements.

No outcome attribution

Did the BPC-157 heal the rotator cuff, or did 8 weeks of rest? Without structured before/during/after biomarker comparison, every reported outcome is unfalsifiable anecdote. The biohacker community generates enormous volume of outcome claims with zero methodological rigor.

No aggregate intelligence

Across the millions of Americans using non-GLP-1 peptides, nobody can answer basic questions: what protocols produce the best outcomes for specific conditions? What doses? What compounds interact? What suppliers have the best quality track record? This dataset does not exist. Not in pharma (no funding for non-patentable compounds). Not in academia (no structured data to analyze). Not in the biohacker community (anecdotal only).

The Market Response

The consumer market has produced 15-20 apps for logging peptide injections. None connect supply chain quality to protocols to biomarker outcomes to aggregate intelligence. The logging layer is commoditized. The intelligence layer is unbuilt.

03

Why This Is Infrastructure

The peptide evidence gap is not a consumer app problem. It is a health infrastructure gap with strategic dimensions.

Strategic Dependency
Chinese supply chain

US peptide supply runs through Chinese synthesis labs. No domestic pharmaceutical-grade manufacturing at scale for non-GLP-1 compounds. Same vulnerability pattern as semiconductors and rare earth minerals. A supply disruption or quality event has no backstop.

Innovation Blind Spot
Patent system failure mode

The patent system creates a structural blind spot for non-patentable therapeutics. An entire class of compounds with therapeutic potential is invisible to the pharmaceutical R&D engine. This is not a bug in the system. It is an emergent property of incentive design.

Public Health
Uncontrolled population experiment

Millions of Americans are running an uncontrolled experiment on themselves. The outcomes are not being captured. Adverse events go unreported. Positive outcomes go unverified. This is both a public health risk and a wasted data opportunity of historic scale.

Regulatory Gap
Framework in flux

FDA removed BPC-157 and TB-500 from compounding lists in 2024. But in February 2026, 14 of 19 Category 2 peptides reportedly moved back to Category 1, potentially re-legalizing compounding access. If access is expanding without evidence infrastructure, we're re-legalizing compounds the system still can't evaluate. The window to build the evidence layer is now, while the regulatory architecture is being reconstructed.

The ARPA-H Case

ARPA-H exists to fund research the market won't. The peptide evidence gap is a textbook case: population-scale experiment already underway, no commercial path to the data, and the system producing neither approval nor rejection. The goal is not to validate peptides. It is to produce definitive answers. Some compounds may prove worth developing patentable analogs of. Others may prove ineffective. Either outcome is a public health contribution. One company (Hudson Biotech) just started Phase 2 trials on BPC-157, TB-500, and GHK-Cu. But one company running three trials is not a system producing answers at the scale the market demands.

The GLP-1 precedent (and its limits)

GLP-1 was discovered in 1983. It took 34 years and over $1B in clinical development to produce semaglutide. The modification: one amino acid substitution and one fatty acid chain. Two changes to a naturally occurring peptide created a $50B market.

But GLP-1 had 2,158 published papers from dozens of independent research groups, an identified receptor (cloned in 1992), and extensive NIH funding before pharma modified it. BPC-157 has 212 papers, 80% from a single Croatian lab, no identified receptor, and no government funding. The analogy is instructive but not predictive. Some of these compounds may prove worth developing analogs of. Others may not. The point is that the current system cannot produce the answer either way.

The evidence platform is upstream of everything else. Without it, regulatory frameworks have no data to reference. Pharma companies have no signal to invest against. Clinicians have no evidence to prescribe from. Patients have no information to make decisions with. And the experiment is already running, uncontrolled, at population scale.

04

Questions for This Room

  • Is there a regulatory framework between FDA approval and DSHEA that could accommodate non-patentable therapeutic compounds with demonstrated real-world evidence? Or does the current binary (approved drug vs. supplement) need a third category?
  • Could real-world evidence at scale substitute for or complement traditional Phase I-III trials for compounds with extensive preclinical data and widespread human use? What evidentiary standard would that require?
  • Is the Chinese peptide supply chain dependency a strategic risk that warrants domestic manufacturing investment, or is the market too small and too gray to justify intervention?
  • What role should ARPA-H play in funding evidence infrastructure for non-patentable therapeutics? Is this a public good that the market structurally cannot provide?
  • How do we capture the data from the experiment already underway? Millions of Americans are taking these compounds. The outcomes are happening. The question is whether we build the infrastructure to learn from them or continue to let the data dissipate.
