01

Shared vocabulary

A few concepts I find useful that I'd propose we anchor on going forward.

Default-converging
A system where local decisions by individual actors naturally produce collective improvement. Each interaction makes the next one more valuable. The system pulls you back without requiring push. "First, get the loop to close. Then get it to be tight. Getting the loop to close is going from default-divergent to default-convergent. Once it's default-convergent, tightening is a simple matter of hill-climbing."
Default-diverging
A system that falls apart without constant external motivation. Users have to push themselves to engage. Nothing compounds. Effort doesn't accumulate. Most products are default-diverging. The rare ones that flip to default-converging become unstoppable.
Grubby Truffle
Something that looks rough but gets more compelling the closer you look. Exponential value for logarithmic cost. The opposite: a Gilded Turd. Looks polished but is hollow underneath. "If you over-polish for the level of underlying momentum, it looks like a Gilded Turd. If you under-polish, the closer people look, the more compelling it looks."
Compounding engineering
Every time an agent can't figure something out on its own, ask: "what tool would have helped you solve that?" Then build that tool. It accumulates durable leverage. Each tool compounds because you build it once and it keeps producing value. Convert recurring cognitive labor (opex) into durable tooling (capex).
Ephemeral to durable
Tokens are ephemeral. They only create value at time of use. But they can be used to create durable goods: tools, data, insights, code. The product that helps you convert ephemeral tokens into durable personal value is the one that wins. "All meaningful state accretes in the filesystem as data and code, that can easily be understood by any model."
Pace layers
Different parts of a system move at different speeds. Lower layers (data model, API) should be stable and boring. Higher layers (UI, features) can move fast. Connect them with rubber bands (loose coupling), not steel bands (tight coupling). If a fast layer is locked to a slow layer, everything stalls.
Gardener, not builder
You can't build an emergent system. You can only set the conditions and grow one. "It's best to grow an organization from a seed, carefully iterating and evolving it to stay continuously viable, than to try to build it." Products, codebases, coaching relationships all have this shape.
The synthesis pass
Spend 80% of time in fast execution, 20% in synthesis. During execution, you accumulate model error. During synthesis, you extract learning from that error. "The synthesis pass is important but never urgent. This is the closest to my secret: I just give it the time and attention it deserves." The sprint artifact template is our synthesis pass.

03

Health intelligence that compounds

The product should be a system where data you put in makes the system smarter about you, which makes the next interaction more valuable, which makes you want to put in more data. Default-converging.

Check habit
System reads biometrics
Surfaces insight
User sees impact
More motivated tomorrow

You check off all 3 bedtime habits. Tomorrow morning: "You hit bedtime at 9:30. You slept 7h 12m. That's 39 min more than your average." The habits feel meaningful because you see the result.

The outcomes this should compound toward:

Longevity (live longer), healthspan (live better), peakspan (stay near your best). These aren't features. They're the backdrop that gives every habit, every metric, every coaching note its meaning. "Your sleep consistency improved 18% this month. That's directly correlated with healthspan." That's the connection the product needs to make.

07

Agentic health infrastructure

The coaching prompt in the app is good. Peter Attia framing, 4-beat conversation, risk assessment, care team recommendations. That's the "what to recommend." But the full intelligence layer goes much further. Here's what exists and what's possible.

What's running today

Milo (Coach)
Conversational coaching agent. Context-aware: knows your cut phase, meds, training schedule, sleep patterns. Daily check-ins, meal logging with macro targets, trend analysis. Runs on Sonnet for coaching, Haiku for classification.
Atlas (Research)
PubMed research scanner. Daily RSS pulls from targeted searches (HRV wearable validation, sleep interventions, metabolic health). Haiku filters and summarizes. Sonnet runs weekly synthesis against our health metrics framework. Cost: ~$1.50/month.
Ops (System)
Infrastructure agent. Garmin data pulls every 4 hours. Trend updates. Cron health checks. Session management.

What becomes possible

When you combine the coaching prompt (your surface) with the intelligence layer (Kiso's backend), the product stops being a weekly text dump and becomes something alive:

Evidence-grounded coaching. Atlas pulls new research daily. When a PubMed paper validates or challenges a habit recommendation, Milo can reference it: "New study from Stanford shows your bedtime consistency approach reduces cardiovascular risk by 12%. Keep going."

Trend-aware responses. Not "eat 30g protein" as a static card. "You've averaged 142g protein this week, 48g short of your target. Your strength sessions drop off when protein dips below 160g. Here are 3 options to close the gap today."

Cross-metric pattern detection. "Your HRV drops the morning after nights you eat past 8pm. This has happened 6 of the last 8 times. Consider moving dinner earlier." This requires the habit data, the biometrics, and the intelligence layer all talking to each other.

The Focus Plan prompt is the architect's blueprint. The intelligence layer is the building that responds to weather, traffic, and the people inside it. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

Building with agents, not just using them

There's a difference between using AI to generate a weekly text report and building an agentic system that compounds. The current app uses AI as a one-shot oracle: dump context in, get a Focus Plan out. That's the chatbot paradigm.

The compounding paradigm is different. Real agents accumulate internal state. Each interaction should change the context for the next one. Each tool the agent builds for itself is a stepping stone it can reuse. Each coaching note that references a user's actual trend data is a cached insight that makes the next note sharper.

This is what Kiso does that a single prompt call can't: it remembers what worked. It tracks which habits actually moved which metrics for which person. It can say "the last time you were consistent with bedtime for 10 days, your HRV improved 8%. You're on day 6." That's not a prompt. That's accumulated state producing compounding leverage.

The question for the sprint: are we building a system that gets smarter about each user over time, or are we running a fresh prompt against a static profile once a week?
