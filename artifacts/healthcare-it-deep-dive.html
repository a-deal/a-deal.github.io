Chapter 1 / The Patient Reality

What the Patient Sees vs. What Actually Happens

A patient checks in, sees a doctor, gets a prescription, receives a bill. Four steps. Behind each one runs a hidden pipeline: insurance verification, benefits calculation, payment authorization, prior approval scan, clinical documentation, drug formulary checks, allergy cross-references, pharmacy routing, coverage verification, medical coding review, claims submission, adjudication, and patient billing.

Healthcare IT is only ~3.5% of total healthcare spending. But it is the connective tissue everything depends on. Hospitals can't bill without it. Physicians can't prescribe without it. Claims can't move without it.

The problem starts when care crosses vendors. Within a single provider system, applications communicate because they share infrastructure. When a patient moves between an Epic hospital and a Cerner hospital, records must be translated, verified, or rebuilt manually. The U.S. has no universal patient identifier. HIPAA mandated one in 1996, but Congress blocked funding two years later citing privacy concerns, and that ban has been renewed in every appropriations bill since.

Chapter 2 / The 1966 Decision

The Origin: MUMPS

Massachusetts General Hospital needed a computer system for 20 departments on a PDP-7 with 4KB of RAM. Three developers (Neil Pappalardo, Robert Greenes, Curt Marble) in Dr. G. Octo Barnett's lab built MUMPS: Massachusetts General Hospital Utility Multi-Programming System. An operating system, programming language, and database fused into one inseparable layer.

It stored data as trees (globals), not tables. Branches only existed when data existed. If a patient had no allergies, there was no allergy branch. No wasted memory. Each department got its own branch of the tree, so dozens of departments could write to the same patient record simultaneously without conflicts.

The tradeoff: reading across patients (population analytics) requires walking every tree, one by one. MUMPS was built to answer "what is happening with this patient right now?" Not "what is happening across all patients."

The solution they built still forms the foundation of Epic, Meditech, and every major EHR vendor in 2025.

The Dictionary Problem

On 4KB, even labels were too expensive. So they replaced readable names with numeric codes. Field .01 means "Patient Name." Field .02 means "Gender." Nothing in the data itself tells you that. The meaning lives in a proprietary data dictionary controlled by the vendor.

// What you export
^PATIENT(123,.01) = "SMITH,JOHN"
^PATIENT(123,.02) = "M"
^PATIENT(123,.03) = "19850315"
^PATIENT(123,.09) = "401.9"

// Without the vendor dictionary, this is meaningless.
// With it: Smith, John. Male. Born 1985. Hypertension.

You can export raw data, but without the dictionary, that export is a list of numbers with no meaning attached. Vendors treated the dictionary as intellectual property. This structural lock persists to this day.

Chronicles and Clarity

Population-level queries compete with the same resources clinicians depend on. Epic solved this with a dual-database architecture. Chronicles (the live MUMPS database) handles real-time clinical data. Every night, a batch process copies it into Clarity, a separate SQL database built for analytics.

The workaround became the industry standard. But it introduced a permanent constraint: analytics always runs on yesterday's data.

21 hrs
A sepsis patient's vitals spike at 2 AM in Chronicles. The Clarity alert fires at 11:30 PM: "Patient showed sepsis indicators 21 hours ago." The intervention window closed before the data arrived.

Why No One Has Escaped

Migrating a hospital EHR costs $50M to $500M and takes 18 to 36 months. A new system must prove it will never crash during patient care before hospitals will adopt it. The cost of leaving exceeds the pain of staying.

Network effects compound the lock. Epic hospitals can access other Epic hospitals through Care Everywhere. Epic added 176 hospitals in 2024. Roughly 90% of medical residents now train on Epic. Epic has lost extremely few customers in its 47-year history, with one documented instance of a customer leaving for 6 months before returning.

Chapter 3 / Rising Costs & Population-Level Pressure

The Spending Crisis

6.9% → 18%
Healthcare as a share of U.S. GDP, from 1970 to 2024. The U.S. spends roughly 50% more per capita than peer nations while achieving worse outcomes. The gap persists even among higher-income Americans.

The Payment Model Shifted

Under fee-for-service, providers are paid for each service delivered. More tests, more visits, more revenue. The only data required is what was done and who should be billed.

Payers began experimenting with value-based care, where providers are paid for outcomes rather than volume. Keeping patients healthy increases revenue. Poor outcomes reduce it. Medicare now ties a significant share of hospital payments to quality metrics and readmission rates.

Fee-for-service needs documentation. Value-based care needs measurement. The EHR was built to do the first, and the economy now runs on the second.

Value-based care requires tracking outcomes over time, comparing performance across providers, and identifying patients at risk before they deteriorate. All of these require reading across patients, the one thing the architecture can't do without risking the live clinical system.

Chapter 4 / The Illusion of Interoperability

40 Years of Attempts

Interoperability requires solving four layers: foundational (connect), structural (format), semantic (meaning), and organizational (governance). Over four decades, the industry tried six major approaches. Each solved a layer. None solved all four.

1987
HL7 v2: shared format, but vendors kept 20% proprietary. Each interface $500K+.
2005
HL7 v3: removed ambiguity, spec ran thousands of pages. Too complex. Never adopted.
2009
HITECH: $27B for EHR adoption. 9% to 97% in 6 years. Paper silos became digital silos.
2014
FHIR: REST + JSON. Interface cost $500K to $50K. Vendors in no rush.
2016
Cures Act: data blocking illegal. $1M fines. 1,500+ complaints since 2021.
2023
TEFCA: hub-and-spoke. But exchanges deliver PDFs, not structured data.

Data that is technically interoperable is not necessarily integrated. It arrives in separate portals, un-normalized, or too late to influence decisions. And liability compounds the problem: when data lives outside a clinician's own EHR, acting on it creates legal exposure.

Why Estonia Doesn't Translate

Estonia has 1.3 million people and a single national health IT infrastructure. Every citizen accesses their complete medical history through one system. The U.S. has 330 million people, 6,000+ hospitals, 50 state regulatory regimes, and a multi-payer system with competing commercial interests. Even the UK's NHS, with a national patient identifier, still fragments across hospital trusts and GP systems.

The foundational, structural, and organizational layers are largely addressed. The semantic layer remains the gap. Machines still can't tell that "MI" and "heart attack" and "acute myocardial infarction" all mean the same thing.
Chapter 5 / Infrastructure Consolidation

If Data Won't Flow, Control the Pipes

When interoperability couldn't be forced, market participants pursued a simpler strategy: buy enough of the system that interoperability becomes internal.

Change Healthcare Breach / Feb 2024

An attacker entered through a remote access portal that lacked multi-factor authentication. Nine days later, BlackCat ransomware encrypted systems responsible for 40% of U.S. medical claims. UnitedHealth paid $22M in Bitcoin. The ransomware group kept the payment without deleting the stolen data.

192.7 million Americans exposed. Total financial impact exceeded $2.45 billion. 94% of hospitals reported financial damage. Concentration creates efficiency. It also creates catastrophic single points of failure.

Both strategies work around interoperability by making it internal. For everyone outside those walls, the constraints remain. Neither reduced the administrative layer between providers and payers.

The Translation Workforce

Healthcare billing runs on ICD-10 (over 70,000 diagnosis codes) and CPT (over 10,000 procedure codes). Every clinical encounter must be translated into both before payment can happen.

A physician documents: "65 year old male with exertional chest pain relieved by rest. Family history of coronary artery disease. Stress test ordered." A billing system requires ICD-10 code I25.10 and CPT code 93015.

The system employs roughly 185,000 medical coders to perform that translation. The code set includes W56.22XA: "struck by orca, initial encounter." One of 70,000+ codes a coder must classify.

Revenue Cycle Management

The billing pipeline became complex enough to support an entire outsourcing industry. RCM companies charge 5-10% of collections but often recover 3-5% more than in-house teams. The RCM market hit $142B in 2024 and is projected to reach $273B by 2030. The growth reflects rising complexity, not improving efficiency.

Why the Layer Persists

Nobody with significant power has a strong financial reason to simplify it. Vendors earn from integration services. RCM companies grow when billing gets harder. The 185,000 coding workforce has professional associations, certifications, and training programs with deep institutional interest. Payers use prior authorization to control utilization, even when it adds friction for everyone else.

Chapter 7 / LLMs Bypass Old Constraints

The Reinforcing Loop

Architecture constrains what regulation can change. Weak enforcement preserves intermediary margins. Those margins fund resistance to architectural reform. Three locks: safety over intelligence, the individual over the crowd, margin from friction. They compound into an equilibrium that absorbs reform from any single direction.

The $850M Lesson

In 2017, Olive AI raised $850M to automate EHR workflows through RPA: bots clicking through interfaces. Peak valuation: $4B. Shut down by 2023. Healthcare software interfaces change frequently. Each update moved the buttons the bots depended on. A human finds the right field by reading the label. Olive's robot clicked where the button used to be. It failed because it targeted the UI layer, not the semantic layer.

Why Previous AI Didn't Help

Over 70% of FDA-approved AI devices focus on imaging: reading scans, detecting tumors. Those work on structured pixels. Every constraint in this analysis requires something imaging AI was never built to do: understand unstructured clinical language.

What Changed

For sixty years, "MI" and "heart attack" and "acute myocardial infarction" were the same condition to a physician and three different strings to software. LLMs developed contextual comprehension. This capability is now in production:

The Patient Pathway

Everything so far has been the institutional vector: trying to get organizations to share data through standards, regulation, and infrastructure. Slow, full of loopholes. A second vector is forming entirely outside those walls.

HIPAA governs how institutions share data with each other. When patients share their own records, different rules apply and institutional barriers largely disappear. Healthcare is one of the last industries where consumers don't have meaningful control over their own data. Less than 5% of health system maturity benchmarks even measure patient digital engagement.

The Pincer Strategy

Consumer Aggregation (OpenAI / ChatGPT Health, Jan 7 2026): Patients download and aggregate their own records, bypassing institutional barriers. When patients share their own data, HIPAA's institutional rules largely don't apply.

Enterprise Compression (Anthropic / Claude Healthcare, Jan 11 2026): Automate the $1T administrative layer from inside. Documentation, coding, prior auth, clinical trial management.

Both launched within days of each other at the J.P. Morgan Healthcare Conference.

CMS Interoperability Framework

July 2025: CMS launched a voluntary framework with 60+ companies (Amazon, Apple, Google, OpenAI, Anthropic) pledging to meet data sharing criteria. The framework is voluntary. The binding deadline for FHIR-based data exchange is July 2026.

The patient consent pathway may move more usable data than four decades of institutional standards.
