Chapter 1 / The Patient Reality

What the Patient Sees vs. What Actually Happens

A patient checks in, sees a doctor, gets a prescription, receives a bill. Four steps. Behind each one runs a hidden pipeline: insurance verification, benefits calculation, payment authorization, prior approval scan, clinical documentation, drug formulary checks, allergy cross-references, pharmacy routing, coverage verification, medical coding review, claims submission, adjudication, and patient billing.

Healthcare IT is only ~3.5% of total healthcare spending. But it is the connective tissue everything depends on. Hospitals can't bill without it. Physicians can't prescribe without it. Claims can't move without it.

The problem starts when care crosses vendors. Within a single provider system, applications communicate because they share infrastructure. When a patient moves between an Epic hospital and a Cerner hospital, records must be translated, verified, or rebuilt manually. The U.S. has no universal patient identifier. HIPAA mandated one in 1996, but Congress blocked funding two years later citing privacy concerns, and that ban has been renewed in every appropriations bill since.

Chapter 2 / The 1966 Decision

The Origin: MUMPS

Massachusetts General Hospital needed a computer system for 20 departments on a PDP-7 with 4KB of RAM. Three developers (Neil Pappalardo, Robert Greenes, Curt Marble) in Dr. G. Octo Barnett's lab built MUMPS: Massachusetts General Hospital Utility Multi-Programming System. An operating system, programming language, and database fused into one inseparable layer.

It stored data as trees (globals), not tables. Branches only existed when data existed. If a patient had no allergies, there was no allergy branch. No wasted memory. Each department got its own branch of the tree, so dozens of departments could write to the same patient record simultaneously without conflicts. A nurse entering vitals never touched the branch where lab results lived.

The tradeoff: reading across patients (population analytics) requires walking every tree, one by one. MUMPS was built to answer "what is happening with this patient right now?" Not "what is happening across all patients."

The solution they built still forms the foundation of Epic, Meditech, and every major EHR vendor in 2025.

The Dictionary Problem

Field names like "Patient Name" and "Date of Birth" take up memory. On 4KB, even labels were too expensive. So they replaced readable names with numeric codes. Field .01 means "Patient Name." Field .02 means "Gender." But nothing in the data itself tells you that.

// What you export
^PATIENT(123,.01) = "SMITH,JOHN"
^PATIENT(123,.02) = "M"
^PATIENT(123,.03) = "19850315"
^PATIENT(123,.09) = "401.9"

// What it means (locked in vendor dictionary)
// Patient Name: SMITH, JOHN
// Gender: Male
// Date of Birth: March 15, 1985
// Diagnosis: Hypertension (ICD-9 401.9)

The meaning of each field is stored in a proprietary data dictionary controlled by the vendor. You can export raw data, but without the dictionary, that export is a list of numbers with no meaning attached. Vendors treated the dictionary as intellectual property. This created a structural lock that persists to this day.

Epic's Workaround: Chronicles and Clarity

Population-level queries require scanning large portions of the database, competing with the same resources used by clinicians. Analytical queries can interfere with the live system. Epic solved this with a dual-database architecture:

The Nightly ETL

Chronicles (the live MUMPS database): real-time clinical data, tree format, cannot be queried safely for analytics.

Clarity (the SQL database): analytics-ready, table format, safe to query. Updated by a nightly batch process that copies data from Chronicles.

The workaround became the industry standard. But it introduced a permanent constraint: analytics always runs on yesterday's data.

The 24-Hour Gap

For compliance reporting, day-old data is acceptable. The Joint Commission and CMS do not require infection rates by the minute. But clinical care is different. A sepsis patient's vitals can deteriorate within minutes. The pattern is visible in the data. An algorithm watching in real-time could flag it, giving clinicians a critical head start.

But analytics runs on Clarity, which receives its data during the nightly batch process. When the data becomes available for analysis, the opportunity for intervention may already be gone. Vitals spike at 2 AM in Chronicles. The alert fires at 11:30 PM in Clarity: "Patient showed sepsis indicators 21 hours ago."

Why No One Has Escaped

Migrating a hospital EHR system costs $50M to $500M and takes 18 to 36 months, with both systems running simultaneously during transition. A new system must prove it will never crash during patient care before hospitals will adopt it. Demonstrating that reliability requires operating at scale. But reaching scale requires hospitals willing to take the initial risk. The cost of leaving exceeds the pain of staying.

Network effects compound the lock. Epic hospitals can access other Epic hospitals through Care Everywhere. The more hospitals join, the more valuable Epic becomes. Epic added 176 hospitals in 2024. Roughly 90% of medical residents now train on Epic, reinforcing the cycle through muscle memory. Epic has lost extremely few customers in its 47-year history, with one documented instance of a customer leaving for 6 months before returning.

Chapter 3 / Rising Costs & Population-Level Pressure

The Spending Crisis

In 1970, healthcare accounted for 6.9% of U.S. GDP. By 2000, 13.3%. By 2024, 18%. The U.S. spends $14,775 per person on healthcare each year. Germany spends $9,365. The UK spends $6,747. Japan spends $5,790. The U.S. spends roughly 50% more than peer nations while achieving worse outcomes on many population health metrics. The gap persists even among higher-income Americans.

The Payment Model Shifted

Under the traditional fee-for-service model, providers are paid for each service delivered. More tests, more procedures, more visits generate more revenue. The only data required is what was done and who should be billed.

Payers began experimenting with value-based care, where providers are paid for outcomes rather than volume. Keeping patients healthy increases revenue. Poor outcomes reduce it. Medicare now ties a significant share of hospital payments to quality metrics and readmission rates.

Fee-for-service needs documentation. Value-based care needs measurement. The EHR was built to do the first, and the economy now runs on the second.

This model requires a different data capability: tracking outcomes over time, comparing performance across providers, and identifying patients at risk before they deteriorate. All of these require reading across patients, the one thing the architecture can't do without risking the live clinical system.

Chapter 4 / The Illusion of Interoperability

40 Years of Failed Interoperability

Interoperability requires solving problems at four layers: foundational (connect), structural (format), semantic (meaning), and organizational (governance). Over four decades, the industry tried six major approaches. Each solved a layer. None solved all four.

1987
HL7 v2: shared format, but "Z-segments" let vendors keep 20% proprietary. Each interface cost $500K+.
2005
HL7 v3: removed ambiguity, spec ran thousands of pages. Too complex. HL7 v2 still dominates (80%+ of exchanges).
2009
HITECH Act: $27B for EHR adoption. Medicare: $44K/physician. Medicaid: $63,750. 9% to 97% in 6 years. Paper silos became digital silos.
2014
FHIR: REST APIs + JSON. Cost per interface $500K to $50K. Vendors in no rush to adopt.
2016
Cures Act: data blocking illegal. FHIR R4 APIs required. $1M fines. 1,500+ complaints filed since 2021.
2023
TEFCA: hub-and-spoke via QHINs. But exchanges deliver PDFs, not structured data. Providers hesitate on data quality.

Compliance Does Not Equal Utility

The metrics look promising on paper. 70% of U.S. hospitals engage in core interoperability actions. But only 42% of clinicians actually use external data at the point of care. 15-30% of key clinical variables are missing in exchanges. Only ~35% of interoperability projects meet their ROI targets.

Data that is technically interoperable is not necessarily integrated. It arrives in separate portals, un-normalized, or too late to influence decisions. Liability compounds the problem: when data lives outside a clinician's own EHR, acting on it creates legal exposure.

Why Estonia Doesn't Translate

Estonia has 1.3 million people and a single national health IT infrastructure maintained by government. Every citizen can access their complete medical history through a national system. The U.S. has 330 million people, over 6,000 hospitals, 50 state regulatory regimes, and a multi-payer system with competing commercial interests. Even the UK's NHS, with a national patient identifier and single-payer structure, still operates with significant fragmentation across hospital trusts and GP systems.

The foundational, structural, and organizational layers are largely addressed. The semantic layer remains the gap. Machines still can't tell that "MI" and "heart attack" and "acute myocardial infarction" all mean the same thing.

Change Healthcare Breach (Feb 2024)

An attacker entered Change Healthcare's network through a remote access portal that lacked multi-factor authentication. Nine days later, BlackCat ransomware encrypted systems responsible for 40% of U.S. medical claims. UnitedHealth paid $22M in Bitcoin. The ransomware group kept the payment without deleting the stolen data. A second group later attempted extortion using the same records.

192.7 million Americans exposed (roughly 2 out of 3 people in the U.S.). Total financial impact exceeded $2.45 billion. 94% of hospitals reported financial damage. Concentration creates efficiency. It also creates catastrophic single points of failure.

Both consolidation strategies work around interoperability by making it internal. Data flows freely inside Epic's ecosystem and inside United's subsidiaries. For everyone outside those walls, the constraints remain. Neither strategy reduced the administrative layer that sits between providers and payers.

The Translation Workforce

Healthcare billing runs on two coding systems: ICD-10, which classifies why the patient was treated (over 70,000 diagnosis codes), and CPT, which classifies what was done (over 10,000 procedure codes). Every clinical encounter has to be translated into both before payment can happen.

A physician documents: "65 year old male with exertional chest pain relieved by rest. Family history of coronary artery disease. Stress test ordered." A billing system requires structured codes: ICD-10 diagnosis code I25.10 and CPT procedure code 93015.

The system employs roughly 185,000 medical coders to perform that translation. The code set includes W56.22XA: "struck by orca, initial encounter." One of over 70,000 codes a coder must be prepared to classify.

How a Claim Becomes Payment

The U.S. has thousands of insurers, each with its own billing rules and reimbursement logic. Every stage of the claims process requires translating clinical information into a new format: Encounter, Documentation, Coding, Claim Assembly, Clearinghouse, Payer Adjudication. Payment arrives 14-30 days later. Many providers report over 10% denial rate.

Permission Before Treatment

Before many treatments move forward, providers must obtain permission from insurers through prior authorization. The system intentionally introduces friction to slow expensive interventions.

Revenue Cycle Management

The billing pipeline became complex enough to support an entire outsourcing industry. RCM companies handle the whole process for providers, from initial claim submission through denial management to final payment collection. They charge 5-10% of collections but often recover 3-5% more revenue than in-house teams because they specialize in navigating payer-specific rules and denial patterns.

Why the Layer Persists

The administrative layer persists because nobody with significant power has a strong financial reason to simplify it. Vendors earn revenue from integration services and hold the proprietary data dictionaries. RCM companies grow when billing gets more complicated. The 185,000 coding workforce has professional associations, certifications, and training programs with deep institutional interest in continuity. Payers use tools like prior authorization to control utilization and costs, even when those tools add friction for providers and patients.

Chapter 7

LLMs Bypass Old Constraints

The Reinforcing Loop

Architecture constrains what regulation can change. Weak enforcement preserves intermediary margins. Those margins fund resistance to architectural reform. Three locks: safety over intelligence, the individual over the crowd, margin from friction. They compound into an equilibrium that absorbs reform from any single direction.

The $850M Lesson: Olive AI

In 2017, Olive AI raised $850M to automate EHR workflows through RPA (rule-based bots clicking through interfaces). Peak valuation: $4B. Shut down by 2023. Healthcare software interfaces change frequently. Each update moved the buttons and fields the bots depended on. A human finds the right field by reading the label. Olive's robot clicked where the button used to be. It failed because it targeted the UI layer, not the semantic layer.

Why Previous AI Didn't Help

AI has been in healthcare for years. Over 70% of FDA-approved AI devices focus on imaging: reading scans, detecting tumors, flagging anomalies. Those applications work on structured pixels. Every constraint in this analysis requires something imaging AI was never built to do: understand unstructured clinical language.

What Changed

For sixty years, software could not interpret what clinical data actually means. "MI" and "heart attack" and "acute myocardial infarction" are the same condition. A physician knows that instantly. Software could not. LLMs developed contextual comprehension. This capability is now in production:

The Patient Pathway

Everything covered so far has been the institutional vector: trying to get organizations to share data through standards, regulation, and infrastructure. Slow progress, full of loopholes. A second vector is forming entirely outside those walls.

HIPAA governs how institutions share data with each other. When patients share their own records, different rules apply and institutional barriers largely disappear. Healthcare is one of the last industries where consumers don't have meaningful control over their own data. Less than 5% of health system maturity benchmarks even measure how well they engage patients digitally.

The Pincer Strategy

Consumer Aggregation (OpenAI / ChatGPT Health, Jan 7 2026): Patients download and aggregate their own records, bypassing institutional barriers. When patients share their own data, HIPAA's institutional rules largely don't apply.

Enterprise Compression (Anthropic / Claude Healthcare, Jan 11 2026): Automate the $1T administrative layer from inside. Documentation, coding, prior auth, clinical trial management, regulatory operations.

Both launched within days of each other at the J.P. Morgan Healthcare Conference. Both target the same locked system from opposite sides simultaneously.

CMS Interoperability Framework

In July 2025, CMS launched a voluntary Interoperability Framework with over 60 companies including Amazon, Apple, Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic pledging to meet data sharing criteria. Early networks are beginning implementation as of Q1 2026. The framework is voluntary. The harder binding deadline for FHIR-based data exchange is July 2026.

The patient consent pathway may move more usable data than four decades of institutional standards.
