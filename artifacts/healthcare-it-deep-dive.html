The Origin: 1966

Massachusetts General Hospital needed a computer system for 20 departments on a PDP-7 with 4KB of RAM. Three developers built MUMPS: an operating system, programming language, and database fused into one inseparable layer. It stored data as trees (globals), not tables. Branches only existed when data existed. Fast writes, concurrent access, zero wasted memory.

The tradeoff: reading across patients (population analytics) requires walking every tree, one by one. MUMPS was built to answer "what is happening with this patient right now?" Not "what is happening across all patients."

The solution they built still forms the foundation of Epic, Meditech, and every major EHR vendor in 2025.

Each attempt solved one layer. None solved all four: foundational (connect), structural (format), semantic (meaning), organizational (governance). 70% of hospitals can exchange data. Only 42% of clinicians actually use it at point of care.

The Reinforcing Loop

Architecture constrains what regulation can change. Weak enforcement preserves intermediary margins. Those margins fund resistance to architectural reform. Three locks: safety over intelligence, the individual over the crowd, margin from friction. They compound into an equilibrium that absorbs reform from any single direction.

The $1T Administrative Layer

185,000 medical coders translate every clinical note into billing codes. Physicians spend 1-2 hours per day on documentation after patient care. Prior authorizations require 14 hours of physician time per week per practice. The administrative layer exists because machines couldn't read clinical language. The system runs on human translation.

What Changed: LLMs

For sixty years, software could not interpret what clinical data actually means. "MI" and "heart attack" and "acute myocardial infarction" are the same condition. A physician knows that instantly. Software could not.

LLMs developed contextual comprehension. This capability is now in production: ambient documentation (100+ health systems), clinical trial matching (minutes vs weeks), prior auth automation. Olive AI raised $850M for RPA automation and shut down by 2023 because it targeted the UI layer, not the semantic layer.

The Pincer Strategy

Consumer Aggregation (OpenAI): Patients download and aggregate their own records, bypassing institutional barriers. When patients share their own data, HIPAA's institutional rules largely don't apply.

Enterprise Compression (Anthropic): Automate the $1T administrative layer from inside. Documentation, coding, prior auth.

Both target the same locked system from opposite sides simultaneously.

The patient consent pathway may move more usable data than four decades of institutional standards.

Why This Matters for Kasane

We're on the consumer aggregation side, but going deeper. ChatGPT Health lets you upload and read your records. We connect records to behavior change. The gap Social Capital identifies ("patients downloading their own records") is literally what our Apple Health shortcut, Garmin integration, and lab upload pipeline do.

But we add the coaching layer. Data without action is a dashboard. Data with coaching is a program. That's the gap nobody else is filling.

The value-based care shift (providers paid for outcomes, not volume) is our tailwind. If keeping patients healthy increases revenue, then tools that actually change patient behavior become infrastructure, not nice-to-haves.
