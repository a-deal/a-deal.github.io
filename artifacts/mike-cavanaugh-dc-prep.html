The Opportunity

Why warehouse owners in Austin are sitting on something valuable

Austin has a supply/demand mismatch that's creating a once-in-a-cycle opportunity for warehouse owners. The short version: AI companies need buildings with power, and there aren't enough.

21.9%
Industrial warehouse vacancy in Austin. Up 860 bps YoY. Warehouses are struggling.
10-14x
Value uplift converting a qualifying warehouse to data center use.
$196/kW
Monthly colocation rate. Expected to breach $200 in 2026.

The math in plain English

A 200,000 sq ft warehouse in Austin generates about $2.6M/year in rent and is worth roughly $38M. That same building converted to a powered shell data center generates $29M/year and is worth $364M. Even at half that scale, a 100K sq ft building goes from a ~$15M asset to $150M+.

Why now

AI companies (Meta, Microsoft, Google) are spending $600B+ this year building compute infrastructure. Austin is the #2 market nationally for new data center construction. Dallas is running out of room, pushing demand south. There are 170,000+ tech workers in Austin generating baseline enterprise cloud demand.

Why not everyone can do this

The bottleneck is power, not buildings. 225+ GW of large load requests are queued at ERCOT (the Texas grid operator), and 73% are data centers. New applications enter a multi-year process. Realistic timeline for 75+ MW: 3-5 years from application to energized substation.

The small operator advantage: Projects under 75 MW bypass the ERCOT large load queue entirely. They go through Austin Energy's distribution planning instead, cutting 1-2 years off the timeline. A 50,000-100,000 sq ft warehouse converting to 5-15 MW is the sweet spot.

Qualification

Does the building work?

Not every warehouse qualifies. Six factors determine if a conversion is viable. A building needs to hit at least 4 of 6.

Physical requirements
Ceiling height: 24 ft minimum, 30-36 ft preferred
Floor load: 150 lbs/sq ft minimum, 250 preferred
Column spacing: 40x40 ft minimum, 50x50+ preferred
Footprint: 50,000 sq ft minimum, 150-500K preferred
Power path: Achievable within 36 months
Year built: Post-2000 (pre-2000 stock at 18-22 ft ceilings is generally a disqualifier)

The first conversation with any warehouse owner should cover these specs. If the building doesn't qualify physically, the rest doesn't matter.

Paths Forward

A warehouse owner doesn't need to become a data center operator

There are four paths. The right one depends on capital access, risk appetite, and how long Mike (or whoever owns the building) wants to stay involved.

Option 1: Joint Venture
Contribute the building. Partner provides capital + expertise.
Building contributed at appraised value. Development partner (Skybox, Prologis, etc.) handles engineering, permitting, tenant leasing. Owner participates in upside with a 20-40% promote on stabilized value. Lowest risk, still significant upside.
Option 2: Sell at DC Premium
Exit now at 3-6x replacement cost for power-committed sites.
Clean exit. Liquidity now. The 2025-2027 window is likely the strongest near-term seller's market. Best for owners who want proceeds, not a development project.
Option 3: Ground Lease
Retain fee interest. 50-99 year lease. Participate in upside.
Keep the land, collect lease payments, participate in development economics. Long-term income stream without capital outlay.
Option 4: Convert (Owner-led)
Retain ownership. Requires $50M+ capital and development expertise.
Highest upside, highest complexity. Requires long hold horizon, significant capital, and either in-house or contracted MEP/data center engineering. Not realistic for most small operators without a development partner.
Meeting Plan

30 minutes. One question determines the shape.

This is a first meeting with someone you don't know. The goal is not to pitch. The goal is to understand what Mike actually has and whether the thesis applies. Everything hinges on one question at minute 5.

0:00 - 5:00 / Connect
Be a person first. "Tell me about Austin Cleaning Crew. How'd you and Tommy get that going?" He built a services business from scratch. He understands hustle, margins, managing people. Meet him there. Don't jump to data centers.
5:00 / The Pivot Question
"So what got you interested in the data center side of this?" His answer tells you which shape the rest of the meeting takes. Listen carefully. Reflect back what you hear before moving forward.

Three shapes the meeting can take

Shape A: Mike has a building
Run the diagnostic. This is where you deliver the most value.
Spend minutes 7-20 on the conversational diagnostic (below). If the building looks viable, orient him on JV and sell as the two realistic paths. Close with: "Send me the address and any building docs you have. I'll run the full qualification and get back to you within 48 hours."
Shape B: Mike knows someone with a building
Equip him to be the messenger.
Give him the simple thesis and the five questions to ask the actual owner. The value you deliver: making Mike useful to his contact. Close with: "Here's the framework. If the building fits, I'd love a three-way intro. You'll know in five minutes whether it's worth the conversation."
Shape C: Mike is just curious
Be generous with 15 minutes. Let him go early.
No specific property. The buddy mentioned data centers, Mike thought it sounded interesting. Share the thesis at a high level, send the link, tell him what to look for if he comes across a building. Don't stretch it to 30 minutes. Respecting his time builds more trust than filling the clock.

Every shape ends the same way: one named next step, one owner, one date. Not "let's stay in touch."

Diagnostic

Qualifying the building in conversation

The technical criteria matter, but Mike probably can't answer them in engineering terms. Here's what to ask and what it tells you.

What Mike can answer in the meeting

Size

"How big is the building?"

You need 50,000 sq ft minimum. Under that, it's probably not worth the conversion cost. 100K-200K is the sweet spot for a small operator play.

Age

"Roughly when was it built?"

Post-2000 is needed. Pre-2000 warehouse stock typically has 18-22 ft ceilings, which can't accommodate the cooling infrastructure. This is the fastest disqualifier.

Height

"Is it tall inside? Like could you stack shipping containers two high?"

You need 24 ft minimum, 30-36 ft preferred. A shipping container is about 8.5 ft, so "two high with room to spare" means you're in range. Most people can eyeball this even if they don't know the exact number.

Utility

"Who's the electric provider? Austin Energy?"

Austin Energy = access to distribution planning, which bypasses the ERCOT large load queue. This is the sub-75 MW advantage. He may not know the service capacity, but the provider tells you the path.

Status

"Is the building being used right now, or is it sitting?"

Empty = faster timeline, no tenant displacement. Occupied = longer but still viable. Tells you urgency and flexibility.

What Mike probably can't answer (becomes the follow-up)

Follow-up homework
Floor load capacity — needs structural engineer or original building plans. 150 lbs/sq ft minimum.
Column spacing — needs plans or a site visit. 40x40 ft minimum.
Electrical service detail — needs utility records. What's the current service? Is there a nearby substation?
Lot size beyond the building — room for transformer pads, generator yards, parking for service vehicles.

The close if the building sounds viable: "Send me the address and any building docs you have. Original plans, tax records, whatever. I'll run the full qualification check and tell you within 48 hours whether this is worth pursuing and what the next step would be."

The close if it doesn't qualify: Be honest and direct. "Based on what you're describing, this building probably doesn't fit the data center conversion model. Here's why." Honesty builds more trust than forcing a maybe. He'll remember you as the guy who gave him a straight answer.

Ask

The question that determines the path

The motivation question

"What are you hoping to get out of this? Are you looking for an exit, ongoing income, or something else?"

Only ask this if the building looks viable. His answer determines which path to emphasize. Exit = sell at DC premium. Income = ground lease. Upside = JV. Don't walk through all four options. Match the one or two that fit what he wants.

Operating Notes

Running the meeting well

Mochary: Fundraising Relationship Method applies here. Build trust before discussing the opportunity. Ask about his life, prove you heard him, prove you remembered. Appreciation before business. The data center thesis is not a pitch. It's a gift you give after you've earned the right.

"Heard" before solutions

When Mike describes his situation, reflect it back before analyzing. "What I'm hearing is you've got a building in [area] that's been sitting, and a friend told you there might be a better play. Is that right?" Don't jump to the qualification checklist until he confirms you understand his situation.

Separate decision from implementation

If the building qualifies, don't go down the rabbit hole of JV structures and ERCOT timelines. First question: "Does this interest you?" Second question (different meeting): "How do we do it?" Mixing them in 30 minutes overwhelms a first conversation.

One DRI, one next step

End with a named action and a named owner. "I'll run the qualification check and get back to you by [day]." Or "You'll send me the building specs and I'll tell you what I see." No "let's stay in touch." Impeccable agreements, even small ones, build trust.

Above the line

Check yourself before the call. Are you going in curious and open, or are you going in trying to close something? Mike will feel the difference. This is a 30-minute conversation where the best outcome is that he walks away thinking "that guy gave me something useful and didn't ask for anything."
