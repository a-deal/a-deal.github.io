Private Equity Exit:
Why This Matters

SCRAM Systems is owned by The Riverside Company, a global PE firm focused on the lower middle market. This is a classic roll-up: buy adjacent monitoring companies, consolidate operations, sell the combined platform at a premium.

2014 Riverside makes minority investment in SCRAM's parent company

2026 Full acquisition is 5 years old. Minority stake is 12 years old.

Typical PE hold periods are 3-7 years. Fund lifecycles run ~10 years. At 5 years post-acquisition, SCRAM is squarely in the exit window. A sale, recapitalization, or secondary buyout in the next 1-3 years is plausible.

What this means for you: If Riverside exits while you're employed at SCRAM, your Ownership Works equity could pay out. This is potentially the most valuable part of your compensation that you don't yet understand. Don't negotiate salary until you know what this is worth.

03

Ownership Works:
The Equity Program

A nonprofit founded in 2021 by Pete Stavros (KKR partner). It helps PE-backed companies create broad-based employee equity programs. 37 PE firms participate. As of late 2024: 113 companies, 163,000+ employees, $570M in equity payouts.

How it typically works

  • Employees receive equity grants (not options) on top of existing wages and benefits
  • Typical payout: 6-12 months of salary when the PE firm sells the company
  • Payout triggered by a "liquidity event": PE sale, secondary buyout, or IPO
  • Vesting schedules vary. Common: 3-4 year vest with 1-year cliff
  • Some programs pay incrementally at performance milestones, not just at exit

Questions to ask Brad

  1. What form does the equity take? (Phantom equity, profit interest units, RSUs?)
  2. What's the vesting schedule? Is there a cliff?
  3. What triggers a payout? Only a full sale, or are there interim distributions?
  4. Have any SCRAM employees received payouts yet?
  5. Is the grant sized relative to salary, role level, or tenure?

If the payout is 6-12 months of salary on a PE exit in 1-3 years, that's $50K-$100K on top of your base. That changes the math on whether $100K vs. $120K base even matters.
