A counterbalancing read on Anthropic's "restraint," what it actually signals, and what the NYT article left out.

April 8, 2026 · In response to Thomas Friedman, NYT, April 7

What Friedman reported

The facts, stripped of theater

Anthropic released a model called Claude Mythos Preview to roughly 40 companies via a consortium called Project Glasswing. Google, Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, JPMorgan, Palo Alto Networks, Nvidia.

The model can find software vulnerabilities at unprecedented scale. Thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities discovered in every major operating system and web browser.

Anthropic is not making it generally available. Controlled release only. Private conversations with the Trump administration preceded the announcement.

Friedman frames it as a nuclear-nonproliferation-scale event and calls for US-China collaboration on AI cyber defense.

~40 companies

Received controlled access. Friedman frames this as restraint. It's also the most sophisticated enterprise sales channel in AI history.

What he got right

Two things

Capability is outrunning governance

Anthropic's own statement says they didn't anticipate getting this good this fast. If the company building the model is surprised by its capabilities, every governance framework built on assumed timelines is already obsolete. The gap between capability and regulation is widening, not closing. Friedman is right to flag this as urgent.

Defense is now an AI product category

If Mythos can find vulnerabilities faster than any human team, then every security company either integrates this capability or becomes obsolete. Palo Alto Networks is in the consortium for a reason. Cybersecurity just became AI-native, and that shift is permanent.

What he missed

Six things

01 — The restraint IS the marketing

Anthropic is announcing a capability AND announcing they're withholding it. "We have something nobody else has, and we're choosing not to sell it to you." That positions them as the responsible actor while establishing they're ahead. Friedman takes the safety framing at face value. The restraint is real AND it's a business strategy. Both are true.

02 — The consortium is the moat

40 companies getting early access isn't charity. It's lock-in. If your critical infrastructure depends on Anthropic's model to find and patch vulnerabilities, you're an Anthropic customer at a level that's very hard to switch from. Glasswing isn't nonproliferation. It's an enterprise sales channel dressed in safety language.

03 — The China framing is naive

Friedman proposes US-China collaboration on AI cyber defense. China's APT groups (Volt Typhoon, Salt Typhoon) have been in US infrastructure for years. The idea that they'd join a defensive consortium that limits their offensive operations is geopolitically illiterate. What Friedman is actually making the case for is why the US needs to move faster, not cooperate.

04 — The "kids could do it" framing

"Honey, what did you do after school today?" "We took down the power grid." This is designed for NYT readers who are afraid of technology. The real threat isn't kids. It's ransomware groups, state hackers, and intelligence services. The gap between "find vulnerabilities" and "exploit them at scale against hardened infrastructure" is enormous. Friedman collapses that gap for dramatic effect.

05 — Nobody's asking who pays

If Mythos finds thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities in every major OS and browser, the patching cost across global infrastructure is tens of billions. Who pays? The consortium companies? Taxpayers? The hospitals and utilities running vulnerable legacy systems? Friedman mentions this in passing. It's actually the biggest practical question.

06 — The nonproliferation analogy is wrong

Nuclear weapons need enriched uranium, centrifuges, and nation-state resources. AI models need compute and weights. Weights can be stolen, leaked, or independently reproduced. You can't bottle this the way you can (partially) limit uranium enrichment. The better analogy is encryption in the 1990s. Governments tried to control it (Clipper chip, export restrictions). They failed. The technology diffused. The question isn't whether these capabilities proliferate. It's how fast and to whom first.

The structural forces

What's actually happening underneath

Capability is outrunning governance

If the company building the model is surprised by what it can do, every governance timeline is already wrong. The frameworks being discussed in Washington were designed for capabilities that existed two years ago.

Defense is now an AI-native market

Every cybersecurity company either integrates this capability or gets disrupted by someone who does. Anthropic just claimed the platform position for the defensive AI layer of global infrastructure.

Anthropic is building a regulatory moat

By going to government first, creating a consortium of the biggest companies, and framing this as national security, they're making the case that frontier AI should be regulated in a way only well-resourced incumbents can comply with. Whether intentional or not, "responsible release" is regulatory capture dressed in safety language.

Proliferation is inevitable

The question isn't whether these capabilities spread. It's whether the 12-18 month head start Glasswing buys is enough to find and patch the critical vulnerabilities before the capability diffuses. History says the technology always diffuses. The race is between patching speed and proliferation speed.

So what does this mean

For people building with AI

The platform you build on can change the rules

If you build products on top of AI models (and increasingly, everyone does), you're dependent on decisions made by a small number of companies about what capabilities to release and to whom. Today it's cybersecurity. Tomorrow it could be medical reasoning, legal analysis, or financial modeling. "Controlled release" means your product roadmap is subject to someone else's safety assessment.

The product layer gets more valuable

Here's the counterintuitive take. When the model layer gets commoditized (everyone has access to powerful AI), what matters is what you build on top. The judgment layer. The user context. The domain expertise. The data relationships. The model is the electricity. Your product is the appliance. Nobody buys electricity. They buy what it powers.

The governance gap is your operating environment

If you're building AI products today, you're operating in the gap between what the technology can do and what the rules say you can do with it. That gap is widening. The regulators will show up eventually, and they'll show up faster than expected. Building compliance infrastructure before you're forced to is an investment, not a cost.

The real question Friedman should have asked

Not "should the US and China collaborate?" but: who is training the people who will oversee these systems? Mythos can find vulnerabilities. But someone has to decide which ones to fix first, how to prioritize resources, and what trade-offs to accept. That's a human judgment problem. And right now, almost nobody is training for it.

The takeaway

Anthropic just executed the most sophisticated go-to-market strategy in AI history: announce a capability so powerful that governments pay attention, withhold it to create scarcity, distribute it to 40 enterprise customers as a "safety measure," and position yourself as the responsible actor while competitors look reckless by comparison.

The safety framing is real. And it's a business strategy. Both things are true. Friedman only sees the first one.

The structural bet: whoever controls the defensive AI layer for global infrastructure has the most durable business in technology. Whether the 12-18 month head start is enough depends on whether patching speed beats proliferation speed. History says the technology always diffuses. The question is whether the patch lands before the exploit does.
