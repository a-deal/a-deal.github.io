01

The situation

What Trisha said in her email

Two-pronged

Joe's role
Referral partnership and early connection-building. This is his lane. Sales only, sabbatical mode.

02

Your positioning

You own Track 2

Program development. Joe owns Track 1 (referral sales). These are separate. Don't let them blur. Track 1 can happen without you. Track 2 can't happen without you.

Show the iceberg, not the glacier

Don't show the full concept brief. Talk to it at a high level: "I've been developing a curriculum framework with my brother that maps military operational experience to AI coordination roles. We've done employer research. We have a thesis on why veterans are uniquely positioned." They should sense depth and want to explore further.

Don't commit before April 7

Thursday is positioning for the 4-way call with Justin and Teresa. That's the real meeting. Everything you say Thursday should strengthen your hand for that conversation.

03

The one question to ask

"Between now and our call with Justin and Teresa, what are you going to build?"

If she answers with coordination tasks (scheduling the meeting, recapping today's conversation, keeping people informed), that tells you her role is coordination.

If she answers with employer contacts she's reaching out to, veteran organizations she's engaging, or curriculum work she's contributing, she's a real execution partner.

Her answer defines the dynamic going into April 7.

04

Green and red flags

Green flags

She names specific employers or veteran orgs she's going to contact. She talks about concrete next steps with dates. She acknowledges the 5-month infrastructure gap. She asks you what you need to execute.

Red flags

More "strategic framing" without action items. Positions herself as translator between you and Justin/Teresa (gatekeeper dynamic). Wants to see your full work product without offering her own deliverables. Vague language about "continuing to shape" without committing to anything specific this week.

05

What not to do

  • Don't negotiate structure, equity, or titles. Way too early. Those conversations happen after the April 7 call at the earliest.
  • Don't hand over documents. Talk to the work, don't share it. The full thesis, curriculum framework, and employer research stay in our pocket until the partnership structure is clear.
  • Don't criticize Trisha or Joe's output. Just demonstrate yours. The quality gap speaks for itself.
  • Don't commit to deliverables on behalf of both of us. You can share what you're working on. Don't promise what Andrew will bring to April 7.
06

The leverage

LearnAIR has had 5+ months on the veteran program. Zero infrastructure. No dedicated emails for outreach. No landing page. No executed outreach on Joe's 40 target organizations. Five months of meetings that produced meetings.

You and Andrew built a comprehensive thesis, curriculum framework, competitive landscape analysis, employer readiness targets, and policy alignment in days. The work product gap between what we've produced and what LearnAIR has produced is the strongest card in this negotiation.

You don't need to say this out loud. It's obvious from the quality of what you bring to conversations. Trisha already knows. Justin already knows. That's why they're leaning in.

07

"I've been doing real work on this with my brother. We have a clear thesis, we've done the employer research, and we have a curriculum direction. I want to bring that to the April 7 call. Between now and then, I'd love to know what you're working on so we can come in aligned."

Collaborative but clear. You're building, you're ready, and you want to see what she's building too. If she's building, you have a partner. If she's coordinating, you have a coordinator. Either is fine. You just need to know which one before April 7.
