Curriculum philosophy

Evergreen by design

The curriculum teaches principles, not tools. Commander's intent. Feedback loops. Output validation. Managing ambiguity. These are the skills that transfer regardless of which AI platform is dominant in 6 months.

The tools change. The judgment doesn't. That means the curriculum doesn't expire when the next model drops. LearnAIR can run cohort after cohort without rebuilding from scratch each time. That's the difference between a course and a program.

Why this section exists

Trust with clarity

Mike, I know your instinct is going to be that this section is cynical. That asking hard questions about LearnAIR's structure is "fucking with the human element." I want to name that directly because I think there's a misunderstanding between us about what due diligence actually is.

Asking tough questions about a partner's business isn't negative energy. It's the opposite. It's how you show up as a better partner. When we understand LearnAIR's real situation, strengths AND constraints, we make better decisions about how to help. We set expectations that don't lead to disappointment. We avoid overpromising and underdelivering, which is the thing that actually damages relationships.

What damages the human element is walking in blind, assuming everything is great, and then getting surprised when reality doesn't match the optimism. That's when trust breaks. Not because someone asked a hard question early, but because nobody did.

This isn't cynicism vs. trust. It's trust with clarity. We trust Justin and Teresa enough to build alongside them. AND we maintain enough distance to see the full picture and make good decisions. Both things are true at the same time. Holding that tension is a skill.

None of what follows changes our commitment to the 90-day engagement. It just means we're walking in with our eyes open.

Reading the company

Everyone is 1099

Everyone at LearnAIR is a 1099 contractor. Justin, Teresa, and now us. Three years in, that's a signal worth reading honestly.

It might be deliberate and smart

Staying lean, no payroll overhead, scaling with contractors as needed. Valid for early stage. But at year 3, early stage is a long time.

It might mean they can't afford payroll

Revenue doesn't support W2 hires. If that's the case at year 3 with SDVOSB status and government connections, the business model hasn't found its margin yet.

It might mean nobody has fully committed

All-1099 means everyone is optionally participating. No vesting, no equity, no incentive alignment beyond the current invoice. Including us.

What this means for the 90 days

Our success criteria depend on people who have no contractual obligation to meet them. Justin opening his employer network, Teresa providing accreditation guidance, Mike showing up consistently. None of these are enforceable. They're all goodwill. The fix: make every commitment impeccable. DRI + date + written deliverable. The 1099 structure demands more precision in agreements, not less.

Accreditation expertise is listed as a key LearnAIR contribution. By day 45, we should see concrete evidence: a credential path, a compliance framework, or an accreditation timeline. If it stays "Teresa handles compliance" without specifics, it's a title, not a function.

If the day-90 structure conversation happens and nobody at LearnAIR is willing to discuss W2, vesting, or real commitment, we have our answer. The business isn't ready to be a business. That's not a failure. But it means the "longer-term" conversation is about a retainer, not a partnership.

The best outcome is: you build real skills (session delivery, partnership development, curriculum ownership), you get paid fairly, and at day 90 you have evidence about whether this is your thing. If it is, great. If it's not, you've spent 90 days getting paid to learn, not 90 days hoping.
