The Evergreen curriculum

From Oversight
to Orchestration

Topical, not tool-based. Model-agnostic by design. Whether someone uses ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini is below the line. What we teach is above the line.

Topic 1

Mission Command & Commander's Intent

Veterans already know this. They just don't know it transfers. Mission command, MCPP, BAMCIS: defining boundaries, validating outcomes, driving results through ambiguity. These are the same skills required to supervise AI systems. The curriculum surfaces this tacit knowledge and makes it explicit.

Maps to: defining agent intent, setting success criteria, structured execution frameworks

Topic 2

In-the-Loop vs. On-the-Loop

In the loop: human required for every step. You are the bottleneck. On the loop: human can correct, but the system moves autonomously. Order of magnitude efficiency gain. This is THE curriculum gradient. We teach the transition from tool use to system oversight.

Maps to: delegation patterns, autonomy levels, when to intervene vs. when to let the system run

Topic 3

Output Validation & Structural Honesty

AI systems select which facts to present. They can climb the wrong hill without telling you. Your team feels productive. They don't realize the oxygen is running out because nobody trained them to check. The supervision layer: knowing when the system is optimizing for the wrong goal.

Maps to: verification frameworks, adversarial testing, distinguishing real progress from apparent progress

Topic 4

Adversarial Collaboration

"Sir, respectfully, I think we're on the wrong hill." Veterans are trained to challenge assumptions up the chain. That's the skill AI oversight requires. Not agreement. Not blind trust. Constructive challenge that improves the output. This is what separates a tool user from an operator.

Maps to: prompt refinement, red-teaming outputs, feedback loops that improve system quality over time

Topic 5

Delegation & Feedback Architecture

How to specify what you want without over-constraining execution. How to design correction loops that catch errors early. How to structure feedback so the system gets better, not just compliant. Decentralized execution philosophy: define the outcome, trust the execution layer.

Maps to: agent orchestration, multi-step workflows, human-in-the-loop design patterns

Topic 6

Model Evaluation & Platform Agnosticism

We don't teach loyalty to a model. We teach evaluation as a skill. Which tool fits this task? How do you test that? What changes when the model changes, and what doesn't? The judgment layer is permanent. The tool layer is temporary. Graduates learn to navigate both.

Maps to: tool selection, capability assessment, building workflows that survive model transitions

Topic 7

Ecosystem Thinking

Operating in systems of systems. Inputs, outputs, dependencies, feedback loops. Understanding how a change in one agent affects the rest of the workflow. This is where military systems thinking, managing logistics, comms, and personnel simultaneously, maps directly to managing AI-augmented organizations.

Maps to: workflow design, dependency management, operating across multiple AI tools and teams simultaneously

Curriculum philosophy

Evergreen by design

Principles, not tools

Commander's intent. Feedback loops. Output validation. Managing ambiguity. These transfer regardless of which platform is dominant in 6 months.

Foundational, not just-in-time

Two types of knowledge: what you internalize (judgment, critical thinking, systems reasoning) and what you look up (API docs, market research). We teach the first kind.

The curriculum doesn't expire

The tools change. The judgment doesn't. LearnAIR can run cohort after cohort without rebuilding from scratch when the next model drops.

The identity shift

The real outcome isn't the certificate. It's that veterans see themselves as capable of leading in AI-enabled ecosystems. Not AI literate. AI capable. That's the shift.

Why this section exists

Trust with clarity

Mike, I know your instinct is going to be that this section is cynical. That asking hard questions about LearnAIR's structure is "fucking with the human element." I want to name that directly.

Asking tough questions about a partner's business isn't negative energy. It's how you show up as a better partner. When we understand their real situation, we set expectations that don't lead to disappointment. We avoid overpromising. That's what actually protects relationships.

What damages the human element is walking in blind, assuming everything is great, and getting surprised when reality doesn't match. That's when trust breaks. Not because someone asked a hard question early, but because nobody did.

This isn't cynicism vs. trust. It's trust with clarity. Both things at the same time. Holding that tension is a skill, and it's one that will make you a better program lead, a better partner, and a better negotiator.

Reading the company

Everyone is 1099

Justin, Teresa, and now us. Three years in. That's a signal.

Deliberate and smart

Lean, no overhead, scaling with contractors. Valid for early stage. But at year 3, early stage is a long time.

They can't afford payroll

Revenue doesn't support W2s. At year 3 with SDVOSB status and government connections, that says something about the business model.

Nobody has fully committed

Everyone is optionally participating. No vesting, no equity, no incentive alignment beyond the current invoice. Including us.

Our success criteria depend on people with no contractual obligation to meet them. The fix: make every commitment impeccable. DRI + date + written deliverable. The 1099 structure demands more precision, not less.

For Mike

How to read your own position

Financial pressure distorts signal

You need income. Every opportunity looks like the answer when you're under pressure. Test yourself: when Justin says something promising, ask "would I find this equally exciting with $50K in savings?" If yes, it's real. If no, it's relief.

The $100/hr rate protects you

You're compensated regardless of where the company goes. Don't discount it out of gratitude. The opportunity is mutual.

Clarity is not cynicism

When I flag a red flag, I'm making sure we notice if the partnership is one-sided before we're too invested to course-correct. The human element gets damaged far more by unspoken disappointment at day 90 than by an honest conversation at day 30. Asking "is this working?" is how you protect relationships. Avoiding the question is how you lose them.

The best outcome isn't "LearnAIR works out"

The best outcome: you build real skills, get paid fairly, and at day 90 you have evidence. If it's your thing, great. If not, you spent 90 days getting paid to learn.
